# Chapter 15 — The Sun: A Garden-Variety Star

*Why the Most Ordinary Star in the Galaxy Is Trying to Kill Your Power Grid.*

---

On the morning of September 1, 1859, an English amateur astronomer named Richard Carrington was drawing sunspots through a projecting telescope when two intensely bright points of white light appeared among the spot group. They flared, moved, and within five minutes faded. He noted it, told a friend, and waited.

He did not have to wait long.

About seventeen hours later, something arrived at Earth. It had no weight that any scale could measure. It made no sound. It was not visible. But at telegraph stations across North America and Europe, the instruments began to spark. Some wires became hot enough to burn operators who touched them. Systems that had run reliably for years failed simultaneously. And in the night sky — over Hawaii, over Cuba, over Rome — the aurora blazed. People woke at two in the morning convinced it was dawn.

Carrington had witnessed a solar flare. The flare had launched a cloud of magnetized plasma — we now call it a coronal mass ejection — that traveled ninety million miles in seventeen hours, slammed into Earth's magnetic field, and lit up the sky from pole to nearly equator. The telegraph network of the industrialized world simply stopped working.

I want to start with that event, not with how the Sun shines, because the Carrington Event is the reason the mechanism matters. The Sun is not a lantern hanging in the sky. It is a dynamic, magnetically driven machine that is constantly reaching across empty space to touch everything in the solar system. Understanding how it works is the only way to understand why, on an otherwise ordinary morning, telegraph operators across two continents burned their hands.

## What the Sun Is Made Of

For two thousand years, the dominant view was that the Sun was made of something fundamentally different from earthly matter. Aristotle was confident about this. The Sun was perfect and eternal, composed of a fifth element that terrestrial things were not. This was not superstition. It was a reasonable inference from observation: the Sun did not rust, decay, or transform the way earthly things did.

In 1925, a young British astronomer named Cecilia Payne-Gaposchkin used a spectroscope to read the Sun's composition from its light. Every element produces a distinctive pattern of dark absorption lines when its atoms remove specific wavelengths from passing light — chemical fingerprints. She measured those lines, ran the calculations, and got a number she found so improbable she doubted herself: the Sun is approximately 73 percent hydrogen by mass and 25 percent helium. Everything else — carbon, oxygen, nitrogen, iron, calcium, all the elements you associate with planets and biology — makes up about 2 percent.

She wrote in her doctoral thesis that these enormous abundances were "almost certainly not real." She was wrong to doubt. She was right about the numbers.

<!-- → [INFOGRAPHIC: two pie charts side by side — left: Sun's composition by mass (hydrogen 73%, helium 25%, all other elements 2%); right: Earth's bulk composition by mass (iron ~32%, oxygen ~30%, silicon ~15%, magnesium ~14%, other ~9%); caption: "Payne-Gaposchkin's result, which she initially walked back under pressure from her advisor. The contrast with Earth is the visual argument for why it was so unexpected — Earth is mostly iron and rock; the Sun is mostly the two simplest atoms."] -->

The Sun is not a rock. It is not burning coal, as nineteenth-century physicists had hoped. It is a ball of the two simplest elements in the universe, held together by gravity, so hot throughout that the electrons have been stripped from the atoms entirely. What remains is plasma: a soup of bare protons and electrons, electrically conducting, intimately responsive to magnetic fields. Nearly all the visible matter in the universe is in this state. We live in the exception.

## The Interior: Energy's Long Journey Out

The Sun has a core, a radiative zone, a convection zone, a photosphere, and an atmosphere. These are not sharp boundaries — they are regions where different physics dominates. Understanding each one requires understanding one central problem: how does energy get from the inside to the outside?

At the core, the temperature reaches 15 million Kelvin. That is hot enough to force hydrogen nuclei together against the electromagnetic repulsion that normally keeps them apart. When four hydrogen nuclei fuse into one helium nucleus, about 0.7 percent of the mass is converted to energy according to $E = mc^2$. The Sun fuses about 620 million tons of hydrogen per second.

But here is the first surprise: that energy does not rush outward. The interior of the Sun is so dense — so packed with nuclei and electrons — that a photon produced in the core travels only about a centimeter before colliding with a particle and being sent in a random new direction. This is the radiative zone, extending from the core to about 70 percent of the way to the surface. A photon produced in the core undergoes something like $10^{26}$ collisions before reaching the edge of this zone. The random walk takes, on average, about 100,000 years.

The number is worth sitting with. The Sun takes eight minutes to send light from its surface to your eye. But it takes 100,000 years for that same light to travel from the core to the surface. The interior is not empty space. It is the most opaque material in the solar system. The light reaching you today left the core when Homo sapiens was in its earliest infancy.

At around 70 percent of the radius, the plasma cools enough — about 2 million Kelvin — that atoms begin to recombine and hold onto their electrons. Atoms with electrons absorb radiation far more efficiently than bare nuclei. The plasma becomes so good at capturing photons that radiation can no longer carry the energy outward efficiently. At this point a different mechanism takes over: convection.

Hot plasma is less dense than cool plasma. It rises. As it rises, it expands and cools. When it reaches the surface and radiates energy away, it becomes denser, sinks, is reheated by the flow from below, and rises again. This cycle — the same thing that boils water in a pot, the same thing that drives thunderstorms — operates across a shell 200,000 kilometers deep. The tops of these convection cells are visible as granules: bright polygonal patches roughly 1,000 kilometers across, surrounded by darker lanes where cooler plasma is sinking. Each granule rises, spreads, cools, and vanishes in about eight minutes. The photosphere is covered with millions of them at any moment, constantly forming and dissolving.

The convection zone is also where the Sun's magnetic field is generated. Moving plasma carries electric currents, and electric currents generate magnetic fields. The convection is turbulent — different latitudes rotate at different speeds, the equator taking 25 days per rotation, the poles about 36 — and this differential rotation stretches and twists the magnetic field into increasingly complex configurations. Think of stirring chocolate syrup into vanilla ice cream: the strands get longer, thinner, more twisted, and eventually break apart and reconnect in new configurations. The Sun's magnetic field does something similar over years, and the consequences are everything we find dramatic about the Sun.

The field lines wind tighter every year. Eventually they must break and reconnect."] -->

## The Temperature That Goes the Wrong Way

The photosphere is where light escapes. Below it, the plasma is opaque. Above it, the plasma is thin enough that photons travel freely into space. This transition happens across a layer about 400 kilometers thick — essentially nothing compared to the Sun's 696,000-kilometer radius. The photosphere is not a solid surface, but it is where the Sun becomes visible to us, at a temperature of about 5,800 Kelvin.

Now move upward from the photosphere. Common sense says temperature should fall as you move away from the heat source. For a few hundred kilometers above the photosphere, it does. But then something strange happens. The temperature reaches a minimum of about 4,400 K at roughly 500 kilometers altitude, and then begins to rise. By 2,000 kilometers up, in the chromosphere, temperatures reach 10,000 K. And in the corona — the outermost atmosphere, visible as the pearly halo during total solar eclipses — the temperature climbs to between one and three million Kelvin.

To be clear about how strange this is: you are moving away from the most powerful energy source in the solar system, and the temperature is rising by a factor of nearly 200. If you walked away from a bonfire and the air got hotter the further you went, you would correctly suspect something was happening that you did not yet understand.

Something is happening that we do not yet fully understand. The solar corona is one of the major unsolved problems in astrophysics. What we know is that the magnetic field is intimately involved: waves and oscillations propagating upward from the convection zone carry energy into the corona, and magnetic reconnection events release energy there. But the details of how this energy is efficiently converted to heat remain genuinely open. The corona is simultaneously the most visible and the least understood part of the Sun.

Sunspots are a consequence of the same magnetic field. Where particularly strong field lines break through the photosphere, they suppress the convection underneath. The magnetic field holds the hot rising plasma back, leaving the region cooler — about 3,800 Kelvin. At that temperature, sunspots appear dark against their surroundings. But "dark" is relative: a piece of sunspot material transported to a dark room would glow brilliantly. It is only in contrast to the surrounding photosphere that it looks black.

## The Eleven-Year Cycle

The sunspot cycle was discovered by an amateur pharmacist.

Heinrich Schwabe decided in the 1820s to search for a hypothetical planet inside Mercury's orbit by watching for it to cross the solar disk. To avoid confusing it with sunspots, he kept meticulous daily counts. He did this for seventeen years. He never found the planet. But by 1843 he had enough data to notice something nobody had noticed before: the number of sunspots waxes and wanes on a regular cycle averaging about eleven years.

The cycle is driven by the differential rotation described above. The faster equatorial rotation winds the magnetic field around the Sun like thread around a spool. After several years, the field becomes so amplified that it forms powerful flux tubes that rise through the convection zone like buoyant ropes. Where a tube punches through the photosphere, it creates a pair of sunspots — one where the field emerges and one nearby where it re-enters the Sun. The strong magnetic field in the tube suppresses convection, creating the characteristic dark cool region.

Over about eleven years, the field becomes increasingly twisted, sunspot activity grows, then the field breaks apart and reconnects, releasing energy and rebuilding with opposite polarity. What was north is now south. The true cycle — from one magnetic configuration back to the identical configuration — takes 22 years, since the polarity must reverse twice to return to the start.

<!-- → [CHART: sunspot number vs. year, from approximately 1700 to present — time series showing the 11-year cycle clearly, with numbered solar cycles labeled at each maximum; annotate four key events: the Maunder Minimum (~1645–1715, when cycles nearly vanished, shaded), the Carrington Event at Solar Cycle 10 maximum (1859), the 1989 Quebec blackout, and the 2012 near-miss; the student should see that cycle amplitude varies considerably and that the most damaging events cluster near cycle maxima] -->

The cycle is regular enough to predict in broad terms. What we cannot predict is the timing and strength of individual events — which specific active region will produce a major flare, on which day, aimed at which part of the solar system. The cycle is the background rhythm. The individual events are the improvisation on top of it.

## Flares and Coronal Mass Ejections

At sunspot maximum, the magnetic field above the photosphere is a tangled mess of loops, all trying to expand upward while gravity and neighboring fields constrain them.

When two magnetic loops with opposite orientations approach each other in the corona, the field lines break and reconnect to new partners. The stored magnetic energy — which had been building since those field lines were first pulled upward from the photosphere — releases all at once. A solar flare is this reconnection event: a sudden explosion heating the surrounding plasma to tens of millions of Kelvin, flooding the inner solar system with X-rays and extreme ultraviolet radiation. These arrive at Earth at the speed of light, eight minutes after the flare. They ionize the upper atmosphere. Radio communication is disrupted or blacked out on the sunlit side of Earth. Satellites experience increased drag. This is the flare's immediate effect — felt before anyone can warn anyone.

<!-- → [DIAGRAM: magnetic reconnection in four panels — (1) two opposing magnetic loops approaching in the corona above the photosphere; (2) field lines beginning to break at the contact point; (3) explosive reconnection: field lines snap to new partners, stored energy released as X-ray and UV emission (arrows toward Earth), and particle acceleration downward; (4) post-flare loops in lower-energy configuration; annotate the energy release in panel 3 as "equivalent to ~10⁹ hydrogen bombs"; mark the X-ray emission direction toward Earth with a speed label "arrives at Earth: +8 minutes"] -->

Sometimes, associated with a flare, a coronal mass ejection erupts. This is something different in character: not just radiation but matter — a bubble containing tens of millions of tons of plasma and an entangled magnetic field, ejected at speeds between 500 and 1,000 kilometers per second. A CME does not travel at the speed of light. It arrives at Earth in two to four days. Unlike the flare's instantaneous punch, a CME is a slow punch you can see coming.

Whether the resulting geomagnetic storm is mild or severe depends on orientation. A CME with a magnetic field oriented southward — opposite to Earth's northward equatorial field — will connect strongly with Earth's field, allowing charged particles to funnel deep into the magnetosphere. This is when transformers fail, when satellites lose orientation, when the aurora blazes at mid-latitudes. A northward-oriented CME slips past with much less drama.

The Carrington Event of 1859 was, by available evidence, the most powerful solar storm in the last five centuries of recorded human observation. In 1989, a much weaker storm knocked out the power grid in Quebec for nine hours, leaving six million people without electricity in a Canadian winter. In July 2012, a CME of Carrington-class intensity erupted from the Sun. It missed Earth by about a week — the Earth happened to be elsewhere in its orbit. Studies published afterward estimated that if the 2012 CME had struck directly, the resulting damage to US infrastructure alone would have exceeded one trillion dollars, with recovery taking years.

This is not hypothetical. It is a near miss that already happened.

## The Solar Wind: Constant and Relentless

Even in quiet periods, the Sun is not truly quiet. The corona is so hot that its thermal energy exceeds the Sun's gravity at the outer boundary of the corona. Plasma evaporates continuously from the corona into interplanetary space — the solar wind: a constant outflow of protons, electrons, and helium nuclei at roughly 400 kilometers per second.

By the time the wind reaches Earth, it has expanded to about five particles per cubic centimeter — one of the lowest-density flows we can measure. But the solar wind is relentless. It has been blowing for 4.5 billion years.

At Earth, the solar wind meets the magnetosphere and compresses it to about 65,000 kilometers above Earth's surface on the sunlit side. On the night side, the wind stretches the field into a tail extending millions of kilometers. Earth orbits inside this cavity. At the north and south magnetic poles, field lines funnel downward into the upper atmosphere. That is where the charged particles enter.

When particles strike oxygen atoms at around 200 kilometers altitude, those atoms emit green light. At higher altitudes, above 300 kilometers, oxygen emits red. Nitrogen at lower altitudes emits blue and purple. The aurora is the visible emission of this particle interaction — a signature, written in light, of the Sun's reach across ninety million miles.

Without the solar wind and the magnetosphere it shapes, Earth would look more like Mars: its atmosphere gradually eroded over billions of years. The same wind that caused the Carrington Event, that sparked telegraph wires in 1859, has also been protecting us since the planet formed.

## The Sun in Perspective

The Sun is a G2V star. G means surface temperature between about 5,200 and 6,000 Kelvin. V means it is a main-sequence star, burning hydrogen in its core. There are billions of G-type stars in the Milky Way. By stellar standards, the Sun is aggressively average.

The Sun formed about 4.6 billion years ago from a cloud of gas and dust that was itself the remnant of previous stellar generations. The heavy elements it contains — iron, carbon, oxygen — were manufactured in stars that lived and died before the Sun existed, scattered into the interstellar medium by supernova explosions, and swept up when the solar nebula collapsed. The iron in your blood was forged in the core of a star that exploded billions of years ago and is not the Sun. We are made of stellar ash.

The Sun will continue fusing hydrogen for about another five billion years. Eventually the hydrogen in the core will be exhausted, the core will contract, the outer layers will expand, and the Sun will become a red giant — possibly large enough to engulf Mercury, Venus, and perhaps Earth. The outer layers will be expelled as a planetary nebula. What remains will be a white dwarf: the dense core of the former Sun, about the size of Earth, slowly cooling over billions of years.

<!-- → [DIAGRAM: Sun's life cycle as a horizontal timeline — left end: formation from nebula (4.6 Ga ago); center: current main sequence (annotated "now — halfway through hydrogen-burning lifetime"); right section: gradual brightening → red giant expansion (~5 Ga from now, dotted arc showing Earth's orbit potentially inside the expanded envelope) → planetary nebula ejection → white dwarf remnant; annotate "dangerous to power grids: now" with a bracket spanning the current main-sequence phase, to connect the life-cycle context back to the chapter's central concern] -->

None of that is relevant to us. In human timescales, the Sun is stable. What matters to us is not the billion-year evolution but the eleven-year cycle, the coronal mass ejections, the solar flares, the charged particles that reach Earth in days. The machine is not threatening us on geological timescales. It is threatening us on the timescale of a week.

Cecilia Payne-Gaposchkin analyzed the Sun's spectrum in 1925 and found that it was mostly hydrogen and helium. She doubted herself. Her advisor told her the result was probably spurious. She published it with a disclaimer walking back her own finding. She was right. The Sun is what it is, not what we expect. That lesson applies to the Sun and to every other object in this book: the universe does not care what we expect. Our job is to read what is actually there.

The Carrington Event of 1859 did its damage with the most advanced technology of its era: telegraph wire. If it happened tomorrow, it would do its damage with the most advanced technology of ours: power grids, satellites, internet infrastructure, hospital equipment, navigation systems. The Sun's violence is constant. Our exposure to it grows with every technology we add to civilization. We have been lucky for 165 years. The machine has not changed. Only our dependence on it has.

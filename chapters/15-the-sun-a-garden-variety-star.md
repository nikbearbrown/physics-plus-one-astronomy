# Chapter 15 — The Sun: A Garden-Variety Star
*Why the Most Ordinary Star in the Galaxy Is Trying to Kill Your Power Grid.*

**TL;DR:** The Sun is not the stable, unchanging thing ancient astronomers imagined. It is a roiling ball of ionized hydrogen and helium held together by gravity, inside which hydrogen fuses to helium at 15 million Kelvin. That fusion radiates outward so slowly through the dense interior that a photon takes a hundred thousand years to reach the surface. Once it does, we see the photosphere — a thin, convective layer where the light finally escapes. Above that sits an atmosphere that does not cool as you move outward, as one would expect; instead, the temperature climbs from 6,000 K to a million. The Sun's magnetic field drives everything dramatic that happens above the surface: sunspots, flares, coronal mass ejections. That field is generated by churning plasma in the convection zone, and it flips every 11 years or so. Particles streaming from the Sun's corona — the solar wind — have shaped Earth's magnetosphere for four billion years, and when they hit hard, they write aurora in the sky and sometimes black out power grids. The Sun is a machine of sublime elegance and brutal indifference.

---

On the morning of September 1, 1859, an English amateur astronomer named Richard Carrington was drawing sunspots through a projecting telescope when two intensely bright points of white light appeared among the spot group. They flared, moved, and within five minutes faded. He had no idea what he had seen. There was no word for it yet. He noted it, told a friend, and waited.

He did not have to wait long.

About seventeen hours later, something arrived at Earth. It had no weight that any scale could measure. It made no sound. It was not visible. But at telegraph stations across North America and Europe, the instruments began to spark. Some wires became hot enough to burn operators who touched them. Systems that had run reliably for years failed simultaneously. And in the night sky — over Hawaii, over Cuba, over Rome — the aurora blazed visible. People woke at 2 a.m. convinced it was dawn.

Carrington had witnessed a solar flare. The flare had launched a cloud of magnetized plasma — we now call it a coronal mass ejection — that traveled ninety million miles in seventeen hours, slammed into Earth's magnetic field, and lit up the sky from pole to nearly equator. The telegraph network of the industrialized world, the most advanced communication technology of the day, simply stopped working.

This is where the chapter begins. Not with how the Sun shines — that comes shortly — but with what the Sun *does to us*, because that is the question that makes the mechanism matter. The Sun is not a lantern hanging in the sky. It is a dynamic, violent, magnetically driven machine that is constantly reaching across empty space to touch everything in the solar system. Understanding how it works is the only way to understand why, on an otherwise ordinary morning in 1859, telegraph operators across two continents burned their hands.

## What the Sun Is Made Of

For two thousand years, the dominant view was that the Sun was made of something fundamentally different from earthly matter. Aristotle was confident about this. The Sun was perfect, eternal, unchanging — composed of a fifth element that terrestrial things were not. This was not superstition. It was a reasonable inference from observation: the Sun did not rust, decay, or transform the way earthly things did.

In 1925, a young British astronomer named Cecilia Payne-Gaposchkin used a spectroscope to read the Sun's composition from its light. Every element produces a distinctive pattern of dark absorption lines when its atoms remove specific wavelengths from passing light — the chemical fingerprint we met in the chapter on telescopes. She measured those lines, ran the calculations, and got a number she found so improbable she doubted herself: the Sun is approximately 73 percent hydrogen by mass and 25 percent helium. Everything else — carbon, oxygen, nitrogen, iron, calcium, all the elements you associate with planets and biology — makes up about 2 percent.

She wrote in her doctoral thesis that the enormous abundances she found for hydrogen and helium were "almost certainly not real." She was wrong to doubt. She was right about the numbers. The Sun is not a rock. It is not burning coal, as nineteenth-century physicists had hoped. It is a ball of the two simplest elements in the universe, held together by gravity, so hot throughout that the electrons have been stripped from the atoms entirely. What remains is plasma: a soup of bare protons and electrons, electrically conducting, intimately responsive to magnetic fields. Nearly all the visible matter in the universe is in this state. We live in the exception.

## The Interior: Three Zones, One Problem

The Sun has a core, a radiative zone, a convection zone, a photosphere, and an atmosphere. These are not sharp boundaries — they are regions where different physics dominates. Understanding each one requires understanding one central problem: how does energy get from the inside to the outside?

At the core, the temperature reaches 15 million Kelvin. That is hot enough to force hydrogen nuclei together against the electromagnetic repulsion that normally keeps them apart. When four hydrogen nuclei fuse into one helium nucleus, about 0.7 percent of the mass is converted to energy according to $E = mc^2$. The Sun fuses about 620 million tons of hydrogen per second. The energy released is staggering.

But here is the first surprise: that energy does not rush outward. It cannot. The interior of the Sun is so dense — so packed with nuclei and electrons — that a photon produced in the core travels only about a centimeter before colliding with a particle and being sent in a random new direction. This is the radiative zone, extending from the core to about 70 percent of the way to the surface. A photon produced in the core undergoes something like $10^{26}$ collisions before it finally reaches the edge of this zone. The random walk takes, on average, about 100,000 years. The light reaching your eyes from the Sun left the core when Homo sapiens was in its infancy.

The number is worth sitting with. The Sun takes eight minutes to send light from its surface to your eye. But it takes 100,000 years for that same light to travel the much shorter distance from the core to the surface. The interior is not empty space. It is the most opaque material in the solar system.

<!-- → [DIAGRAM: cross-section of the Sun's interior showing the three zones — core (~0–25% radius), radiative zone (~25–70% radius), convection zone (~70–100% radius) — with temperature labels at key boundaries (15 million K at core, 2 million K at base of convection zone, 5,800 K at photosphere); annotate the photon travel time (~100,000 years through radiative zone) vs. light travel time to Earth (8 minutes); arrows showing energy transport mechanism in each zone: nuclear reactions → radiation → convection] -->

At around 70 percent of the radius, the plasma becomes cool enough — about 2 million Kelvin — that the atoms begin to recombine and hold onto their electrons. Atoms with electrons absorb radiation far more efficiently than bare nuclei. The plasma becomes so good at capturing photons that radiation can no longer carry the energy outward efficiently. At this point, a different mechanism takes over: convection.

Hot plasma is less dense than cool plasma. It rises. As it rises, it expands and cools. When it reaches the surface and radiates energy away, it becomes denser, sinks, is reheated by the flow from below, and rises again. This cycle — the same thing that boils water in a pot, the same thing that drives thunderstorms — operates across a shell 200,000 kilometers deep, circulating enormous volumes of plasma in cells that reach all the way to the photosphere. The tops of these cells are visible as granules: bright polygonal patches roughly 1,000 kilometers across, surrounded by darker lanes where cooler plasma is sinking. Each granule rises, spreads, cools, and vanishes in about eight minutes. The photosphere is covered with millions of them at any moment, constantly forming and dissolving.

<!-- → [IMAGE: high-resolution solar telescope image of photospheric granulation — show the bright polygon cells (~1,000 km across) with darker intergranular lanes; annotate one granule with its approximate diameter relative to Earth's size for scale; caption: "Each granule is a convection cell: hot plasma rising at center, cooling and sinking at edges, dissolving in ~8 minutes. Millions operate simultaneously across the entire photosphere."] -->

The convection zone is also where the Sun's magnetic field is generated. Moving plasma carries electric currents. Electric currents generate magnetic fields. The convection is turbulent and chaotic — different latitudes rotating at different speeds (the equator takes 25 days per rotation, the poles about 36) — and this differential rotation stretches and twists the magnetic field into increasingly complex configurations. Think of stirring chocolate syrup into vanilla ice cream: the strands get longer, thinner, more twisted, and eventually break apart and reconnect in new configurations. The Sun's magnetic field does something similar, and the consequences are everything we find dramatic about the Sun.

## The Surface and the Temperature Paradox

The photosphere is where light escapes. Below this layer, the plasma is opaque — photons bounce off particles before they can travel far. Above it, the plasma is thin enough that photons travel freely into space. This transition happens across a layer about 400 kilometers thick, which is essentially nothing compared to the Sun's 696,000-kilometer radius. The photosphere is not a surface in any solid sense — there is no edge to stand on — but it is where the Sun becomes visible to us.

At the photosphere, the temperature is about 5,800 Kelvin. Sunspots are regions where particularly strong magnetic fields break through the surface and suppress the convection underneath. The magnetic field holds the hot rising plasma back, leaving the region cooler — about 3,800 Kelvin. At that temperature, sunspots appear dark against their surroundings. But "dark" is relative. A chunk of sunspot material transported to a dark room would glow brilliantly. It is only in contrast to the surrounding 5,800 K photosphere that it looks black.

Now move upward from the photosphere. Common sense says temperature should fall as you move away from the heat source. For a few hundred kilometers above the photosphere, it does. But then something strange happens. The temperature reaches a minimum of about 4,400 K at roughly 500 kilometers altitude, and then begins to rise again. By 2,000 kilometers up, in the chromosphere, temperatures reach 10,000 K. And in the corona — the outermost atmosphere, visible as the pearly halo during total solar eclipses — the temperature climbs to between one and three million Kelvin.

To be clear about how strange this is: you are moving away from the most powerful energy source in the solar system, and the temperature is rising by a factor of nearly 200. If you walked away from a bonfire and the air got hotter the farther you went, you would correctly suspect something was going on that you did not yet understand.

Something is going on that we do not yet fully understand. The solar corona is one of the major unsolved problems in astrophysics. What we know is that the magnetic field is intimately involved: waves and oscillations propagating upward from the convection zone carry energy into the corona, and magnetic reconnection events release energy there. But the details of how this energy is efficiently converted to heat — why the corona reaches a million Kelvin rather than, say, 100,000 K or ten million — remain genuinely open. The corona is simultaneously the most visible and the least understood part of the Sun.

## The Magnetic Cycle: What Makes the Sun Tick

The sunspot cycle was discovered by an amateur pharmacist.

Heinrich Schwabe decided in the 1820s to search for a hypothetical planet inside Mercury's orbit by watching for it to cross the solar disk. To find a planet, he needed to know which spots were sunspots and which might be the shadow of a planet, so he kept meticulous daily counts of sunspot numbers. He did this for seventeen years. He never found the planet. But by 1843 he had enough data to notice something nobody had noticed before: the number of sunspots waxes and wanes on a regular cycle averaging about eleven years. Sometimes more than a hundred spots appear. Sometimes none.

The cycle is driven by the dynamo mechanism described above. The Sun's differential rotation — equator faster than poles — winds the magnetic field around the Sun like thread around a spool. After several years, the field becomes so wound and amplified that it forms powerful flux tubes that rise through the convection zone like buoyant ropes. Where a tube punches through the photosphere, it creates a pair of sunspots — one where the field emerges (north polarity) and one nearby where it re-enters the Sun (south polarity). The strong magnetic field in the tube suppresses convection, creating the characteristic dark cool region.

Over the course of about eleven years, the field becomes increasingly twisted, sunspot activity grows, then the field breaks apart and reconnects, releasing energy and rebuilding with opposite polarity. What was north is now south. The leading sunspots in the Northern Hemisphere change polarity; the same happens in the Southern Hemisphere with its own reversal. This flip takes another eleven years to reverse again, meaning the true cycle — from one configuration back to the identical configuration — takes 22 years.

The cycle is regular enough to predict in broad terms. We know the Sun will approach maximum activity around 2025 and decline thereafter. What we cannot predict is the timing and strength of individual events — which specific active region will produce a major flare, on which day, aimed at which part of the solar system. The cycle is the background rhythm. The individual events are the improvisation on top of it.

## Flares, Prominences, Coronal Mass Ejections

At sunspot maximum, the magnetic field above the photosphere is a tangled mess of loops, some anchored in sunspots, some in quieter regions, all trying to expand upward while gravity and neighboring fields constrain them. Prominences form where dense plasma is suspended in magnetic loops, sometimes hanging stable for days or weeks, sometimes erupting violently.

When two magnetic loops with opposite orientations approach each other in the corona, the field lines break and reconnect to new partners. The stored magnetic energy — which had been building since those field lines were first pulled upward from the photosphere — releases all at once. A solar flare is this reconnection event: a sudden explosion releasing the energy equivalent of a billion hydrogen bombs, heating the surrounding plasma to tens of millions of Kelvin, flooding the inner solar system with X-rays and extreme ultraviolet radiation that reach Earth at the speed of light.

The X-rays and UV arrive at Earth eight minutes after the flare. They ionize the upper atmosphere. Radio communication, which relies on the ionosphere as a reflector, is disrupted or blacked out entirely on the sunlit side of Earth. Satellites in low Earth orbit experience increased drag as the upper atmosphere expands from the heating. This is the flare's immediate effect — arriving at the speed of light, felt before anyone can warn anyone.

<!-- → [DIAGRAM: magnetic reconnection sequence in four panels — (1) two opposing magnetic loops approaching in the corona; (2) field lines beginning to break at the contact point; (3) explosive reconnection: field lines snap to new partners, stored energy released as X-rays, UV, and particle acceleration; (4) post-flare loops — new, lower-energy configuration with visible post-reconnection field lines; annotate the energy release as "equivalent to ~10^9 hydrogen bombs" and mark the X-ray emission direction toward Earth] -->

Sometimes, associated with a flare, a coronal mass ejection erupts. This is something different in character: not just radiation but matter — a bubble containing tens of millions of tons of plasma and an entangled magnetic field, ejected from the corona at speeds between 500 and 1,000 kilometers per second. A CME does not travel at the speed of light. It arrives at Earth in two to four days. Unlike the flare's instantaneous light-speed punch, a CME is a slow punch you can see coming.

When a CME arrives, it encounters Earth's magnetic field — the magnetosphere — and compresses it on the sunlit side, stretching it on the night side. Whether the CME's magnetic field happens to be oriented to connect with and amplify Earth's field or to resist and deflect it determines how severe the resulting storm is. A CME with a magnetic field oriented southward — opposite to Earth's northward equatorial field — will connect strongly, allowing charged particles to funnel deep into the magnetosphere. This is when transformers fail, when satellites lose their orientation, when pipelines accumulate induced currents, when the aurora blazes at mid-latitudes.

The Carrington Event was, by available evidence, the most powerful solar storm in the last five centuries of recorded human observation. In 1989, a much weaker storm knocked out the power grid in Quebec for nine hours, leaving six million people without electricity in a Canadian winter. A storm in 2003 damaged or destroyed several satellites and caused brief power outages in Sweden. In July 2012, a CME of Carrington-class intensity erupted from the Sun. It missed Earth by about a week — the Earth happened to be elsewhere in its orbit. Studies published afterward estimated that if the CME had struck directly, the resulting geomagnetic storm would have caused between one and two trillion dollars in damage to infrastructure in the United States alone, with recovery taking years.

This is not hypothetical. It is a near miss that already happened.

## The Solar Wind: The Sun's Constant Breath

Even in quiet periods, the Sun is not truly quiet. The corona is so hot that its thermal energy exceeds the Sun's gravity at the corona's outer boundary. Plasma evaporates continuously from the corona into interplanetary space. This is the solar wind: a constant outflow of protons, electrons, and helium nuclei streaming away from the Sun at roughly 400 kilometers per second.

By the time the wind reaches Earth, it has expanded and thinned to about five particles per cubic centimeter — one of the lowest-density flows we can measure. But the solar wind is relentless. It has been blowing for 4.5 billion years. Over that time, the Sun has shed perhaps a fraction of a percent of its mass this way.

The solar wind comes preferentially from coronal holes: regions where the Sun's magnetic field opens outward into space rather than looping back. In open geometry, the plasma is not trapped. It flows away freely. Where the field is closed, plasma is contained and the corona builds to high density. Maps of the corona, taken in X-ray light, show the difference starkly: bright, dense closed regions and dark, hollow coronal holes where the wind pours out.

At Earth, the solar wind meets the magnetosphere. The interaction creates a cavity: on the sunlit side, the wind pressure compresses the field to about 65,000 kilometers above Earth's surface. On the night side, the wind stretches the field into a tail extending millions of kilometers. Earth orbits inside this cavity, shielded from the full brunt of the wind. At the north and south magnetic poles, field lines funnel downward into the upper atmosphere. That is where the charged particles enter.

When particles strike oxygen atoms at around 200 kilometers altitude, those atoms emit green light. At higher altitudes, above 300 kilometers, oxygen emits red. Nitrogen at lower altitudes emits blue and purple. The aurora is the visible emission of a particle interaction with an atmosphere — a signature, written in light, of the Sun's reach across ninety million miles.

Without the solar wind and the magnetosphere it shapes, Earth would look more like Mars: its atmosphere gradually eroded over billions of years by unfiltered cosmic rays. The same wind that caused the Carrington Event, that sparked telegraph wires in 1859, has also been protecting us since the planet formed.

## The Sun in Perspective

The Sun is a G2V star. Astronomers use this classification to encode several things: G means surface temperature between about 5,200 and 6,000 Kelvin, placing it in the middle range of stellar temperatures; 2 is a finer subdivision within G; V means it is a main-sequence star — one burning hydrogen in its core, as opposed to a giant or white dwarf. There are billions of G-type stars in the Milky Way. By stellar standards, the Sun is aggressively average.

The Sun formed about 4.6 billion years ago from a cloud of gas and dust that was itself the remnant of previous stellar generations. The heavy elements it contains — the iron, the carbon, the oxygen — were manufactured in stars that lived and died before the Sun existed, scattered into the interstellar medium by supernova explosions, and swept up when the solar nebula collapsed. The iron in your blood was forged in the core of a star that exploded billions of years ago and is not the Sun. We are made of stellar ash.

The Sun will continue fusing hydrogen for about another five billion years. As it ages, it will gradually brighten — it is about 30 percent brighter now than when Earth formed, a change that has had profound effects on Earth's climate and will continue to do so. Eventually, when the hydrogen in the core is exhausted, the core will contract, the outer layers will expand, and the Sun will become a red giant: vastly larger, cooler at the surface, possibly engulfing Mercury, Venus, and perhaps Earth. The outer layers will be expelled as a planetary nebula. What remains will be a white dwarf: the hot, dense core of the former Sun, about the size of Earth, slowly cooling over billions of years.

<!-- → [DIAGRAM: Sun's life cycle timeline — horizontal bar showing: formation from nebula (4.6 Ga ago) → current main sequence phase (now, ~halfway through) → brightening phase → red giant (~5 Ga from now, with a dotted line showing Earth's orbit potentially inside the expanded Sun) → planetary nebula ejection → white dwarf remnant; annotate current age and remaining hydrogen-burning lifetime; the student should see that the dangerous phase for Earth in this chapter (CMEs, flares) is happening right now, in the middle of the Sun's stable life] -->

Nothing in that future has any bearing on us. In human timescales, the Sun is stable. What matters to us is not the billion-year evolution but the eleven-year cycle, the coronal mass ejections, the solar flares, the charged particles that reach Earth in days. The machine is not threatening us on geological timescales. It is threatening us on the timescale of a week.

Cecilia Payne-Gaposchkin analyzed the Sun's spectrum in 1925 and found that it was mostly hydrogen and helium. She doubted herself. Her advisor told her the result was probably spurious. She published it with a disclaimer walking back her own finding. She was right. The Sun is what it is, not what we expect. That lesson applies to the Sun and to every other object in this book: the universe does not care what we expect. Our job is to read what is actually there.

The Carrington Event of 1859 did its damage with the most advanced technology of its era: telegraph wire. If it happened tomorrow, it would do its damage with the most advanced technology of our era: power grids, satellites, internet infrastructure, hospital equipment, navigation systems. The Sun's violence is constant. Our exposure to it grows with every technology we add to civilization. We have been lucky for 165 years. The machine has not changed. Only our dependence on it has.

What would change my mind: evidence that the corona is heated by a mechanism wholly independent of the magnetic field — something not yet conceived — would require fundamental revision of solar atmospheric physics.

Still puzzling: the coronal heating problem. We know the corona is a million Kelvin. We know the magnetic field is involved. We do not fully understand how magnetic energy is converted to thermal energy with the efficiency the observations require. It remains one of the genuinely open problems in solar physics.

## Exercises

The following exercises are designed for use with a language model that can reason through calculations and concepts step by step.

**The random walk problem.** A photon in the Sun's radiative zone travels about 1 cm between collisions and undergoes roughly $10^{26}$ collisions before reaching the edge of the zone. Ask the model to explain why this produces a travel time of ~100,000 years rather than the ~2 seconds a straight-line journey at light speed would take. What is a random walk, and how does the mean free path of 1 cm lead to the observed timescale? Then ask it to contrast this with the 8-minute travel time from surface to Earth — what physical difference between the Sun's interior and empty space explains the factor-of-a-billion difference in travel speed?

**Cecilia Payne-Gaposchkin and the spectroscopy of composition.** Ask the model to explain how a spectroscope can reveal a star's chemical composition from its light. Why do atoms produce dark absorption lines at specific wavelengths, and how does the pattern of lines encode identity? Then push further: Payne-Gaposchkin found hydrogen and helium at 73% and 25% of solar mass. Why was this result so unexpected that she doubted it, and what argument would a contemporary astronomer have made against it? What evidence eventually confirmed she was right?

**The coronal temperature paradox.** Ask the model to state clearly why the Sun's corona (1–3 million K) being hotter than the photosphere (5,800 K) violates naive expectations — and then to explain what the current leading hypotheses are for coronal heating. What is magnetic reconnection, what is wave heating, and what does each mechanism predict about where and how energy is deposited? Ask it to identify what observation or measurement would allow scientists to distinguish between them. Why does this remain an unsolved problem despite decades of satellite observation?

**The 11-year sunspot cycle from first principles.** Ask the model to trace the full causal chain from differential rotation to sunspot pair to polarity reversal to 22-year cycle. At each step, ask it to name the physical mechanism: what does differential rotation do to the magnetic field? Why do flux tubes become buoyant? Why do sunspots appear in pairs? Why does the field eventually break down and rebuild with opposite polarity? Then ask: if the Sun rotated uniformly (same speed at all latitudes), would a sunspot cycle still exist, and why or why not?

**Flare vs. CME — two different threats.** Ask the model to compare a solar flare and a coronal mass ejection: what each is physically, how each is produced, how fast each reaches Earth, and what different kinds of damage each causes. Why does a flare affect Earth before anyone can issue a warning, while a CME gives two to four days of advance notice? Ask it to explain why the orientation of a CME's magnetic field relative to Earth's determines whether the resulting geomagnetic storm is mild or severe — what is "southward Bz" and why does it matter?

**Quantifying the near-miss.** In July 2012, a Carrington-class CME missed Earth by roughly one week of orbital travel. Ask the model to estimate — using Earth's orbital speed (~30 km/s) and the width of a typical CME (~30–60 million km) — how large the "target window" was and what fraction of Earth's orbit it represents. Then ask it to reason through what a Carrington-class event would do to modern infrastructure that didn't exist in 1859: what specific technologies are vulnerable that telegraph wire was not, and why are high-voltage power transformers particularly difficult to replace quickly?

# Chapter 10 — Earthlike Planets: Venus and Mars

*Three planets. Nearly identical at birth. One melts lead, one is a frozen desert, one has you.*

---

Here is a fact I want you to hold onto for this entire chapter.

Venus and Earth are, by most measures, nearly the same planet. Venus is 0.82 Earth masses. It orbits at 0.72 AU — closer to the Sun, yes, but not dramatically so. It formed from the same cloud of debris, at the same time, made of the same silicates and iron and water. If you were a planetary scientist in another solar system, looking at this one from far away, Venus and Earth would be the same data point. Two planets, nearly identical in mass, at similar orbital distances, around a G-type star. Twins.

Venus today has a surface temperature of 730 Kelvin. That is hot enough to melt lead. The atmospheric pressure is 90 times what you breathe right now. It rains sulfuric acid.

How? That is the question this chapter is about. Not just how Venus became what it is, but how three nearly identical starting conditions produced three completely different planets — because Mars, which started with the same ingredients, ended up a frozen desert where the air pressure is less than 1 percent of Earth's. The three of us orbit the same star, formed from the same cloud, and today one of us melts lead, one of us is nearly airless, and one of us has oceans and weather and you.

The mechanisms that produced these outcomes are operating on Earth right now. This is not an academic exercise. It is an attempt to understand the system we are living inside.

## Why Planets Lose Their Air

Start with the simplest thing: size.

Every atom in an atmosphere is moving. Temperature is just another name for the average kinetic energy of those moving atoms. At any given temperature, there is a distribution of speeds — some atoms moving slowly, some quickly, most clustered near an average. The tail of that distribution contains atoms moving fast enough to exceed the planet's escape velocity if they happen to be near the top of the atmosphere and heading upward.

Escape velocity from Earth: 11.2 kilometers per second. From Venus: 10.4. From Mars: 5.0.

The difference between Earth and Venus looks modest. The difference between either of them and Mars is not. An atom moving at 5 kilometers per second escapes Mars but is still bound to Earth. Over billions of years, with the tail of the speed distribution constantly leaking atoms away, a low-gravity planet loses its atmosphere the way a ship with a slow leak loses water — steadily, invisibly, irreversibly.

But size alone is not the whole story. There is a second mechanism, and it is the one that distinguishes Mars from its neighbors most decisively.

A planet with a molten iron core generates a global magnetic field through the circulation of conducting fluid — the same physics as any dynamo. This field extends above the atmosphere and deflects the solar wind: the continuous stream of charged particles flowing outward from the Sun at hundreds of kilometers per second. Without that deflection, the solar wind strikes the upper atmosphere directly, transfers energy to individual atoms and molecules, and pushes more of them above escape velocity. The atmosphere bleeds away faster.

Earth has this shield. Mars does not — not anymore. We know Mars had one, because ancient rocks in the Martian southern highlands carry magnetization signatures: the frozen record of a global field that reversed, the way Earth's field reverses, billions of years ago. Then the field stopped. The core cooled. The circulation ceased. The shield vanished. And the solar wind began its long, patient work.

Venus is the strange case. Venus is nearly Earth's mass. It should have retained a field. But it did not — and why it did not is tangled up with the very catastrophe that made Venus what it is today.

## What Happened to Venus

Consider Venus four billion years ago. The early Sun was dimmer than it is now, so the extra proximity to the Sun was less decisive than it would be today. Venus probably had water — oceans, possibly. A thicker atmosphere of nitrogen and carbon dioxide. The reasonable inference is that it started out resembling Earth more than it resembles what it is today.

Then something shifted. Perhaps the Sun gradually brightened as it aged. Perhaps volcanism released an extra pulse of carbon dioxide. The initial perturbation does not matter much, because what happened next is a feedback loop, and in a feedback loop, small causes become large effects.

Water evaporates from the ocean surface. On Earth this is normal — the water returns as rain, and the cycle is stable. But water vapor is a greenhouse gas. It traps heat. If the temperature rises enough that more water evaporates than returns, the extra vapor traps extra heat, which causes more evaporation. The cycle does not stabilize. Each step amplifies the next. This is the runaway greenhouse effect.

As temperatures climbed, the oceans of Venus evaporated faster. The upper atmosphere became saturated with water vapor. At the top of the atmosphere, where ultraviolet light from the Sun is most intense, those water molecules were broken apart. The oxygen stayed behind, oxidizing surface rocks. The hydrogen — light, energetic, at the very top of the atmosphere — escaped to space.

Once the oceans were gone, a second process engaged. On Earth, carbon dioxide does not simply accumulate in the atmosphere indefinitely. It dissolves in rainwater, reacts with silicate rocks, and the products wash into the ocean where they are precipitated as limestone, burying the carbon. This carbonate-silicate cycle is a genuine thermostat: when CO2 rises, the burial accelerates; when it drops, volcanic outgassing replenishes it. It operates slowly, over millions of years, but it operates.

Venus lost the oceans that make this thermostat work. With no water to dissolve CO2, with no rain to weather the rocks, the gas that volcanoes produced simply accumulated. The atmosphere became nearly 100 bars of carbon dioxide. The surface temperature climbed to 730 Kelvin. The planet crossed a threshold from which no natural process returns it.

The Magellan spacecraft, mapping Venus with radar in the late 1980s and early 1990s, found a surface dominated by lava plains. Crater counts suggest these plains are 300 to 600 million years old — which means Venus experienced a global volcanic resurfacing that erased whatever geological record existed before. The earlier history of Venus, the record of its transition from possibly-wet to certainly-dead, is buried under lava. We cannot read it.

## What Happened to Mars

Mars is the opposite story, told in reverse.

Four billion years ago, Mars was probably wet — not warm and tropical, but wet, at least episodically. Three lines of evidence converge on this conclusion, and the convergence makes it hard to resist.

First, the surface. Orbital imaging shows sinuous runoff channels that resemble what you would get from rainfall draining downhill, and much larger outflow channels of extraordinary scale — ten kilometers wide, hundreds of kilometers long, cut with the signature of catastrophic flooding. The only way to produce such channels is a sudden, massive release of liquid water, which suggests Mars had subsurface ice and that local heating events melted it in violent floods. Crater counts date both kinds of channels to roughly four billion years ago.

Second, the mineralogy. The rovers operating on Mars — Opportunity, Curiosity, Perseverance — have analyzed ancient rocks in detail. Opportunity found sedimentary formations and iron-rich spherules that form only in water. Curiosity found mudstones: rocks formed from ancient lake sediments. The rocks do not lie about having been wet.

Third, meteorites. Rocks knocked from Mars by ancient impacts have fallen to Earth. We identified them as Martian by matching gases trapped inside to the atmospheric composition measured by the Viking landers. The interiors of these rocks show signs of having interacted with liquid water.

So Mars had water. Then it did not. The cause is the same collapse of the magnetic field that I described above. Once the solar wind had direct access to the upper atmosphere, pressure dropped. Below a critical threshold — the triple point of water, at about 0.006 bar — liquid water cannot exist on the surface. It either freezes or boils away, depending on temperature. Mars's current atmospheric pressure is about 0.007 bar. In most places, at most times, liquid water is simply not possible at the surface.

What remains is ice. The north polar cap is water ice, about three kilometers thick. Beneath the south polar cap's dry ice surface is another water ice layer. The Phoenix lander, arriving near the north pole in 2008, dug a few centimeters into the soil and found bright ice immediately below the dust. The water is still there. It is frozen, locked away, unable to support chemistry.

There is one more thing that deserves mention, because it is strange and unexplained. On steep slopes at high Martian latitudes, dark streaks appear and lengthen over the course of days. Spectroscopic analysis found hydrated salts alongside them: the chemical signature of evaporated salty water. Something, somewhere, under some conditions, allows liquid water to exist briefly on Mars right now. The mechanism is unknown — perhaps subsurface groundwater driven up by geothermal heat, perhaps salts absorbing water vapor from the thin air and briefly becoming liquid before evaporating again. The amount involved is tiny. The fact of it is not.

Mars is close to dead. But close to dead is different from dead.

## Three Outcomes, Three Mechanisms

Step back now and state what we have learned.

The first mechanism is **mass and escape velocity**. A small planet has lower escape velocity, so thermal motion carries more atmospheric atoms above the threshold for escape. Mars's mass — 0.11 times Earth's — made it vulnerable from the start.

The second mechanism is **magnetic shielding**. A planet with an active, circulating iron core generates a global field that deflects the solar wind. Without that field, the wind directly strips the upper atmosphere. Mars lost its field early. The stripping accelerated. Venus never regained one after its internal structure changed — possibly because the runaway greenhouse altered the planet's interior thermally, possibly because Venus's extremely slow rotation provides insufficient Coriolis force to organize the circulation that sustains a dynamo. The details are contested. The result is not.

The third mechanism is **feedback loops**. A planet that begins slightly too warm can trigger the runaway greenhouse: more heat, more evaporation, more vapor, more trapped heat. A planet that loses its atmosphere for other reasons can trigger the inverse: less atmosphere, less greenhouse warming, lower temperatures, more freezing, less evaporation, less warming. Both are one-way doors. The passage through either is essentially irreversible on any timescale that matters to us.

Earth avoided both doors. It is massive enough to maintain a magnetic field. It is far enough from the Sun that the extra heat never triggered the runaway evaporation of its oceans. It has liquid water, which means the carbonate-silicate thermostat operates, which means CO2 is regulated rather than accumulated. These three factors work together. Remove any one, and the calculation changes.

The part worth sitting with is how small the differences were. Venus is 0.72 AU from the Sun; Earth is 1.0. A small shift in distance. Venus is 0.82 Earth masses; Earth is 1.0. A modest difference in mass. And the result is one planet where you can stand outside and look at the sky, and another where lead is liquid on the ground.

That is how sensitive these feedback loops are.

## Did Life Begin on Mars?

The deepest question this chapter raises is not about Venus or Mars as physical objects. It is about life.

Mars, four billion years ago, had water. It had chemical energy from geological activity. It had an atmosphere with enough pressure to allow liquid water to persist at the surface. These are the same conditions that existed on early Earth, during the period when life first appeared here.

The Viking landers of 1976 tested Martian soil for biology directly. They added nutrients and water and radioactive tracers and watched for signs of metabolism, respiration, chemical transformation. They found chemical reactivity — but the reactivity came from ordinary chemistry, ultraviolet-activated peroxides in the soil, not from organisms. Life was not detected.

But the surface is a difficult environment. No ozone layer means ultraviolet light sterilizes the top few centimeters of soil continuously. Below the surface, there is no ultraviolet. There is ice. There may be liquid water in thin films around mineral grains, or in subsurface aquifers kept liquid by geothermal heat. If life began on Mars four billion years ago, when conditions were favorable, could it have retreated underground as the surface became hostile and persisted there in some form ever since?

We do not know. The Perseverance rover has been collecting cylinders of ancient sedimentary rock from Jezero Crater, a former lake delta. At some point these samples will be returned to Earth, where they can be analyzed in laboratory conditions far more sophisticated than anything we can land on Mars. What would count as evidence? Particular ratios of carbon isotopes that biological processes preferentially produce. Morphological structures resembling microbial fossils, though these are notoriously hard to distinguish from mineral formations mimicking biological shapes. Molecular complexity — organic molecules arranged in patterns that chemistry alone does not easily produce.

No single finding would be proof. Together, if the right combination appeared, they would make a strong case.

What would prove life never existed? This is harder. Absence of evidence from a few rock samples is not evidence of absence across a planet that has been geologically active for billions of years. A negative result from the sample return mission would narrow the question, not close it.

Here is why the question matters beyond Mars itself. If life began independently on Mars — in the same solar system, at roughly the same time, with similar chemistry but separate origins — then the origin of life is not a miracle. It is a process that happens wherever the conditions are right. The universe is probably full of it.

If life did not begin on Mars, despite conditions being right, then either we got extraordinarily lucky on Earth, or there is something missing from our understanding of how life begins. Something we have not yet identified that distinguishes an ocean with the right chemistry from an ocean that actually produces self-replicating molecules.

Both of those answers are extraordinary. Either one changes how we think about life in the universe. This is what makes the Mars question one of the most important open questions in science: we are not waiting for a finding that confirms what we suspect. We are waiting for a finding that will tell us something we genuinely do not know. Whichever way it goes, we will have learned something fundamental.

## What This Means for the Planet You Are On

I want to end where I began: with the observation that the mechanisms in this chapter are not history lessons about other planets. They are descriptions of processes operating on Earth right now.

The carbonate-silicate thermostat — the cycle that prevents CO2 from accumulating the way it did on Venus — operates on timescales of hundreds of thousands to millions of years. It is the reason Earth's climate has remained in the range of liquid water for four billion years despite the Sun gradually brightening by about 30 percent. The thermostat is real, and it works.

But it works slowly. The question of what happens when CO2 is added to the atmosphere faster than the thermostat can remove it is not answered by pointing to the thermostat. The thermostat cannot respond in decades. On decades-to-centuries timescales, added CO2 simply accumulates and warms the planet, just as it did on Venus — not to Venus-like temperatures, because we are not at Venus-like orbital distances and we are not approaching Venus-like amounts of CO2, but through the same physical mechanism, via the same infrared trapping physics.

Mars tells a different story about a different danger. Not runaway warming but irreversible loss: of atmosphere, of liquid water, of the conditions that make a planet habitable. The Martian surface is sterilized by ultraviolet light that the thin atmosphere cannot block. The thin atmosphere persists because there is nothing to replenish what the solar wind strips away. The chain from magnetic field death to atmospheric loss to surface sterilization took hundreds of millions of years on Mars — but it happened, and it was irreversible, and it began with a change in the planet's interior that no surface process could compensate for.

We are not Mars. We have a magnetic field. We have an active interior. But the chain of dependencies that makes Earth habitable — core heat driving the dynamo, dynamo sustaining the field, field protecting the atmosphere, atmosphere maintaining the pressure for liquid water, liquid water enabling the thermostat — is a chain. Every link depends on the ones before it.

Understanding Earth as a planet means understanding that it is not the default state of rocky worlds orbiting stars. Venus and Mars are the default states. Earth is the exception, maintained by a particular combination of mass, distance, internal activity, and chemical cycles that has held for four billion years. It held before there was anyone to appreciate it. Whether it holds going forward depends, in part, on whether the species currently modifying its atmospheric chemistry understands the feedback loops well enough to take them seriously.

Three planets. Same origin. Three completely different outcomes. The lesson is not that Earth is special in some mystical sense. The lesson is that the mechanisms are real, the feedbacks are powerful, and the margins are smaller than they look.

**Exercise 2**

Ask your LLM: *"Compare the mechanisms of atmospheric loss on Venus and Mars. In both cases the atmosphere became predominantly carbon dioxide. Does that mean the same process produced the same result? Explain why or why not."*

The correct answer is no — the same atmospheric composition resulted from entirely different mechanisms. Venus retained its CO2 because the carbonate cycle stopped working. Mars retained its CO2 because it is the heaviest common gas and was stripped last. Does the LLM distinguish these mechanisms, or does it conflate the similar outcome with a similar cause? What does this tell you about how the model handles causal reasoning?

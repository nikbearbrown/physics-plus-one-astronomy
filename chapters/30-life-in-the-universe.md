# Chapter 30 — Life in the Universe

*Whether we are alone is the most consequential question science has ever been able to ask.*

---

In 1953, Stanley Miller was a graduate student at the University of Chicago, working under Harold Urey. He wanted to test a simple hypothesis: that the molecules life requires could assemble themselves from the chemistry of the early Earth.

He built a closed glass apparatus. In one flask, water. In another, a mixture of gases he believed the early atmosphere contained: methane, ammonia, hydrogen. He connected the flasks so water vapor circulated through the gas mixture. Then he added a spark — simulating lightning.

Within a week, the clear water had turned reddish-brown. When Miller analyzed it, he found amino acids. Several of the twenty that life on Earth uses. He had made the building blocks of proteins in a small glass jar, in one week, from the simplest starting materials.

The deeper point held even when we learned his atmosphere was probably wrong. You don't need life to make the molecules of life. Chemistry does it spontaneously, under conditions that are nothing special. The same organic molecules have been found in meteorites that formed before Earth existed, in comets, in the gas clouds between stars. The building blocks of life are distributed throughout the galaxy like spilled flour.

What happened next — the step from spilled flour to bread — is the question this chapter takes up. Not the full complexity of life's origin, which remains genuinely unresolved, but the machinery: what those molecules do, what conditions allow them to do it, and how we might detect whether they are doing it somewhere else.

---

## What Life Actually Runs On

The atom at the center of everything biological is carbon.

Not because it is rare — carbon is the fourth most abundant element in the universe — but because of what it can do with a bond. Carbon has four electrons available for bonding with other atoms. It can bond to four things simultaneously, including to other carbons, creating chains and rings of arbitrary length and complexity. Nitrogen, oxygen, and hydrogen attach to these carbon scaffolds, creating regions of different electrical character — patches that attract water, patches that repel it, patches that recognize specific other molecules. This variety of shape and charge is what makes biological molecules able to recognize each other, to fit together like keys and locks, to catalyze reactions and store information.

No other abundant element has this combination of properties. Silicon also has four bonding electrons and can form chains. But silicon bonds are stiffer, harder to break and reform. Life requires dynamic chemistry — molecules that assemble, do work, and come apart. Silicon chemistry is slow and rigid. The organic chemistry of life — which can be assembled and disassembled rapidly, which can fold into precise shapes, which can encode information in sequences — belongs to carbon.

Carbon cannot work alone. It needs water.

Water's usefulness comes from its polarity. The oxygen end of a water molecule is slightly negatively charged; the hydrogen ends are slightly positive. This makes water an extraordinary solvent: it pulls charged and polar molecules into solution, giving them freedom to move, collide, and react. It provides a medium in which biochemical reactions can occur. Life does not happen in the solid state; it happens in solution, where molecules can find each other.

Water also has one other gift: ice floats. This seems trivial until you consider the alternative. If ice sank, every pond and lake in a cold climate would freeze solid from the bottom up, killing whatever lived there. Instead, ice forms on the surface and insulates the liquid beneath. Life in cold environments survives because water has this quirk.

Together, carbon and water create a platform on which something extraordinary becomes possible.

The something extraordinary is a molecule that can copy itself.

Consider what that requires. You need a molecule that carries information — a sequence that specifies something. You need machinery to read that sequence and produce something useful, like a catalyst. And you need a way to copy the sequence so the information persists through time.

Modern life separates these functions. DNA stores information. Proteins do the work. Copying DNA requires proteins; making proteins requires DNA. This is the famous chicken-and-egg problem of the origin of life. Neither works without the other, so how did the first one start?

The answer that most biologists find compelling is RNA.

RNA is a simpler cousin of DNA. It can store sequences of information, like DNA. But it can also fold into three-dimensional shapes and catalyze reactions, like proteins. RNA can do both jobs simultaneously — it is both the recipe and the chef. The RNA world hypothesis proposes that the earliest life used RNA alone, storing information and executing chemistry in a single molecule. DNA and proteins came later as refinements.

No one has found RNA world fossils; the transition, if it happened, was over three billion years ago. But the hypothesis is chemically plausible, and researchers have produced RNA molecules in the laboratory that can copy themselves and catalyze reactions. The RNA world is not proven. It is a testable model, and the tests are not failing.

The point to carry forward is this: life's machinery is fundamentally chemistry. Atoms doing what atoms do — forming bonds, exchanging electrons, finding configurations of lower energy. The complexity of life is real, but it is the complexity of chemistry, not of something outside nature. The building blocks are common. The conditions that assemble them are common. The question is only whether they assembled here by chance, or whether they assemble wherever conditions allow.

## Where the Conditions Allow

The concept of a habitable zone sounds simple: the region around a star where water could be liquid on a planet's surface. Not too hot, not too cold. Goldilocks. But the concept unravels quickly as you look at it.

Venus is closer to the Sun than Earth but reflects much more sunlight — its clouds bounce about 70 percent of incoming light back into space. By simple energy balance, Venus should not be as hot as it is. The reason it is 460 degrees Celsius at the surface is its atmosphere: almost pure carbon dioxide, creating a greenhouse effect so intense that the surface glows in infrared. Venus had water once. That water evaporated under the young Sun, rose into the atmosphere, and was broken apart by ultraviolet radiation. Without water, there was nothing to absorb CO₂ from the atmosphere. The greenhouse intensified. The water was gone.

Mars is the reverse. Mars is small — less than one-tenth Earth's mass — and small planets lose their interior heat quickly. As the interior cooled, the magnetic field died. Without a magnetic field, the solar wind gradually stripped away the atmosphere. The pressure fell. Water could no longer remain liquid at the surface. Mars dried.

Earth is between these failures, but it almost failed too. The Sun was about thirty percent dimmer when Earth formed 4.6 billion years ago. By simple calculation, early Earth should have been frozen solid. Yet we have geological evidence of liquid water as far back as 3.8 billion years. Something kept it warm, and the best current answer involves methane produced by early microbes. Life, in other words, may have been the condition for its own survival. The biosphere modified the atmosphere to keep itself alive. This is an uncomfortable fact for the concept of a fixed habitable zone — it suggests that once life exists, it changes the rules.

The habitable zone concept becomes even shakier when you consider moons.

Europa, orbiting Jupiter, is far outside the Sun's habitable zone. Its surface temperature is around minus 160 degrees Celsius. Yet beneath its ice crust there is almost certainly a liquid ocean. The surface is crisscrossed with cracks and ridges that look like floating ice, and when Galileo flew past, its magnetometer detected an induced magnetic field — the signature of a conducting liquid layer. The liquid is kept from freezing not by sunlight but by tidal heating: Jupiter's enormous gravity stretches and squeezes Europa as it orbits, generating heat in the moon's interior through friction.

Enceladus, orbiting Saturn, has something even more striking: active geysers. Water vapor and ice particles erupt from the south polar region at about 200 kilograms per second, feeding Saturn's E ring. Cassini flew through these plumes and tasted them — salt, organic molecules, silica particles indicating hot water dissolving rock. There is an ocean beneath the ice, in contact with the rocky seafloor, with chemistry happening at the water-rock interface.

This is what hydrothermal vents look like on Earth. And on Earth, hydrothermal vents support entire ecosystems that run on chemical energy rather than sunlight. Bacteria and archaea extract energy from the oxidation of hydrogen, sulfur, and iron. These organisms don't need the Sun. They are powered entirely by the planet's interior.

If life can run on chemistry rather than light, then the habitable zone — defined by proximity to a star — is the wrong concept. The right concept is the presence of liquid water and chemical energy gradients. By that standard, Europa and Enceladus are candidates. Mars might once have been one, and its subsurface might still be. There may be worlds throughout the galaxy, far from any star, warmed by radioactive decay or tidal friction, harboring liquid water in their interiors.

We are searching in the wrong places if we search only in the habitable zone. But the habitable zone is where we can see.

## How We Would Know

A biosignature is a feature of a planet that can only be explained by life.

On Earth, the most obvious one is oxygen. Twenty percent of our atmosphere is molecular oxygen — O₂. This is chemically remarkable. Oxygen reacts with almost everything: rock, iron, reduced gases. Without constant replenishment, atmospheric oxygen would be gone in about a million years. The replenishment comes from photosynthesis. Without biology, the oxygen would vanish.

From across the galaxy, an observer with the right instruments could detect oxygen in Earth's atmosphere. Combined with methane — also present in trace amounts, also replenished by living things — the simultaneous presence of these reactive gases is almost impossible to explain without biology. They react with each other and should cancel out. The fact that both persist means something is constantly regenerating both. That something is life.

The James Webb Space Telescope is now capable of detecting atmospheric composition on some exoplanets. Not Earth-sized planets orbiting Sun-like stars — those are still beyond reach. But rocky planets orbiting small, cool stars called red dwarfs are accessible: the planet is close to the star (staying warm despite the star's dimness), and the star is small enough that the planet blocks a detectable fraction of starlight during transit. By comparing the spectrum of the star with and without the planet in front, you can identify which wavelengths the atmosphere absorbs — which reveals which molecules are present.

There is a critical complication: false positives. Oxygen can be produced abiotically on certain worlds. Ultraviolet light can break water vapor apart, releasing oxygen without any biology. An observer seeing oxygen in such an atmosphere would be misled.

This is why biosignature detection requires not just detecting one molecule but constructing a coherent picture. Oxygen plus methane plus the specific reflectance signature of chlorophyll, together, form a pattern difficult to explain without biology. Any single signal can be mimicked by geology or photochemistry. The combination is harder to fake.

Carl Sagan noted that Earth itself looked ambiguous in the Galileo spacecraft's data when it flew past in 1990. If you did not know what you were looking at, you could construct non-biological explanations for most of what Galileo saw. The standard for claiming detection of life elsewhere should be extraordinarily high. False discovery would be one of the most consequential errors in scientific history.

## The Equation That Organizes the Ignorance

In 1961, Frank Drake attempted to organize the problem of how many communicating civilizations might exist in the galaxy into a single equation. He was preparing for the first conference ever devoted to the search for extraterrestrial intelligence:

$$N = R_\star \times f_p \times n_e \times f_l \times f_i \times f_c \times L$$

Each term asks a question. $R_\star$: how fast do stars form? About ten per year. $f_p$: what fraction have planets? Close to one — we now know most stars have planets. $n_e$: how many habitable-zone planets per system? Probably at least one on average. $f_l$: of habitable worlds, on what fraction does life arise? Unknown. $f_i$: of worlds with life, on what fraction does intelligence emerge? Unknown. $f_c$: of intelligent species, what fraction develop technology capable of transmitting detectable signals? Unknown. $L$: how long does such a civilization broadcast? Unknown.

The known quantities multiply to something large. The unknowns could reduce that to almost anything.

What the Drake equation actually accomplishes is not a calculation but a diagnosis. It shows you where the ignorance is concentrated. The first three terms we now know reasonably well. It is $f_l$ — whether life starts whenever conditions allow, or only rarely — that would most change our picture of the galaxy. One more example of life, anywhere — a microbe under the Martian crust, an organism in Europa's ocean, a biosignature in an exoplanet atmosphere — would constrain $f_l$ more than any theory could.

And then there is $L$.

The only way for many communicating civilizations to exist simultaneously is if they last a long time. If technological civilizations typically destroy themselves within a few centuries of achieving the ability to broadcast, there might be at most a handful in the entire galaxy at any moment, separated by thousands of light-years. If they persist for millions of years, the galaxy could be full of them.

The bottleneck in the Drake equation may not be the origin of life. It may not be the evolution of intelligence. It may be whether intelligent species survive their own technology long enough to fill the cosmos. The question "are we alone?" turns out to hinge partly on a question we are living through right now.

## The Shape of the Search

We have been searching for extraterrestrial radio signals since 1960, when Drake pointed the Green Bank telescope at two nearby stars and listened for ten hours. He heard nothing. In the sixty years since, we have searched more stars, more frequencies, more carefully, and have heard nothing recognizable as artificial.

This is not proof that no one is transmitting. The search space is enormous. The electromagnetic spectrum contains billions of possible frequencies. There are billions of stars to point at. Modern technology allows simultaneous searching across millions of frequencies and multiple stars. The Breakthrough Listen project, launched in 2016, surveyed more stars in a few years than all previous SETI programs combined.

The absence of a signal thus far is not surprising. It is consistent with "no one is out there," or "we haven't looked hard enough," or "they're not transmitting in ways we'd recognize." We cannot yet distinguish between these.

There is also the Fermi paradox. Physicist Enrico Fermi reportedly asked at a lunch in 1950: where is everybody? If intelligent civilizations are common and persist for a long time, some should have had billions of years to spread through the galaxy, leaving visible traces. The galaxy shows no such traces. The absence of evidence for extraterrestrial civilization is itself a datum.

Various solutions have been proposed. Perhaps the galaxy is effectively empty except for us. Perhaps civilizations turn inward rather than outward — into virtual reality, into self-modification, into pursuits that don't require expanding into space. Perhaps they transmit in ways we don't recognize. Perhaps the distances are simply too vast and the timescales too long.

We don't know. The paradox remains.

What we do know is that we are, for the first time in Earth's history, technically capable of answering the question. The tools exist. The methods are improving. Within the next few decades, we may have spectra of rocky exoplanet atmospheres containing biosignature gases. We may sample the ocean of Enceladus and find organic chemistry with a biological signature. We may detect a signal from a distant star that no geological or stellar process can explain.

The Copernican principle has been right every time we have tested it. Earth is not the center of the solar system. The Sun is not the center of the galaxy. The Milky Way is not the center of the universe. Each time we assumed our location was special, we were wrong. To assume that life arose only here, only once, in a universe of two trillion galaxies, is the last of the old specialness claims.

It may be right. But it deserves to be tested.

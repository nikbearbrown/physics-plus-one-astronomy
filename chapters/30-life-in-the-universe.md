# Chapter 30 — Life in the Universe

**TL;DR:** Life on Earth rests on the chemistry of carbon, water, and four other elements forged in stars. The same molecules appear in meteorites and interstellar dust — the building blocks exist everywhere. If those building blocks assembled once, on one ordinary planet around one ordinary star, the simplest reading is that assembly is ordinary. We have no way to know yet whether it is, which is exactly why we search.

---

In 1953, Stanley Miller was a graduate student at the University of Chicago, working under Harold Urey. He wanted to test a simple hypothesis: that the molecules life requires could assemble themselves from the chemistry of the early Earth.

He built a closed glass apparatus. In one flask, water. In another, a mixture of gases he believed the early atmosphere contained: methane, ammonia, hydrogen. He connected the flasks so the water vapor circulated through the gas mixture. Then he added a spark — simulating lightning.

Within a week, the clear water had turned reddish-brown. When Miller analyzed it, he found amino acids. Not all of them, but several of the twenty that life on Earth uses. He had made the building blocks of proteins in a small glass jar, in one week, from the simplest starting materials.

The result was famous and controversial. Controversial partly because the early Earth's atmosphere was probably more carbon dioxide than methane — we later learned the experiment's starting conditions were not quite right. But the deeper point held: you don't need life to make the molecules of life. Chemistry does it spontaneously, under conditions that are nothing special. The same organic molecules have since been found in meteorites that formed before Earth existed, in comets, in the gas clouds between stars. The building blocks of life are distributed throughout the galaxy like spilled flour.

What happened next — the step from spilled flour to bread — is the question this chapter takes up. Not the origin of life in its full complexity, which remains genuinely unresolved, but the machinery of it: what those molecules do, what conditions allow them to do it, and how we might detect whether they are doing it somewhere else.

## What Life Actually Runs On

The atom at the center of everything biological is carbon.

Not because it is rare — carbon is the fourth most abundant element in the universe — but because of what it can do with a bond. Carbon has four electrons available for bonding with other atoms. It can bond to four things simultaneously, including to other carbons, creating chains and rings of arbitrary length and complexity. Nitrogen, oxygen, and hydrogen attach to these carbon scaffolds, creating regions of different electrical character — patches that attract water, patches that repel it, patches that attract specific other molecules. This variety of shape and charge is what makes biological molecules able to recognize each other, to fit together like keys and locks, to catalyze reactions and store information.

No other abundant element has this combination of properties. Silicon also has four bonding electrons and sits just below carbon on the periodic table. It can form chains. But silicon bonds are stiffer, harder to break and reform. Life requires dynamic chemistry — molecules that assemble, do work, and come apart. Silicon chemistry is slow and rigid by comparison. It may be capable of supporting some form of chemistry we haven't imagined, but the organic chemistry that life on Earth uses — the chemistry that can be assembled and disassembled rapidly, that can fold into precise shapes, that can encode information in sequences — belongs to carbon.

Carbon cannot work alone. It needs water.

Water's usefulness comes from its polarity. The oxygen end of a water molecule is slightly negatively charged; the hydrogen ends are slightly positive. This makes water an extraordinary solvent: it pulls charged and polar molecules into solution, giving them freedom to move, collide, and react. It gives biochemical reactions a medium to occur in. This is not incidental. Life does not happen in the solid state; it happens in solution, where molecules can find each other.

Water's other gift is density. Ice floats. This seems trivial until you think about the alternative: if ice sank, every pond and lake in a cold climate would freeze solid from the bottom up, killing whatever lived there. Instead, ice forms on the surface and insulates the liquid beneath. Life in cold environments survives because water has this quirk.

Together, carbon and water create a platform on which something extraordinary becomes possible.

The something extraordinary is a molecule that can copy itself.

Consider what that requires. You need a molecule that carries information — a sequence of some kind that specifies something. You need machinery to read that sequence and produce something useful, like a catalyst. And you need a way to copy the sequence so the information persists through time.

Modern life separates these functions. DNA stores information. Proteins do the work. Copying DNA requires proteins; making proteins requires DNA. This is the famous chicken-and-egg problem of the origin of life. Neither works without the other, so how did the first one start?

The answer that most biologists find compelling is RNA.

RNA is a simpler cousin of DNA. It can store sequences of information, like DNA. But it can also fold into three-dimensional shapes and catalyze reactions, like proteins. RNA can do both jobs. It is simultaneously the recipe and the chef. The hypothesis — called the RNA world — proposes that the earliest life used RNA alone, storing information and executing chemistry in a single molecule. DNA and proteins came later, as refinements: DNA is more stable for long-term storage, proteins are more versatile catalysts. Natural selection eventually replaced the original RNA machinery with more specialized components.

No one has found RNA world fossils. The transition, if it happened, was over three billion years ago, and the earliest organisms we can identify from the fossil record already had both DNA and proteins. But the hypothesis is chemically plausible, and researchers have now produced RNA molecules in the laboratory that can copy themselves and catalyze reactions. The RNA world is not proven. But it is no longer speculative. It is a testable model, and the tests are not failing.

The point to carry forward is this: life's machinery, strange as it appears, is fundamentally chemistry. Atoms doing what atoms do — forming bonds, exchanging electrons, finding configurations of lower energy. The complexity of life is real, but it is the complexity of chemistry, not the complexity of something outside nature. The building blocks are common. The conditions that assemble them are common. The question is only whether they assembled here by chance, or whether they assemble wherever conditions allow.

## Where the Conditions Allow

The concept of a habitable zone sounds simple: the region around a star where water could be liquid on a planet's surface. Not too hot, not too cold. Goldilocks. But the concept unravels quickly as you look at it.

Venus is closer to the Sun than Earth but reflects much more sunlight — its thick clouds bounce about 70 percent of incoming light back into space. By simple energy balance, Venus should not be as hot as it is. The reason it is 460 degrees Celsius at the surface is its atmosphere: almost pure carbon dioxide, with clouds of sulfuric acid, creating a greenhouse effect so intense that the surface glows in infrared. The planet had water once, in its early history. That water evaporated under the young Sun, rose into the atmosphere, and was broken apart by ultraviolet radiation. Without water, there was nothing to absorb CO₂ from the atmosphere. The greenhouse intensified. The water was gone.

Mars is the reverse. Mars is small — less than one-tenth Earth's mass — and small planets lose their interior heat quickly. As the interior cooled, the magnetic field died. Without a magnetic field, the solar wind gradually stripped away the atmosphere. The pressure fell. Water could no longer remain liquid at the surface. Mars dried.

Earth is between these failures. Its interior is still warm enough to drive plate tectonics, which cycles carbon dioxide in and out of the atmosphere through volcanic outgassing and weathering, maintaining a roughly stable greenhouse. Its magnetic field deflects the solar wind. Its oceans cover seventy percent of the surface, acting as a massive heat buffer.

But here is the part the simple habitable zone picture misses: the Sun was about thirty percent dimmer when Earth formed 4.6 billion years ago. By simple calculation, early Earth should have been frozen solid. Yet we have geological evidence of liquid water on Earth's surface as far back as 3.8 billion years. Something kept it warm.

The answer is almost certainly methane. Early methane-producing microbes may have been the difference between a frozen Earth and a habitable one. Life, in other words, may have been the condition for its own survival. The biosphere modifies the atmosphere to keep itself alive. This is not a comfortable fact for the concept of a fixed habitable zone — it suggests that once life exists, it changes the rules.

The habitable zone concept becomes even shakier when you consider moons.

Europa, orbiting Jupiter, is far outside the Sun's habitable zone. Its surface temperature is around minus 160 degrees Celsius. Yet beneath its ice crust — probably several kilometers thick — there is almost certainly a liquid ocean. The evidence is in the ice itself: the surface is crisscrossed with cracks and ridges that look like the ice is floating on something fluid, and when Galileo flew past, its magnetometer detected an induced magnetic field, the signature of a conducting liquid layer. The liquid is kept from freezing not by sunlight but by tidal heating: Jupiter's enormous gravity stretches and squeezes Europa as it orbits, generating heat in the moon's interior through friction.

Enceladus, orbiting Saturn, has something even more striking: active geysers. Water vapor and ice particles erupt from the south polar region at about 200 kilograms per second, feeding Saturn's E ring. Cassini flew through these plumes and tasted them: salt, organic molecules, silica particles that indicate hot water dissolving rock. There is an ocean beneath the ice, in contact with the rocky seafloor, with chemistry happening at the water-rock interface.

This is what hydrothermal vents look like on Earth. And on Earth, hydrothermal vents support entire ecosystems that run on chemical energy rather than sunlight. Bacteria and archaea extract energy from the oxidation of hydrogen, sulfur, and iron. These organisms don't know there is a Sun. They have never needed it. They are powered by the planet's interior.

If life can run on chemistry rather than light, then the habitable zone — defined by proximity to the star — is the wrong concept. The right concept is the presence of liquid water and chemical energy gradients. By that standard, Europa and Enceladus are candidates. Mars might once have been one, and its subsurface might still be. There may be worlds throughout the galaxy, far from any star, warmed by radioactive decay or tidal friction, hosting liquid water in their interiors.

We are searching in the wrong places if we search only in the habitable zone.

But the habitable zone is where we can see.

## How We Would Know

A biosignature is a feature of a planet that can only be explained by life.

On Earth, the most obvious one is oxygen. Twenty percent of our atmosphere is molecular oxygen — O₂. This is chemically remarkable. Oxygen reacts with almost everything: rock, iron, reduced gases. Without constant replenishment, atmospheric oxygen would be gone in about a million years. The replenishment comes from photosynthesis. Every cyanobacterium and plant on Earth is pumping oxygen into the air. Without them, it would vanish.

From across the galaxy, an observer with the right instruments could detect oxygen in Earth's atmosphere. Combined with methane — also present in trace amounts, and also replenished by living things — the simultaneous presence of these reactive gases is almost impossible to explain without biology. They should react with each other and cancel out. The fact that both persist means something is constantly regenerating both. That something is life.

The James Webb Space Telescope is now capable of detecting the atmospheric composition of some exoplanets. Not Earth-sized ones orbiting Sun-like stars — those are still beyond reach. But rocky planets orbiting small, cool stars, called red dwarfs, are more accessible: the planet is closer to the star (keeping it warm despite the star's dimness), and the star is small enough that the planet blocks a detectable fraction of the starlight when it transits. By comparing the spectrum of the star with the planet in front versus not, you can identify what wavelengths the atmosphere absorbs — which tells you what molecules are present.

In 2023, JWST detected carbon dioxide and other molecules in the atmosphere of exoplanet K2-18b. This is not a biosignature. But it is the first direct detection of atmospheric chemistry on a potentially habitable exoplanet, and it demonstrates the method. The technology is advancing fast enough that genuine biosignature detection may happen in the next decade or two.

There is a critical complication: false positives. Oxygen can be produced abiotically on certain worlds. Ultraviolet light can break water vapor apart, releasing oxygen without any biology involved. Hydrogen escapes to space faster than oxygen, so the oxygen accumulates. An observer seeing oxygen in such an atmosphere would be misled.

This is why biosignature detection requires not just detecting one molecule but constructing a coherent picture. Oxygen plus methane plus the specific colors of chlorophyll in reflected light, together, form a pattern difficult to explain without biology. Any single signal can be mimicked by geology or photochemistry. The combination is harder to fake.

Carl Sagan, writing about this, noted that Earth itself looked ambiguous in the Galileo spacecraft's data when it flew past in 1990. If you did not know what you were looking at, you could construct non-biological explanations for most of what Galileo saw. The standard for claiming a detection of life elsewhere should be extraordinarily high. False discovery would be one of the most consequential errors in scientific history.

## The Framework for Honest Uncertainty

In 1961, Frank Drake attempted to organize the problem of how many communicating civilizations might exist in the galaxy into a single equation. He was preparing for a small conference at the National Radio Astronomy Observatory — the first ever devoted to the search for extraterrestrial intelligence. He needed a way to structure the discussion:

$$N = R_\star \times f_p \times n_e \times f_l \times f_i \times f_c \times L$$

Each term asks a question. $R_\star$: how fast do stars form? About ten per year. $f_p$: what fraction have planets? Close to one — we now know most stars have planets. $n_e$: how many of those planets per system are in the habitable zone? Probably at least one per system on average. $f_l$: of habitable worlds, on what fraction does life arise? Unknown. $f_i$: of worlds with life, on what fraction does intelligence emerge? Unknown. $f_c$: of intelligent species, what fraction develop technology capable of transmitting detectable signals? Unknown. $L$: how long does such a civilization broadcast? Unknown.

The known quantities multiply to something large. The unknowns could reduce that to almost anything.

What the Drake equation actually accomplishes is not a calculation but a diagnosis. It shows you where the ignorance is concentrated. The first three terms we now know reasonably well. It is $f_l$ — whether life starts whenever the conditions allow, or only rarely — that would most change our picture of the galaxy. One more example of life, anywhere — a microbe under the Martian crust, an organism in Europa's ocean, a biosignature in an exoplanet atmosphere — would immediately constrain $f_l$ more than any theory could.

And then there is $L$.

If we are thinking about communicating civilizations, the only way for many to exist simultaneously is if they last a long time. If technological civilizations typically destroy themselves within a few centuries of achieving the ability to broadcast, there might be at most a handful in the entire galaxy at any moment, separated by thousands of light-years. If they typically persist for millions of years, the galaxy could be full of them.

This is a sobering point. The bottleneck in the Drake equation may not be the origin of life. It may not be the evolution of intelligence. It may be whether intelligent species survive their own technology long enough to fill the cosmos. The question "are we alone?" turns out to hinge partly on a question we are living through right now.

## The Shape of the Search

We have been searching for extraterrestrial radio signals since 1960, when Drake pointed the Green Bank telescope at two nearby stars and listened. We heard nothing. In the sixty years since, we have searched more stars, more frequencies, more carefully, and have heard nothing recognizable as artificial.

This is not proof that no one is transmitting. The search space is enormous. The electromagnetic spectrum contains billions of possible frequencies. There are billions of stars to point at. For most of that time, we could search only one frequency at a time, pointing at only one star. Modern technology allows simultaneous searching across millions of frequencies and multiple stars. The Breakthrough Listen project, launched in 2016 with substantial funding, has surveyed more stars in a few years than all previous SETI programs combined.

The absence of a signal thus far is not a surprise. It is consistent with either "no one is out there" or "we haven't looked hard enough yet" or "they're not transmitting in ways we'd recognize." We cannot distinguish between these.

There is also the Fermi paradox, named for physicist Enrico Fermi who reportedly asked at a lunch in 1950: where is everybody? If intelligent civilizations are common and persist for a long time, some of them should have had billions of years to spread through the galaxy, colonizing star systems, leaving visible traces. The galaxy shows no such traces. The absence of obvious evidence for extraterrestrial civilization is itself a kind of datum.

Various solutions to the Fermi paradox have been proposed. Perhaps the galaxy is effectively empty except for us. Perhaps civilizations turn inward rather than outward — into virtual reality, into self-modification, into pursuits that don't require expanding into space. Perhaps they are transmitting in ways we don't recognize — compressed, encrypted, at frequencies we haven't thought to monitor. Perhaps the distances are simply too vast and the timescales too long for any civilization to encounter another.

We don't know. The paradox remains.

What we do know is that we are, for the first time in Earth's history, technically capable of answering the question. The tools exist. The methods are improving. Within the next few decades, we may have actual spectra of rocky exoplanet atmospheres containing biosignature gases, or we may sample the ocean of Enceladus and find organic chemistry that could only have a biological origin, or we may detect a signal from a distant star that no geological or stellar process can explain.

We sent the Voyager record into space knowing the odds were nearly zero. But the question it asked — is anyone out there? — has moved from the realm of speculation to the realm of science. We will search because not searching would be a form of willful blindness we can no longer afford.

The Copernican principle has been right every time we have tested it. Earth is not the center of the solar system. The Sun is not the center of the galaxy. The Milky Way is not the center of the universe. Each time we assumed our location was special, we were wrong. To assume that life arose only here, only once, in a universe of two trillion galaxies, is the last of the old specialness claims. It may be right. But it deserves to be tested.

What would change my mind: confirmed evidence of even one other origin of life — microbial fossils on Mars, active biology in Europa's ocean, or an unambiguous biosignature in an exoplanet atmosphere — would immediately and profoundly alter our estimate of how common life is. It would be the most important discovery in the history of science.

Still puzzling: how chemistry crossed the threshold to self-replication. We understand the building blocks, we understand the machinery of modern cells, and we have plausible hypotheses about the RNA world. But the specific sequence of events by which non-living chemistry first produced a molecule capable of copying itself — the moment before there was natural selection to guide the process — remains the deepest open question in biology.

# Chapter 20 — Between the Stars: Gas and Dust in Space

*Why the darkness between the lights is not empty — and why that matters.*

---

Edward Emerson Barnard grew up in Nashville in poverty, teaching himself astronomy at night while working as a photographer's aide by day. He was obsessed with finding comets — discovering one could earn a cash prize. He found seventeen.

But something else was bothering him. As he photographed the Milky Way year after year, he noticed dark lanes cutting through the crowded star fields — regions that looked empty, like holes punched in the fabric of the galaxy. The prevailing view was simple: there were just no stars there. Herschel had looked at such a region decades before and written, "Here truly is a hole in heaven."

Barnard disagreed. He compared photographs taken from different locations and watched the dark regions shift against the background stars. That shift is what you expect from something relatively nearby — not from genuine emptiness. He concluded the dark lanes were not voids. They were clouds. Dense enough to block all the light from thousands of stars behind them.

This was a difficult claim. No one could quite imagine a gas tenuous enough to fill the space between stars, dense enough to block starlight, and yet undetectable by any other means. For decades, the substance Barnard suspected existed mainly as absence — as the darkness between the lights.

He was right. The space between the stars is not empty. It is filled with gas and dust, and understanding what that material is and what it does turns out to be essential to understanding where stars come from and where the atoms in your body have been.

---

## What the Interstellar Medium Actually Is

The name for this material is the interstellar medium, or ISM. It is roughly 99% gas and 1% solid particles by mass. The gas is mostly hydrogen and helium — the two most abundant elements in the universe — with trace amounts of carbon, oxygen, nitrogen, and metals. The "dust" is not what you find under furniture. Each grain is a tiny solid particle, ten to a hundred nanometers across — smaller than a virus, roughly the size of a wavelength of visible light. The grain has a rocky or carbon core — silicate minerals or graphite — coated with a frozen mantle of water ice, methane, and ammonia.

The average density of this medium throughout the galaxy is about one atom per cubic centimeter. A sugar cube of interstellar space contains fewer atoms than a single breath you take. The dust is rarer still — a few hundred grains per cubic kilometer of space.

Yet the ISM's total mass is enormous, because the galaxy is enormous. The material between stars amounts to roughly 15% of the mass locked in stars — about ten billion solar masses of gas and dust. Enough raw material to build two billion more stars like the Sun.

This medium is not uniform. It exists in three distinct phases, each at a radically different temperature, all coexisting in rough pressure balance with each other.

The hot phase, at temperatures around a million Kelvin, was a surprise when discovered in the 1960s through X-ray observations. That kind of temperature cannot come from starlight — it requires something more violent. The source is supernovae. When a massive star explodes, it launches material at tens of thousands of kilometers per second. When that material collides with the surrounding ISM, the shock heats the gas to a million degrees. A supernova goes off in the Milky Way roughly every hundred years. The hot bubbles threaded throughout the galaxy are the accumulated aftermath of billions of years of stellar deaths.

The warm phase, at temperatures from a few hundred to several thousand Kelvin, fills most of the volume of the ISM. This is the patchy, turbulent background gas — cold pockets adjacent to warm regions, constantly stirred by the energy from nearby stars.

The cold phase, at temperatures around 10 Kelvin — ten degrees above absolute zero — exists in the densest molecular clouds. These are regions where gravity has pulled gas together and where surrounding dust is thick enough to block the ultraviolet radiation that would otherwise heat and dissociate everything. In the cold and the dark, hydrogen exists not as isolated atoms but as H₂ molecules, and complex chemistry proceeds.

The three phases are not separate compartments. They interpenetrate, push against each other, and exchange material. Stellar radiation heats and ionizes the warm gas. Supernova shocks heat the hot bubbles. Gravity compresses the cold clouds. It is dynamic equilibrium, not static.

## How Dust Reveals Itself

The dust grains are too small and cold to emit visible light on their own. So how do we know they are there? Four independent lines of evidence, each teaching us something different.

**Blocking.** The simplest is what Barnard saw. A dense cloud in the right line of sight makes the stars behind it invisible. Barnard 68, a dark nebula about 160 light-years away, sits in front of thousands of stars. At visible wavelengths, that region of sky looks nearly empty. Point an infrared telescope at it and the background stars reappear — infrared radiation passes through dust more easily than visible light does. The cloud has not moved; the wavelength of observation has changed.

This fact immediately constrains the grain size. If the grains were much smaller than a wavelength of visible light, light would pass around them without interacting — the way sound passes around a dust mote. If the grains were much larger than a wavelength, they would block all colors equally. But dust blocks some colors more than others, which requires grains sized in the range where they interact efficiently with light — the ten to hundred nanometer range, comparable to visible wavelengths. The observations constrain the physics of the grains before you have touched one.

**Reddening.** Dust does not block all colors equally. Small particles scatter short-wavelength light (blue, violet) far more efficiently than long-wavelength light (red, infrared). This is the same physics that makes Earth's sky blue: air molecules scatter the blue component of sunlight in all directions, while the red component passes through more directly. At sunset, the light has passed through a long column of atmosphere and the blues have been scattered away, leaving the Sun appearing red.

When starlight passes through a dust cloud, the same thing happens. Blues are scattered or absorbed. Reds pass through. The star reaches our detectors appearing redder and dimmer than it should. This is called interstellar reddening, and it caused confusion for decades. Astronomers would observe a star that looked red and conclude it must be cool — because cool stars are red. But spectroscopic analysis of the same star's light would show features characteristic of a hot star. The contradiction dissolved when someone understood: the star was not cool. Its light was reddened by intervening dust. The spectrum told the truth about the star. The color told the truth about what lies in between.

**Infrared emission.** Dust grains absorb visible and ultraviolet light from nearby stars and re-radiate that energy as heat — infrared photons. A cold dark cloud completely opaque in visible photographs glows in infrared images. This is why infrared astronomy transformed our ability to study star-forming regions. At wavelengths of two micrometers, dust obscuration is only one-sixth as great as in the visible. You can see twice as far into a dense cloud.

**Reflection.** When a cloud sits close enough to a bright star, the dust scatters the star's light back to us — a reflection nebula. Because small grains scatter blue light more efficiently, reflection nebulae appear bluer than the stars illuminating them. The famous blue haze around the brightest stars of the Pleiades cluster is reflected starlight, preferentially scattering the short wavelengths of the hot stars' light.

## What the Dust Is Made Of

We cannot collect interstellar dust directly. A few meteorites contain microscopic grains that appear to be interstellar in origin — identifiable by isotopic ratios inconsistent with the solar system — but these are rare. Mostly we infer the composition from observations and laboratory experiments.

The constraint is immediate: dust must be made of abundant elements. After hydrogen and helium, the most common elements in the universe are oxygen, carbon, nitrogen, magnesium, silicon, and iron. Any proposed grain composition must be built from these.

Spectroscopic analysis of starlight passing through dust clouds reveals absorption features at specific wavelengths matching the signatures of silicate minerals and carbon compounds — graphite and amorphous carbon. The grains have rocky or carbon-rich cores, in the size range already inferred from the reddening observations. The two independent lines of evidence agree.

In the coldest, densest molecular clouds, those cores acquire icy mantles. In the darkness, shielded from the ultraviolet photons that would break chemical bonds, water vapor freezes onto grains, followed by methane and ammonia. The frozen surfaces become chemically active. Reactions occur on the grain surface that would not occur in the gas phase, because the surface acts as a catalyst. These icy-mantled grains are chemical factories operating at 10 Kelvin in the dark.

The size range — ten to a hundred nanometers — is not arbitrary. It is the range that explains everything observed: efficient scattering of visible light, preferential reddening, blocking that varies with wavelength in the right way. If you tried to explain the observations with grains ten times larger or ten times smaller, you would fail. The observations constrain the physical properties of material you cannot touch.

## Ionized Gas: The Fluorescent Nebula

Pivot now to a completely different part of the medium — the gas near hot, massive stars.

A hot star emits copious ultraviolet radiation. When a UV photon strikes a neutral hydrogen atom, it carries enough energy to knock the electron completely free. The hydrogen is ionized. The free electron is quickly captured by another proton, but arrives in an excited state. As it cascades down through the hydrogen energy levels to the ground state, it emits visible light.

This is fluorescence, exactly the mechanism in a fluorescent tube. In the tube, an electric current excites mercury vapor; electrons drop to lower energy levels emitting UV photons; those UV photons hit a phosphor coating that re-emits visible light. In an H II region — the astronomer's name for an ionized hydrogen cloud near a hot star — the hot star provides the energizing UV, hydrogen atoms are the fluorescent material, and the result is a glowing cloud.

The strongest hydrogen emission line in the visible spectrum is at 656 nanometers — deep red. This is why emission nebulae glow red. Other elements — nitrogen, oxygen, sulfur — are also ionized and contribute additional lines, giving some nebulae their complex color structure. But the red hydrogen line dominates.

The edge of an H II region is sharp. The star's UV photons are absorbed in the ionization process. At a certain distance from the star, the radiation field falls below the threshold needed to keep the hydrogen ionized. Beyond that boundary, neutral hydrogen survives. The transition is abrupt — bright ionized gas on one side, dark neutral gas on the other. The interior runs at about 10,000 Kelvin. Outside it, neutral gas may be a hundred times cooler.

## Neutral Hydrogen: The 21-Centimeter Line

Most of the hydrogen in the galaxy is neither ionized nor in molecular clouds. It is ordinary neutral hydrogen — single atoms, neither stuck together nor stripped apart. For much of the twentieth century, it was largely unmappable. It emits no visible light. It has no strong ultraviolet transitions. Observation required a different approach entirely.

In 1944, a Dutch student named Hendrik van de Hulst made a prediction. A hydrogen atom consists of a proton and an electron, both of which act as though they are spinning. When the spins are aligned — pointing in the same direction — the atom has slightly higher energy than when the spins are opposed. The energy difference is tiny: about one-billionth of an electron-volt. When an aligned-spin atom spontaneously flips to the anti-aligned state, it releases this energy as a radio photon with a wavelength of 21 centimeters.

The transition is fantastically slow — a single hydrogen atom might wait ten million years before the flip occurs. But the galaxy contains roughly $10^{57}$ hydrogen atoms. The collective emission from even the rarest transition among that many atoms is detectable. In 1951, radio telescopes detected the 21-centimeter line. Within a few years, it had been used to map the distribution of neutral hydrogen throughout the entire Milky Way.

What the maps showed: the neutral hydrogen is concentrated in an extremely flat disk, less than 300 light-years thick, tracing the spiral arms. The 21-centimeter maps also showed Doppler shifts — the line at different wavelengths in different parts of the galaxy — directly measuring the rotation curve of the Milky Way. The neutral hydrogen gave us a way to take our galaxy's pulse.

## Molecular Clouds: Chemistry in the Dark

In the densest clouds, where gravity and dust shielding have both concentrated the gas and blocked the UV radiation that would destroy molecules, something remarkable happens: complex chemistry.

In the open ISM, ultraviolet photons efficiently dissociate molecules — break them apart. In a dense cloud, the dust intercepts those photons before they penetrate. Molecules accumulate. Hydrogen atoms combine on grain surfaces to form H₂. Once H₂ exists, it catalyzes more complex molecules. Carbon monoxide forms, then formaldehyde, then ammonia and water.

Since the 1960s, millimeter and radio astronomers have detected over 200 distinct molecules in interstellar clouds. The catalog runs from simple diatomics like CO to polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons with dozens of carbon atoms in hexagonal rings to fullerenes — geodesic cages of 60 carbon atoms. In 2025, cyanocoronene was detected — a molecule with 37 atoms, the kind of compound we associate on Earth with combustion chemistry.

None of this indicates life between the stars. What it indicates is something broader: the chemical complexity that underlies biology — amino acids, sugars, ring structures — is not unique to Earth. It forms spontaneously under a wide range of conditions throughout the universe. The chemistry is written into the physics. Wherever a dense, cold, shielded cloud of gas exists and there is enough time, this chemistry happens.

The giant molecular clouds are also the coldest places in the ISM — typically 10 Kelvin — and the sites where new stars form. It is in these dark, molecule-rich, chemically active regions that gravity eventually wins, pulling pockets of gas together until they are dense and hot enough to ignite nuclear fusion. The material that will become a star and its planets begins as a freezing dark cloud full of organic molecules, interstellar ice, and presolar grains older than the solar system.

## What Barnard Saw

Return to the photographs. Barnard's dark lanes were not holes in heaven. They were the cold dense phase of the interstellar medium — molecular clouds, shielded from starlight, cold enough to harbor complex molecules, massive enough to eventually collapse and form new stars.

The full picture took a century to assemble. The hot phase was discovered in the 1960s through X-ray observations. Mapping neutral hydrogen with the 21-centimeter line took off in the 1950s. Molecular clouds were confirmed in the 1970s. The organic chemistry in them is still being catalogued.

The medium is not a backdrop to the story of stars. It is part of the same story. Stars form from dense molecular clouds. When stars die — in stellar winds or supernova explosions — they scatter the heavy elements forged in their cores back into the medium. That material mixes with existing gas and dust, enriching it for the next generation. The iron in your blood was forged in the core of a star that died before the Sun existed. The carbon in your cells was assembled in a stellar interior and scattered by an explosion. That material passed through the interstellar medium before being incorporated into the molecular cloud that became the solar system.

The interstellar medium is not a passive reservoir. It is the cycle. Stars form from it. Stars return to it. The chemistry of life is seeded in it. When Barnard looked at those dark lanes and refused to accept that the darkness was just absence, he was, unknowingly, looking at the cradle of the next generation of stars.

The darkness was not empty. It was waiting.

**Exercise 5 (challenge).** Molecular hydrogen (H₂) is the most abundant molecule in the interstellar medium, but it does not radiate efficiently at the temperatures of cold molecular clouds (~10 K).

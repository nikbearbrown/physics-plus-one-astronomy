# Chapter 26 — Galaxies: Islands in the Dark

*How a flickering star in Andromeda proved the universe was unimaginably larger than anyone believed.*

---

In 1920, the most prominent astronomers in the world could not agree on what they were looking at when they looked at spiral nebulae.

Harlow Shapley thought the spirals were clouds of gas within the Milky Way — relatively nearby, part of our own galaxy. Heber Curtis thought they were separate island universes, systems of stars as large as the Milky Way but so distant that their individual stars blurred into a haze. Both men were brilliant. Both made compelling arguments. The evidence was genuinely ambiguous. The most powerful telescopes available could not settle the question because neither man could measure the distance to a spiral.

Without distance, you cannot tell whether a faint fuzzy patch is a small cloud nearby or an enormous system of stars far away. It is the same problem you face at night on a highway: a light ahead might be a motorcycle a hundred meters distant or a truck ten kilometers away. You need a way to calibrate the light source.

Four years later, Edwin Hubble, working with the new 2.5-meter telescope at Mount Wilson, found a flickering star in the Andromeda nebula — a Cepheid variable — and used it to measure the distance. The number came back outside the Milky Way by a large margin. Andromeda was not a cloud of gas nearby. It was another galaxy, comparable to our own, separated from us by something like a million light-years.

When Hubble presented this result at the American Astronomical Society on New Year's Day 1925, the universe went, in a single evening, from being one galaxy to being something nobody had previously been able to think.

Everything in this chapter flows from that measurement. Understanding what galaxies are, how to classify them, how much they weigh, and what the universe looks like at the largest scales — all of it depends on answering one prior question: how far away is it?

## The Cepheid Method: A Ruler Built from Flicker

The technique Hubble used rests on a discovery made by Henrietta Swan Leavitt in 1912. She was studying variable stars in the Small Magellanic Cloud and noticed something unexpected. The Cepheid variables there — stars that rhythmically brighten and fade — showed a clear pattern: brighter Cepheids had longer periods. A star that took 30 days to complete one cycle was intrinsically more luminous than one that cycled in 10 days.

This is the period-luminosity relation, and it is a gift.

Luminosity compared to apparent brightness gives distance. Leavitt gave astronomers their first ruler that reaches between galaxies."] -->

Here is why. If you observe a Cepheid in a distant galaxy, you measure its period — just watch it over enough nights to record one complete cycle. The period tells you the star's intrinsic luminosity. Compare that to how bright the star appears in your telescope. The difference between how bright it really is and how bright it looks is entirely determined by distance. The inverse-square law — brightness falls as the square of distance — lets you solve for how far away the star must be.

The chain: period tells you luminosity, luminosity compared to apparent brightness gives distance. Each link is testable. The period-luminosity relation was calibrated using Cepheids in the Milky Way, where we verify distances by parallax. If the same relation holds for Cepheids elsewhere — and it does — then we have a ruler that reaches across the gulf between galaxies.

The concrete calculation. A Cepheid with a period of 30 days has an intrinsic luminosity of about $10^4$ solar luminosities. Suppose you observe it in a distant galaxy and measure its apparent flux $f$. Using:

$$d = \sqrt{\frac{L}{4\pi f}}$$

you calculate the distance $d$ directly. If the flux is $10^{-12}$ watts per square meter:

$$d = \sqrt{\frac{10^4 \times 3.8 \times 10^{26}}{4\pi \times 10^{-12}}} \approx 5.5 \times 10^{20} \text{ meters} \approx 58{,}000 \text{ light-years}$$

That places the star, and the galaxy it lives in, far beyond anything in the Milky Way.

The important caveat: the method requires resolving the Cepheid as an individual star against the glow of its host galaxy. For nearby galaxies like Andromeda, this is achievable. For galaxies beyond a few tens of millions of light-years, even the best telescopes cannot isolate individual Cepheids. So the Cepheid method is the first rung of a cosmic distance ladder. For greater distances, astronomers use other indicators — most notably Type Ia supernovae, which briefly outshine entire galaxies and can be seen across billions of light-years. Different methods are cross-checked against each other wherever they overlap.

Hubble's original estimate for Andromeda was off by a factor of 2.4, because he used the wrong type of Cepheid. There are two populations, obeying different period-luminosity relations. Walter Baade discovered this in the 1940s and corrected the distance. This is not a failure of the method — it is science working exactly as it should. The technique was solid; the calibration needed refinement.

## Galaxy Classification: What Shape Tells You

Once Hubble had established that spiral nebulae were independent galaxies, he faced thousands of galactic photographs in every conceivable configuration. His response was taxonomy: sort them by shape.

Hubble's classification divides galaxies into three broad types. The appearance is purely morphological — it describes what the galaxy looks like — but appearance encodes genuine physics.

**Spiral galaxies** are organized into a rotating disk with a central bulge and arms winding outward. The arms are not rigid structures. They are density waves, like slow-moving traffic jams. Cars pile up in a traffic jam and then leave, while the jam itself persists; stars and gas crowd into spiral arms and move through them, while the arm pattern remains. In the arms you find young, hot, blue stars and emission nebulae — evidence that gas is being compressed and forming new stars. The central bulge contains older, redder stars. The disk is dusty, visible as a dark band when the galaxy is seen edge-on.

Hubble subdivided spirals from Sa to Sc. Sa galaxies have large prominent bulges and tightly wound arms; Sc galaxies have tiny bulges and loose open arms. The Milky Way and Andromeda are Sb, intermediate. A majority of nearby spirals also have a bar — a linear structure of stars running through the center from which the arms emerge.

**Elliptical galaxies** show none of this. They are smooth, featureless ovals with no disk, no spiral arms, no visible dust. Their stars are almost exclusively old and red — no young blue stars, no ongoing star formation. Whatever gas existed in an elliptical was consumed or expelled long ago, in a brief intense burst of star formation that ended billions of years before today. Ellipticals range from nearly spherical (E0) to highly flattened (E7), and in total mass from giant systems containing ten trillion solar masses down to dwarfs barely brighter than a globular cluster.

**Irregular galaxies** have no organized symmetry. The Large and Small Magellanic Clouds are the most familiar examples — pulled by gravitational tides from the Milky Way into elongated shapes, with a trail of gas extending between them and us called the Magellanic Stream.

What morphology actually tells you: spiral galaxies have gas, dust, and ongoing star formation. Elliptical galaxies have none of this — their stellar populations are old. Irregulars are often in gravitational encounters or have been disturbed by neighbors.

What morphology does not tell you: a simple evolutionary sequence. There is no universal arrow pointing from one type to another through normal aging. What is true is that mergers between galaxies transform morphology — when two spirals collide and merge, the result is often an elliptical. But spirals do not spontaneously become ellipticals on their own.

## Mass and the Hidden Universe

In the 1930s, Fritz Zwicky was studying the Coma Cluster — hundreds of galaxies bound together by gravity — and performed a calculation that bothered him. He added up all the visible matter and asked whether there was enough mass to explain how fast the galaxies were moving. If the cluster is gravitationally bound, the typical speed of its galaxies cannot exceed what the enclosed mass can hold.

The galaxies were moving far too fast. By a large factor. The visible matter was completely insufficient. Zwicky proposed that there must be additional mass — dark matter — invisible but gravitationally present. For decades, almost nobody took this seriously.

Vera Rubin's work in the 1970s made the case undeniable. She was measuring the rotation curves of spiral galaxies — how fast stars and gas orbit the galactic center at different radii. According to Kepler's third law, objects farther from the center of mass should orbit more slowly, just as outer planets orbit the Sun more slowly than inner ones. For a galaxy whose mass is concentrated in the central bulge and visible disk, rotation speed should peak near the center and fall off with radius.

It does not fall off. For galaxy after galaxy, the rotation speed is roughly constant far beyond the visible disk. This can only be explained if there is substantial mass in a large dark halo surrounding the visible galaxy — a halo that contains several times more mass than all the visible stars and gas combined.

The mathematics is straightforward. For a circular orbit, gravitational force provides centripetal acceleration:

$$\frac{GM}{r^2} = \frac{v^2}{r}$$

Solving for the mass enclosed within radius $r$:

$$M = \frac{v^2 r}{G}$$

If $v$ is constant as $r$ increases, then $M$ must increase proportionally with $r$. Mass that you cannot see but whose gravitational effects are unmistakable.

Consider Andromeda. Trace the rotation speed of hydrogen gas (using the 21-centimeter Doppler shift) out to 20 kiloparsecs from the center. At 5 kiloparsecs the rotation speed is about 200 km/s. At 20 kiloparsecs it is still about 215 km/s. The mass enclosed at 5 kpc is roughly $10^{11}$ solar masses. At 20 kpc it is $4 \times 10^{11}$ solar masses. But the visible disk contains at most $10^{11}$ solar masses of stars and gas. All that additional mass at large radii is invisible.

This pattern holds for essentially every spiral galaxy measured. The rotation curves are flat. The dark matter halos are real. What dark matter is made of — whether it is an undiscovered fundamental particle or something else — remains the most significant open question in astronomy. That it exists is not in doubt. Every method we have for measuring mass — rotation curves, velocity dispersions, gravitational lensing, the dynamics of galaxy clusters — points to the same conclusion: the visible universe is a small fraction of the total.

## The Expanding Universe

In 1912, Vesto Slipher at the Lowell Observatory began taking spectra of spiral nebulae. By 1914 he had measured the Doppler shifts of a dozen of them. Most were redshifted — their spectral lines shifted to longer wavelengths, indicating recession. The nebulae were moving away from us.

Slipher had no explanation for why. His observations sat for over a decade, accumulated and unexplained, until Hubble combined them with his distance measurements. The pattern that emerged was stark: the farther a galaxy, the faster it recedes. Hubble published this in 1929:

$$v = H_0 d$$

where $v$ is the recession velocity, $d$ is the distance, and $H_0$ is the Hubble constant. Its value is approximately 70 kilometers per second per megaparsec — for every megaparsec of additional distance, a galaxy recedes roughly 70 km/s faster.

The interpretation is not that galaxies are flying through space away from us. The better picture is that space itself is expanding — every point in space is moving away from every other point, and distant regions recede faster simply because there is more expanding space between us and them. The analogy that captures this correctly: raisins in a rising bread loaf. As the dough expands, every raisin moves away from every other raisin, with faster separation for raisins that start farther apart. No raisin is the center. The expansion is in the medium.

Hubble's law means you can use recession velocity as a distance indicator for galaxies far enough that their peculiar motion — their motion through space relative to their local environment — is small compared to the Hubble recession. Measure a galaxy's redshift from its spectrum, apply Hubble's law, and you have its distance.

This is the culmination of the chapter's central theme. Cepheid variables are rulers that reach to nearby galaxies. From there, you calibrate Type Ia supernovae, which reach to billions of light-years. From there, Hubble's law takes you to the edge of the observable universe. The full structure is the cosmic distance ladder: each rung calibrated against the rung below it, reaching progressively farther, with each method cross-checking the others where they overlap.

Hubble's flickering Cepheid in Andromeda was its first rung.

## What Galaxies Are

Step back from the details and look at the picture as a whole.

A galaxy is a gravitationally bound system of stars, gas, dust, and dark matter. Its shape tells you about its stellar population and recent dynamical history — whether it is still forming stars, whether it has undergone a merger. Its rotation or velocity dispersion tells you its total mass, including the dark matter that dominates the outer halo. Its distance tells you when its light was emitted — looking at a distant galaxy is looking backward in time; we see it as it was when the light left, not as it is today.

Together, these facts make galaxies the basic units of cosmic structure. The Milky Way is one of roughly two trillion galaxies in the observable universe. Most are smaller and fainter. Many are organized into groups and clusters, which are themselves organized into filaments and walls, with enormous voids between them. That large-scale structure — the cosmic web — is the subject of what comes next.

But all of it was made possible by one observation: a variable star in Andromeda, brightening and fading with a period that Hubble recognized, carrying in its flicker the distance to another galaxy.

The universe turns out to be full of islands. You just needed a way to see how far the water goes.

# Chapter 14 — Cosmic Samples and the Origin of the Solar System

*What a stone that fell from the sky in 1969 tells us about events that happened before the Sun existed.*

---

Every living thing on Earth uses left-handed amino acids.

This is not because right-handed amino acids cannot exist — they can, and in the laboratory you can make both versions, and they are chemically almost identical. But somewhere in the deep history of life, before there was anything we would recognize as an organism, the chemistry that would become life chose the left-handed form and locked it in. Bacteria, plants, animals, fungi — all of them, without exception, use the left-handed versions of the twenty amino acids that matter.

In November 1969, a meteorite broke apart over Murchison, Australia, and scattered dark lumps across a paddock. Scientists collected the pieces before rain could alter the chemistry. When they ground a fragment to powder, dissolved it in acid, and isolated the amino acids, they found something that took a moment to make sense of.

The Murchison stone contained amino acids in equal parts left-handed and right-handed.

That is the proof that what fell on Australia had never passed through any living thing. It had been made by chemistry in a place where no organism had ever existed — chemistry that had no reason to favor one orientation over the other, because there was no life there to make a choice. A stone that formed before Earth existed, carrying molecules that resemble the building blocks of life, indifferent to handedness.

The message in the stone is not just that amino acids exist in space. The message is deeper: the universe was doing chemistry before it was doing biology. The atoms and molecules we think of as belonging to life were being synthesized in interstellar clouds and protoplanetary disks long before any world was warm enough to host a cell. Some of that chemistry arrived here from outside.

What I want to show in this chapter is how we read these stones — how we extract from meteorites and protoplanetary disks the story of where the solar system came from and how it assembled. The chain of evidence runs from chemistry that predates the Sun to images of planets forming around other stars right now.

---

## What the Stones Are

Most of what falls from the sky never reaches the ground. A grain of cometary dust, a few milligrams, enters the atmosphere at tens of kilometers per second. The kinetic energy is enormous relative to the mass. Friction converts it to heat. The grain vaporizes. For a fraction of a second the incandescent gas glows — a meteor, a shooting star — and then it is gone.

The pieces that survive to the ground come from a different source: asteroids, not comets. Asteroids are denser, their fragments larger, their impact velocities lower. A fist-sized piece of asteroid, entering at 15 kilometers per second, loses its outer layers to ablation but retains its core. It slows down. It falls. It lands.

Our understanding of these stones changed fundamentally with Antarctica. Since the 1980s, more than ten thousand meteorites have been collected from the Antarctic ice sheets. The mechanism is elegant: a stone falls somewhere on the ice, is buried and slowly transported by the ice's flow toward the Transantarctic Mountains. There, where wind strips the surface ice faster than it can be replaced, the stone emerges — sitting on white ice, easy to spot, preserved in the cold against weathering and chemical alteration. Meteorites that fell across a continent over thousands of years concentrate in these ablation zones. Most of what we know about the early solar system comes from rocks preserved by this single quirk of one planet's climate.

The most important classification of meteorites is not what they look like but what happened to them. Some come from bodies that were once molten — asteroids that grew large enough to generate internal heat, differentiated by density into core, mantle, and crust, and were later shattered by collisions. An iron meteorite is literally a piece of someone else's planetary core, ripped out by an ancient impact and eventually delivered here. These have been scrambled by heat. Their original chemistry is gone.

The others — the majority — come from bodies that were never melted. They are made of material that condensed from the solar nebula, stuck together, and has sat essentially undisturbed ever since. These primitive meteorites are the closest thing we have to a sample of the material from which the solar system was made. The most primitive class is the carbonaceous chondrites, of which Murchison is one. They contain chondrules — small round silicate spheres that were briefly molten droplets in the hot nebula before they froze — and a dark carbon-rich matrix that has seen almost no heat processing. Inside them, if you know how to look, are grains even older than the solar system itself.

## Stardust Inside the Stone

Here is the thing that still strikes me as remarkable. Inside some carbonaceous chondrites, we find individual grains of material that formed in other stars, before our Sun existed.

To identify them, we use isotope ratios. Carbon has two stable isotopes: carbon-12 (six protons, six neutrons) and carbon-13 (six protons, seven neutrons). The ratio in which they are produced depends on the nuclear reactions happening in the star where they form. Red giant stars at the end of their lives produce carbon with one characteristic ratio. Supernovae produce a different ratio. Novae produce yet another. These ratios are fingerprints — specific to the stellar process that made the material.

When we dissolve a primitive meteorite in acid, most of it dissolves. But some tiny grains — mostly silicon carbide, sometimes graphite — resist the acid. When we measure the isotope ratios in these grains, some of them have ratios so far from anything that could have formed in our solar system that there is only one interpretation: they formed in a different star.

The Murchison meteorite contains presolar grains. Some have carbon ratios characteristic of red giants. Some carry signatures of supernovae. We can isolate a single grain smaller than a human hair and say: this grain is five billion years old. It formed before our solar system existed. It was expelled from a dying star, wandered through interstellar space as part of the gas and dust between stars, was swept up when the collapsing cloud that became our Sun incorporated the surrounding material into its disk, ended up in an asteroid that was never hot enough to melt it, and then in a meteorite that fell on Australia in 1969, and then into a laboratory.

What this means physically: the solar system did not form from fresh material. The disk around the young Sun contained debris from previous generations of stars — material already processed through stellar furnaces, in which elements had been forged in nuclear reactions and expelled. The atoms in your body have been through this. The carbon in your cells may include atoms made in supernovae that exploded billions of years before Earth existed. We are not the first generation of chemistry that these atoms have participated in.

## Reading the Age

How do we know the solar system is 4.567 billion years old?

The method is radiometric dating, and it is one of the most reliable measurement techniques in science. Every radioactive isotope decays at a fixed rate, characterized by its half-life — the time it takes for half the original atoms to transform into the daughter product. The rate is constant. Temperature does not change it. Pressure does not change it. Chemical bonds do not change it. The nucleus decays on its own schedule, governed by quantum mechanics, indifferent to its surroundings.

Uranium-238 decays to lead-206 with a half-life of 4.468 billion years. If you know when a rock formed — when its minerals crystallized from a melt or condensed directly from a gas, locking the uranium and lead inside — then you can measure the ratio today and work backward. A rock with no lead-206 would be brand new. A rock with equal amounts of uranium-238 and lead-206 would be exactly one half-life old: 4.468 billion years. The ratio is a clock.

The clock works only for closed systems. Once a mineral crystallizes, the uranium and lead are trapped — the uranium decays, the lead accumulates, and nothing escapes. If the mineral is later melted, the lead escapes and the clock resets. This is actually useful: a meteorite from a once-molten asteroid gives the date when it solidified, not when the solar system formed. A primitive meteorite from a body that was never melted gives the date when the original nebular material first solidified. That is the date we want.

The reliability of the method comes from independent confirmation. Uranium decays to lead, but rubidium decays to strontium with a different half-life, and potassium decays to argon with yet another. If a primitive meteorite gives the same age from three independent decay chains, that convergence is not a coincidence. The number that converges for primitive meteorites is 4.567 billion years, with an uncertainty of less than ten million years — less than one quarter of one percent of the total age.

4.567 billion years is when the first solid grains condensed from the cooling solar nebula. This is when the clock started ticking. Not when the cloud began to collapse — that was earlier. Not when the planets fully assembled — that took tens of millions of years more. The meteorites preserve the specific moment when chemistry first locked into solid form.

## How a Cloud Becomes a Solar System

Something, roughly 4.6 billion years ago, triggered the collapse of a region of gas and dust between the stars. The trigger may have been a nearby supernova — a shock wave of compressed gas pushing the cloud past the point where gravity could overwhelm the internal pressure holding it up. The cloud began to fall inward.

As it fell, it heated. Gravitational potential energy converts to kinetic energy, kinetic energy heats the gas. But the cloud was also rotating — slowly, but rotating. A slowly rotating cloud, as it contracts, spins faster, for the same reason a spinning skater accelerates when she pulls her arms in. Conservation of angular momentum.

Near the poles, where rotation was slowest, gas fell directly onto the forming star. Near the equator, where rotation was fastest, centrifugal effects balanced gravity in the horizontal direction. Material could not fall straight inward — it spread outward into a rotating disk. Thin, flat, orbiting. This is the solar nebula.

The nebula was hot at first, then cooled. As it cooled, materials condensed in a predictable sequence — the sequence of chemistry, determined by the freezing points of different compounds. Very close to the Sun, where temperatures were highest, only the most refractory materials could solidify: metallic iron and nickel, and mineral compounds rich in calcium and aluminum. These calcium-aluminum-rich inclusions, the CAIs, are the oldest solid material we can measure in the solar system. Their ages are the ages we date at 4.567 billion years.

Farther from the Sun, as temperature dropped, silicate minerals condensed. Farther still, water vapor could freeze. The frost line — roughly where the asteroid belt is today, about three times Earth's distance from the Sun — marked the boundary beyond which water ice was stable. Beyond that, methane and ammonia froze.

This temperature gradient explains why the planets are what they are. Terrestrial planets close to the Sun formed from rock and metal, the only things that could condense in the hot inner disk. The giant planets formed in the cold outer regions, where solid material included ice in addition to rock. The extra building material let the outer-planet cores grow large enough to trigger gravitational capture of hydrogen and helium from the surrounding nebula — a runaway process that turned rocky cores into gas giants. Jupiter probably swept up a third of all the gas remaining in the disk.

Earth has water despite forming where ice was not stable. The most likely solution: after the terrestrial planets assembled, gravitational interactions between the giant planets and small bodies of the outer solar system flung some of them inward, and some of those impacted the inner planets. Earth's water may be largely an import, delivered hundreds of millions of years after the planet formed.

Grain by grain, pebble by pebble, the solid material in the disk accumulated. Electrostatic forces stuck grains into pebbles. Pebbles accumulated into kilometer-sized planetesimals. Planetesimals collided and merged. The collisions released energy. The forming planets were hot — from impact energy and from the rapid decay of short-lived radioactive isotopes that were more abundant then than now. They melted. Iron sank to form cores. Silicate rock floated up. The rocky planets we have today are the end product of this process, their original chemistry thoroughly scrambled by heat. The primitive meteorites are fragments of bodies that never completed this journey — assembled, but not melted, not differentiated, their original chemistry intact.

## What We Are Watching Happen Now

For decades, our theory of planet formation rested on a sample size of one. Then, in 1995, an exoplanet was found orbiting another star every four days — a Jupiter-mass world in an orbit smaller than Mercury's.

This was impossible under the old theory. Jupiter-sized planets cannot form close to a star. There is not enough solid material there; the cores cannot grow large enough to capture gas. So the planet did not form there. It formed farther out and migrated inward, dragged by gravitational interactions with the gas disk in which it was embedded. This was not a flaw in the theory so much as a simplification we had not accounted for. Planets move.

The exoplanet census that followed revealed something more unsettling: our solar system is not typical. The most common type of planet in the galaxy appears to be the super-Earth — a planet between Earth's and Neptune's mass, in orbits of a few months. We have none. Hot Jupiters are common; our giant planets are far from the Sun. Whatever our solar system is, it is not the template.

But even as the exoplanet observations reframed expectations, another kind of observation confirmed the basic picture.

The Atacama Large Millimeter Array — ALMA, a network of radio telescopes in the Chilean desert — images protoplanetary disks around young stars. In 2014 it produced an image of the disk around a star called HL Tau, about 450 light-years away. HL Tau is only about a million years old. The image showed concentric rings of dust separated by dark gaps — multiple rings, multiple gaps, unmistakable structure. The gaps are cleared by planets. A growing protoplanet gravitationally perturbs the material at its orbital distance, sweeping it away, opening a lane. The structure in the HL Tau disk is the footprint of multiple forming planets, each carving its orbital zone.

A million years old. Theoretical models had suggested it might take several million years to form objects massive enough to shape a disk. HL Tau said: no, the planets are already there, already shaping things, before the disk has had much time to evolve. The process is faster than we thought.

## What the Evidence Tells

Here is the coherent picture.

A cloud collapses. Angular momentum flattens it into a disk. The disk cools from inside out, and materials condense in temperature sequence — metals near the star, silicates farther out, ices at and beyond the frost line. Grains stick together, grow into planetesimals. Some planetesimals reach the threshold where their gravity pulls in more material faster than collisions can destroy them. They become planets. The inner planets form from rock and metal. The outer planets form from rock, ice, and captured gas.

The first solid grains to condense — preserved in primitive meteorites — carry the isotopic record of that condensation: 4.567 billion years ago. The presolar grains inside those meteorites carry the record of earlier stellar generations, of supernovae that exploded before the Sun existed. The amino acids in the Murchison stone carry the record of chemistry in the void — chemistry that had no reason to prefer left over right.

We can watch this process now, around HL Tau and many other young stars, seeing the gaps and rings that indicate planets already shaping the disk. The process happening there is the process that happened here, 4.5 billion years ago. The details differ — each disk has different mass, temperature, composition. But the mechanism is the same.

What still puzzles me: we do not fully understand how the planets in the HL Tau disk grew large enough to clear gaps in only a million years. Pebble accretion — the idea that planets grow quickly by sweeping up centimeter-sized pebbles spiraling inward through the gas — may be the answer, but the details of going from pebble to protoplanet at the observed timescale remain an active problem.

And then there is Murchison. Left-handed and right-handed amino acids in equal parts. We know the molecules were made in space. We do not know whether this delivered chemistry had anything to do with the chemistry that eventually became living things on Earth. That is a different question, and we do not have the answer.

What we do know is that the stone that fell in Australia was not a visitor from outside. It was a fragment of what we came from — a piece of the early solar system that survived 4.5 billion years without being melted or altered, waiting on the ice or in a paddock to be found. It tells us when. It tells us how. And in the amino acids with their equal handedness, it reminds us that the universe was doing chemistry long before it was doing biology, and that the line between the two may not be as sharp as it seems.

# Chapter 11 — The Giant Planets: Worlds of Hydrogen and Fire

*What happens when a planet grows large enough to change the rules entirely.*

---

On August 24, 1989, Voyager 2 flew past Neptune. It was traveling at about ten kilometers per second — ten times the speed of a rifle bullet — and it was 4.8 billion kilometers from Earth. The radio signal from the spacecraft took more than four hours to arrive.

No human operator could steer Voyager in real time. The sequence of camera moves had been programmed months in advance: the spacecraft had to slew its camera at precisely the rate needed to compensate for its own motion during the exposure, or the image would blur. The geometry was calculated on Earth by people who would not see the result for hours after it happened.

What they saw when the signal arrived: Neptune, a pale blue sphere, with white clouds moving at supersonic speeds and a dark storm system the size of Earth rotating through its atmosphere. Rings, faint and delicate. Moons.

That flyby is the only close look humanity has ever had at Neptune. Voyager 2 is the only spacecraft to have visited all four giant planets. After Neptune, it kept going. It carries a golden record with greetings in fifty-five languages and the sound of Johnny Cash. It is still transmitting. We are still listening.

I want to tell you why Neptune looks the way it looks — why it is a different kind of world from Earth, built from different materials, running on different physics. The story starts not with Neptune, but with a question about what the early solar system was made of and why the planets ended up so different depending on where they formed.

---

## Why Some Planets Got Hydrogen and Others Did Not

Here is something I want you to hold onto. Hydrogen and helium are the most abundant elements in the universe. Roughly three-quarters of all ordinary matter, by mass, is hydrogen. Most of the rest is helium. Everything else — oxygen, carbon, iron, silicon, all the stuff that makes up rocks and atmospheres and living things — is a small fraction of what is out there.

The early solar system was a disk of gas and dust swirling around the young Sun, and most of that disk was hydrogen and helium. The question is not why the giant planets captured so much hydrogen. The question is why the inner planets did not.

The answer has two parts. First, temperature. Near the Sun, the early solar nebula was hot. Hydrogen and helium are light gases, and at high temperatures the thermal motion of those atoms and molecules was fast enough for them to escape from small gravitational fields. Earth, Venus, and Mars were too small and the gas was too hot. What they kept were the heavy things: iron, silicates, rock and metal.

Second, the frost line. Near the Sun, only materials with high melting points could condense into solid grains. Farther out — past a distance of about three to four times the Earth-Sun distance — temperatures dropped enough for water to freeze. Ice. Beyond that, methane and ammonia froze too. These ices, mixed with rock, gave the forming outer planets a much larger pool of solid material to accumulate. A growing planet at Jupiter's distance had not just rock to gather, but ice on top of it.

Here is the critical sequence. A growing planet needs a solid core before it can capture gas. You cannot gravitationally trap a hydrogen atmosphere if there is nothing to trap it against. But once a core reaches roughly ten times the mass of Earth, its gravity becomes strong enough to begin pulling in the surrounding hydrogen and helium. And once that starts, it feeds on itself. More gas means more mass, which means stronger gravity, which means more gas. A gravitational runaway. Jupiter's core hit that threshold and ran away, sweeping up about a third of all the remaining gas in the entire solar nebula.

This is why Jupiter is 318 times more massive than Earth. It is not that Jupiter was especially lucky. It formed at the right distance at the right time, in a region where ice supplemented rock to build a large enough core before the gas dispersed.

Saturn did the same thing, but formed farther out with less material available. It accumulated 95 Earth masses — enormous, but noticeably less than Jupiter.

Uranus and Neptune, forming even farther out and even later, in a region of the nebula more sparsely populated, grew cores that may have each reached 15 Earth masses but never triggered runaway gas capture. They have hydrogen and helium envelopes, but modest ones. They are ice giants: worlds dominated by water, methane, and ammonia under pressure, with a relatively thin hydrogen atmosphere on top. Different in kind from Jupiter and Saturn, not just in size.

The solar system's architecture is a direct consequence of this chemistry. The frost line is a physical fact about where water freezes at a given temperature. Gravity is a physical fact about mass. What we see in the outer solar system is what happens when you apply those facts to the available material over a few million years.

## What Pressure Does to Hydrogen

Now I want to talk about what the inside of Jupiter is actually like, because it is one of the strangest things in the solar system.

On Earth, hydrogen is a gas — colorless, light, two-atom molecules bouncing around at room temperature. Compress it enough and it liquefies. Liquid hydrogen is familiar to engineers; it is used as rocket propellant. But go deeper into Jupiter, where the pressure reaches millions of bars, and something surprising happens.

In ordinary liquid hydrogen, each electron stays associated with its own atom — localized, bound. At a few million bars, the hydrogen atoms are packed so tightly that electrons have no room to remain localized. They are forced to roam freely through the material, the way electrons do in a metal. Hydrogen becomes metallic hydrogen.

This is not a metaphor. Metallic hydrogen conducts electricity. It is shiny. It behaves like a metal in all the ways that matter. We have created it briefly in laboratories using shock waves, but inside Jupiter it exists permanently, sustained by the weight of everything above. The ocean of metallic hydrogen in Jupiter's interior is tens of thousands of kilometers deep.

Why does this matter? Because moving electrical conductors generate magnetic fields. The metallic hydrogen in Jupiter's interior, set into motion by heat rising from the core and stirred by the planet's ten-hour rotation, creates a dynamo — the same mechanism that generates Earth's magnetic field, but far more powerful. Jupiter's magnetic field is about twenty thousand times stronger than Earth's. It extends 63 Earth radii toward the Sun and several hundred Earth radii in the downwind direction. It is the largest structure in the solar system, dwarfing even the Sun's corona.

Here is a rough way to estimate the central pressure in Jupiter. The pressure at the center of a self-gravitating sphere of gas scales approximately as:

$$P_{\text{center}} \sim \frac{GM^2}{R^4}$$

Plugging in Jupiter's mass ($1.9 \times 10^{27}$ kg) and radius ($7 \times 10^7$ m), with the gravitational constant $G = 6.67 \times 10^{-11}$ N m² kg⁻², you get roughly $10^{12}$ pascals. One bar is $10^5$ pascals, so this is about $10^7$ bars — tens of millions of bars. That is indeed the pressure range at which hydrogen becomes metallic. The rough calculation and the detailed models agree.

Saturn has metallic hydrogen too, but less of it — the planet is less massive, so interior pressures are lower. Saturn's magnetic field is still substantial but much weaker than Jupiter's.

Uranus and Neptune are too small to create metallic hydrogen at all. Their interiors are dominated by ices under pressure — water, methane, and ammonia in states we have no everyday names for, neither solid nor liquid in the usual sense, but conducting electricity through ionic motion. They still generate magnetic fields through this mechanism, but the resulting fields have unusual geometries — tilted and offset from the rotation axes in ways that Earth's and Jupiter's are not. The misalignment suggests the conducting region in each planet is not a deep central shell but something shallower and more irregular.

There is one more remarkable thing about Jupiter's interior: it radiates about twice as much energy as it receives from the Sun. The extra heat is primordial — leftover warmth from the planet's formation 4.5 billion years ago, when infalling material released gravitational energy. Jupiter is so massive and so well-insulated by its own hydrogen envelope that it has not yet finished cooling.

Saturn has a second heat source on top of the primordial one. As Saturn cools, helium — slightly heavier than hydrogen — condenses into droplets that sink toward the core, releasing gravitational energy as they fall. Saturn is still differentiating, still settling, still generating heat from the slow rain of helium toward its center. As a consequence, Saturn radiates more energy than its size alone would predict, and its atmosphere near the cloud tops is depleted of helium relative to Jupiter's.

Uranus, strangely, has almost no internal heat source. It radiates about as much energy as it receives from the Sun, meaning it has cooled nearly to equilibrium. Why Uranus cooled so efficiently while Neptune — similar in size and composition — still shows a significant internal heat source is one of the genuinely unresolved puzzles of planetary science. The leading hypothesis is that some early collision disrupted Uranus's interior in a way that suppresses efficient heat transport to the surface. We do not know.

## The Storms That Never Hit Land

The most famous feature on Jupiter is the Great Red Spot: a storm larger than Earth, rotating with a period of about six days, which has been visible in telescopes for at least three hundred and fifty years. I want to explain why it persists, because the answer illuminates both Jupiter and Earth.

On Earth, hurricanes form over warm ocean water. They are heat engines, extracting energy from the temperature difference between the warm surface and the cold upper atmosphere. A typical hurricane lasts days to weeks. The main reason it stops is that it moves over land. Friction with the irregular terrain — mountains, forests, buildings — extracts energy from the rotating air and the storm collapses.

Jupiter has no land. Descend through the clouds and you move from gas to denser gas to liquid. There is a rocky core at the center, but it is buried under 60,000 kilometers of fluid. The atmosphere is in contact with itself all the way down. Without a solid surface to create friction, storms lose energy only through the internal viscosity of hydrogen — and that viscosity is very low. Storms persist for extraordinarily long times.

But the Great Red Spot is not just a persistent hurricane. It is an anticyclone embedded in Jupiter's global belt-and-zone circulation. That circulation is maintained by Jupiter's fast rotation and its internal heat, and the spot is locked between two opposing jet streams that actually feed it energy through the shear on either side.

Jupiter's day is less than ten hours — the fastest rotation of any planet in the solar system. This fast rotation, through the Coriolis effect, organizes atmospheric circulation into thin bands running east-west, parallel to the equator, rather than the large spinning systems characteristic of slow rotators. The light zones in Jupiter's atmosphere are regions of rising air, capped by high white ammonia clouds. The darker belts are regions of sinking air, where we see deeper and therefore warmer material.

The zonal jets that mark the belt-zone boundaries move at speeds up to several hundred kilometers per hour. You can estimate the wind speed at the outer edge of the Great Red Spot directly: the radius is roughly 10,000 kilometers and the rotation period is six days. The circumference is about 63,000 kilometers. Divide by 144 hours and you get about 440 kilometers per hour — faster than any sustained surface wind on Earth, and this has been running for centuries.

The storm has been shrinking. In the nineteenth century it was large enough to hold three Earths. Now it holds about one. It may eventually disappear. But "eventually" is a long time on Jupiter.

Saturn's atmosphere has a similar belt-and-zone structure, but the ammonia clouds are deeper and the color contrast is reduced. The winds are actually faster than Jupiter's: Saturn's equatorial jet stream reaches about 1,800 kilometers per hour. Saturn occasionally produces enormous storms called Great White Spots, appearing roughly every Saturn year — about 30 Earth years — large enough to be visible from Earth with a backyard telescope.

Uranus and Neptune, lacking strong internal heat sources, have more stable atmospheres. Uranus is nearly featureless in visible light. When Voyager 2 visited Neptune, it photographed a Great Dark Spot similar in form to Jupiter's Great Red Spot. When Hubble looked at Neptune a few years later, the spot was gone. A new one had appeared on the other side of the planet. Jupiter's spot persists for centuries; Neptune's last years. Jupiter's spot is driven by powerful internal heat and locked by strong, stable jet streams. Neptune's storms are driven by weaker, more variable dynamics, and when the conditions that maintain them shift, they dissipate.

## Four Experiments in Planet Formation

Stand back and look at the four giant planets as a sequence, and the pattern emerges clearly.

Jupiter, at five astronomical units, had the most material available and formed first. Icy cores grew large, the runaway gas capture happened early, and the planet swept up an enormous fraction of the nebula's hydrogen and helium. The result: a massive gas giant with a deep interior under extraordinary pressure, metallic hydrogen, a powerful dynamo, a magnetic field of staggering size, vigorous internal heat driving atmospheric convection, and storm systems that have outlasted every human civilization.

Saturn, farther out with less material, captured less gas. Smaller core, weaker magnetic field, but still an active dynamo — supplemented by the ongoing helium rain. Similar atmospheric structure, calmer and more pastel in color because the ammonia haze sits higher and softer in the upper atmosphere.

Uranus and Neptune, forming in the sparsest, coldest part of the nebula, captured cores of ice and rock and then modest hydrogen envelopes, but never triggered runaway. Ice giants, different in kind. Their interiors are dominated by pressurized ices rather than metallic hydrogen. Uranus has cooled to near-equilibrium; Neptune retains a small heat source. Their magnetic fields are off-axis, tilted, generated by ionic conductors rather than metallic ones.

This is not a coincidence, and it is not design. It is gravity and temperature and time, working on the material available at each location in the solar nebula 4.5 billion years ago.

There is a broader lesson in this sequence. A planet is not a given. It is the outcome of initial conditions. Change the distance from the Sun, change the timing of formation, change the amount of nearby material, and you get something entirely different. The four giant planets are four experiments nature ran simultaneously with slightly different starting parameters. All four tell us something about how planets form, how they maintain themselves, and what becomes possible when you give gravity enough material to work with.

We have visited all four of them exactly once. There is an enormous amount we do not know — about the deep interiors, about why Uranus behaves so differently from Neptune, about whether the Great Red Spot will eventually disappear, about the precise composition of the ice giant interiors. These are not minor puzzles. They are the open questions at the frontier of planetary science.

Voyager 2 is still out there, past the heliopause, in interstellar space. It transmits at about 160 bits per second — roughly the data rate of a 1970s telephone modem. It takes more than seventeen hours for the signal to reach Earth. We are still listening, and it is still talking, because that is what you do when you have come this far and there is still something to say.

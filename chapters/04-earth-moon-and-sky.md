# Chapter 4 — The Tilt of Everything: Why Our Seasons Run Backwards

*The seasons are not about distance. They are about angle. Once you see that, everything else follows.*

---

Here is a fact that should bother you.

Earth is closest to the Sun in early January. Not in summer — in winter. The Northern Hemisphere's coldest months occur when the planet is at its nearest point to the Sun in the entire year. If warmth came from proximity, January should be the hottest month, not the coldest.

It is not. And the reason it is not contains, compressed inside it, nearly everything interesting about how Earth moves through space.

---

## The Hypothesis That Should Work But Doesn't

The natural explanation for seasons is distance. You feel warmer near a fire and colder far from it. Earth's orbit is an ellipse, so the distance from the Sun varies. The explanation seems to write itself: summer when close, winter when far.

It is wrong. And it fails in three ways simultaneously, each one more damaging than the last.

The first failure is quantitative. Earth's distance from the Sun varies by about 3% over the year — from 147 million kilometers to 152 million kilometers. Sunlight intensity falls off as the square of distance, so this 3% swing produces roughly a 6% change in solar intensity. That is not enough. The difference between a New York January and a New York July is about 30 degrees Celsius. A 6% variation in solar input cannot produce that.

The second failure is hemispheric. If the seasons were caused by Earth's distance from the Sun, both hemispheres would be hot at the same time and cold at the same time. They are not. They are perfectly out of phase. When New York freezes, Sydney swelters. When Toronto is under snow, Cape Town is at the beach. Every year, without exception. A distance explanation has no mechanism to produce this.

The third failure is the decisive one. Earth is closest to the Sun in early January — the dead of Northern Hemisphere winter. The data do not just fail to support the distance hypothesis. They contradict it directly.

So we need a different mechanism. The mechanism is angle.

---

## What Angle Does to Sunlight

I want you to think about a flashlight beam hitting a wall.

Point the flashlight straight at the wall — perpendicular to the surface. The beam makes a small, concentrated, bright oval. Now tilt the flashlight, so the beam hits at 30 degrees from the surface. The same beam now spreads over a much larger area. The light is not weaker — the same number of photons is arriving — but they are now distributed over more square centimeters. Each square centimeter receives less energy.

<!-- → [IMAGE: two-panel diagram of a flashlight beam hitting a flat surface — left panel: beam perpendicular, small concentrated oval labeled "high Sun angle / summer"; right panel: same beam at ~30° to the surface, elongated oval covering visibly more area, labeled "low Sun angle / winter"; annotate both panels with identical photon counts but different area covered, so the reader sees the dilution effect directly; this is the core physical intuition and should appear immediately after the flashlight analogy is introduced] -->

This is what happens to sunlight hitting Earth's surface at different angles. When the Sun is high in the sky, its light arrives nearly perpendicular to the ground. When the Sun is low — at a shallow angle to the horizon — the same sunlight spreads over a larger surface area. The heating per square meter drops. Not because there is less light, but because the same light is diluted over more ground.

There is a second effect compounding this one. When the Sun is low, sunlight travels through more atmosphere to reach you. The path length through the air is longer at oblique angles. More atmosphere means more absorption and scattering, more loss before the light arrives. So low-angle sunlight is weakened twice: spread over more ground, and partially absorbed on a longer path through the air.

Add a third effect: day length. When the Sun is high in summer, it rises earlier and sets later. At 41 degrees north latitude — roughly New York — the Sun is up for about 15 hours in June. In December, it manages about 9. More intense light, arriving for more hours. The total daily heating is very different.

None of these three effects requires Earth to be closer to the Sun. All they require is that sunlight arrive at different angles at different times of year. The question is: what causes the angle to change?

---

## The Tilt

Earth's axis — the line through the North and South Poles — is not perpendicular to the plane of Earth's orbit. It is tilted 23.5 degrees away from the perpendicular.

This sounds like a small geometric detail. It is the entire explanation.

As Earth orbits the Sun over the course of a year, its axis does not change direction. It keeps pointing toward the same place in the sky — near the star Polaris. It does not lean toward or away from the Sun in response to being pulled or pushed. It just points steadily in one direction while Earth moves around the Sun.

Here is why that matters. If the axis is fixed in space and Earth is orbiting the Sun, then for half the year the Northern Hemisphere is tilted toward the Sun, and for the other half it is tilted away. When the Northern Hemisphere tilts toward the Sun — around June 21 — the Sun appears high in the northern sky. Sunlight arrives close to perpendicular. Days are long. Heating is intense. Summer.

Six months later, Earth is on the opposite side of its orbit. The axis still points toward Polaris — it has not moved. But now that means the Northern Hemisphere is tilted away from the Sun. The Sun appears low in the northern sky. Sunlight arrives at a shallow angle and spreads over more ground. Days are short. Heating is weak. Winter.

The Southern Hemisphere is always doing the opposite. When the North tilts toward the Sun, the South tilts away. When the North tilts away, the South tilts toward. Exactly out of phase. Sydney in January, when New York is cold: the Southern Hemisphere is tilted toward the Sun, and summer follows directly.

The 3% distance variation is still there, doing almost nothing. It slightly moderates Northern Hemisphere winters — Earth is closer to the Sun then, adding a small amount of intensity — and slightly moderates Northern Hemisphere summers, when Earth is farther. But a 6% swing in intensity is overwhelmed by the angle effects. Angle beats distance. The tilt wins.

## The Latitude Effect

The tilt does not affect all places equally. The variation across latitudes is extreme, and it falls out of the geometry without any additional assumptions.

At the equator, the Sun is never far from overhead. The tilt shifts the Sun's noon position by 23.5 degrees north or south over the course of the year, but it is always high. The change in sunlight angle between June and December is modest. Equatorial regions do not have temperature seasons — they have rainfall seasons, driven by atmospheric circulation, not by the Sun's angle.

At 45 degrees north — roughly Portland, or Milan, or Montreal — the Sun reaches about 68 degrees above the horizon at noon in June. In December, it reaches only 21 degrees. That is a 47-degree swing in noon altitude. Combined with the change from about 16 hours of daylight to 8.5 hours, the total heating difference between summer and winter is dramatic.

At 66.5 degrees north — the Arctic Circle — something qualitatively different happens. On the summer solstice, the Sun does not set. It circles the horizon at midnight and rises again. Twenty-four hours of daylight. On the winter solstice, it barely rises. A few hours of dim gray light, then darkness again. The difference between the seasons is not warm versus cool. It is constant light versus near-constant darkness.

At the North Pole, the variation becomes total. The Sun rises once a year, circles the horizon for six months at a nearly constant angle, then sets. Six months of uninterrupted day, six months of uninterrupted night. Temperature follows accordingly — not because of any mysterious polar phenomenon, but because the angle geometry taken to its extreme produces this outcome.

The pattern is completely regular: the further from the equator, the more dramatically the 23.5-degree tilt rotates you between leaning-toward and leaning-away. Minimal effect at the equator, maximal effect at the poles. Everything is the same physical mechanism, scaled by latitude.

## The Moon's Phases Are Also Geometry

Let me make the same argument in a different domain, because the reasoning is worth repeating.

The Moon's appearance changes over about 29.5 days. For most of human history, the natural explanation was that Earth's shadow was blocking the light. Earth is large. It casts a shadow. The Moon passing through that shadow would explain the darkness.

This is wrong, and you can check it. Earth's shadow is relevant only during lunar eclipses, which happen occasionally and last a few hours. The Moon's phases happen every month, continuously, regardless of whether an eclipse occurs. Earth's shadow cannot cause them.

Here is what does. The Moon reflects sunlight. Half of it — the hemisphere facing the Sun — is always lit. The other half is always dark. What you see from Earth depends entirely on which part of the lit hemisphere is facing you. And that depends on where the Moon is in its orbit around Earth.

Imagine going into a dark room with a bare light bulb. Hold a ball at arm's length and walk in a circle around the lamp, keeping your face turned toward the ball. When the ball is between you and the lamp, the dark side faces you. You see nothing — new moon. Move 90 degrees around. Now you see half the ball lit, the half facing the lamp. Quarter moon. Move to the far side of the lamp from your starting position. The entire lit hemisphere faces you. Full moon. Continue around, and the illuminated portion you can see decreases: gibbous, quarter, crescent, and back to new.

<!-- → [INFOGRAPHIC: circular diagram with Earth at center and Sun off to one side — eight Moon positions shown around the circle at 45° intervals, each drawn as a half-lit sphere with the lit side always facing the Sun; beside each Moon position, a small inset shows what the Moon looks like from Earth at that position (new, waxing crescent, first quarter, waxing gibbous, full, waning gibbous, third quarter, waning crescent); arrows show orbital direction; this makes the viewing-angle explanation immediately legible without requiring the reader to run the ball experiment mentally] -->

This is the complete explanation. No shadow. No mystery. The phase is determined by a single variable: the angle between the Sun, Earth, and Moon — how much of the lit hemisphere you can see from your position.

One consequence that should now be obvious: you can read the phase from the Moon's position in the sky. A crescent Moon is always near the Sun — it rises and sets close to the Sun because it is close to the Sun in its orbit, and you are seeing mostly its dark side. A full Moon is always opposite the Sun — it rises as the Sun sets and sets as the Sun rises, because it is on the far side of Earth from the Sun. A quarter Moon is 90 degrees from the Sun in the sky. The phase and the sky position are the same piece of information stated differently.

One more thing worth understanding: why the phase cycle takes 29.5 days when the Moon's actual orbital period is 27.3 days. The Moon completes one orbit relative to the background stars in 27.3 days. But during those 27.3 days, Earth has moved about 27 degrees along its own orbit around the Sun. The Sun has shifted position in the sky relative to the stars. To return to the same phase — the same angle relative to the Earth-Sun line — the Moon has to cover that extra 27 degrees. This takes about 2.2 more days. The phase cycle, new moon to new moon, is 29.5 days for the same reason that a clock's hour hand, going slightly slower than the minute hand, takes slightly more than one full revolution to lap it.

## The Calendar Problem

Every calendar in history is a negotiation with an inconvenient mathematical fact.

The solar year — Earth's orbital period — is approximately 365.2422 days. The lunar month — new moon to new moon — is approximately 29.5306 days. Twelve lunar months add up to about 354.37 days, which is 10.88 days short of a solar year.

The day, the month, and the year do not share nice common factors. No whole number of days makes a whole number of months. No whole number of months makes a whole number of years. This is not a problem that any human arrangement can solve; it is a fact about three physical periods that arose from different processes and have no reason to be rationally related.

A purely lunar calendar — twelve months of alternating 29 and 30 days, roughly 354 days per year — drifts against the seasons by about 11 days per year. After three years, you are a month behind. After 17 years, the lunar calendar has slid through all the seasons: what started as a winter month is now a summer month. The Islamic calendar does this deliberately. It is internally consistent and works beautifully for its purposes. It just doesn't track the seasons.

Julius Caesar, advised by the Alexandrian astronomer Sosigenes, took a different approach in 45 BCE. Commit to the solar year. Round it to 365.25 days. Add one day every four years — the leap day — to account for the quarter-day. Average year: 365.25 days. This was a massive improvement. The Roman calendar before it had drifted so badly that months fell in the wrong seasons and required political intervention to correct.

But 365.25 is not 365.2422. The difference is 0.0078 days per year — about 11 minutes. Tiny. Except it compounds. After 128 years, the Julian calendar is off by one full day. After 400 years, three days.

By 1582, the drift had grown to ten days. The spring equinox, which the Julian calendar placed on March 21, was actually falling on March 11. This mattered to the Church because Easter's date is calculated from the spring equinox. Pope Gregory XIII issued the correction: skip ten days immediately to resynchronize, and modify the leap year rule going forward. Century years — 1700, 1800, 1900 — are no longer leap years, except when divisible by 400 (so 2000 is a leap year). Skipping three leap years per 400 years removes three days from the cumulative count. The average Gregorian year is 365.2425 days. The error is now 0.0003 days per year — one day off from the solar year in about 3,300 years.

The reform illustrates a genuine trade-off. Every increase in accuracy adds complexity to the rule. The Julian calendar was simple: four years, add a day. The Gregorian calendar is simple plus an exception plus an exception to the exception. More accurate, less elegant. The solar system imposed this cost; Gregory paid it.

Adoption took centuries. Catholic countries switched in 1582. England waited until 1752, when eleven days needed to be dropped — which produced riots from people who believed they were being robbed of eleven days of their lives. Russia held out until after the 1917 revolution. By then the gap had grown to thirteen days. The "October Revolution" occurred in November by the calendar the rest of the world was using. The calendar problem follows you.

## Eclipses: When the Alignment Happens

If the Moon orbited Earth in the exact same plane as Earth orbits the Sun, there would be a solar eclipse at every new moon and a lunar eclipse at every full moon. Twice a month. The alignment would be automatic.

The Moon's orbit is tilted about 5 degrees relative to Earth's orbital plane. This tilt, small as it is, usually prevents the three bodies from aligning. The Moon passes slightly above or slightly below the Sun at new moon, and slightly above or below Earth's shadow at full moon.

Twice a year, the Moon crosses Earth's orbital plane at points called nodes. If a new moon or full moon happens when the Moon is near a node, the alignment is close enough to produce an eclipse. These are eclipse seasons. Outside of them, the 5-degree tilt keeps things separated.

<!-- → [INFOGRAPHIC: two-panel diagram — top panel "Why eclipses aren't monthly": shows the Moon's orbit as a tilted plane intersecting the ecliptic plane, with the Moon shown above the plane at new moon (no eclipse) and at a node at new moon (eclipse); label the nodes and the 5° tilt angle — bottom panel "Solar vs. lunar eclipse geometry": solar eclipse shows the Moon's narrow shadow cone (~160 km path of totality) touching Earth's surface; lunar eclipse shows Earth's much larger shadow engulfing the full Moon; annotate that the lunar eclipse shadow covers the whole Moon and is visible from the entire night side of Earth, explaining why lunar eclipses are seen more often from any given location] -->

At a solar eclipse, the Moon passes directly between Earth and the Sun. The Moon's shadow — a narrow cone — sweeps across Earth's surface. The path of totality, where the shadow fully blocks the Sun, is typically about 160 kilometers wide. Outside that path, the eclipse is partial. This is why solar eclipses are rare from any given location: you need to be in the right narrow strip.

At a lunar eclipse, Earth passes between the Sun and the full Moon. Earth's shadow is much larger than the Moon, so it covers the entire lunar disk, and the eclipse is visible from anywhere on the night side of Earth. This is why, from any given location, you see more lunar eclipses than solar eclipses over a lifetime, even though both require equally specific geometry.

There is one more thing worth mentioning about solar eclipses, because it is too strange to skip.

The Sun's diameter is about 400 times the Moon's. The Sun is also about 400 times farther from Earth than the Moon. These two ratios nearly cancel, and the result is that the Sun and Moon appear almost exactly the same angular size in the sky — both about half a degree across. The Moon can just barely cover the Sun's disk. During a total solar eclipse, the Moon precisely covers the Sun, and the corona — the Sun's outer atmosphere — becomes visible, surrounding the dark circle of the Moon.

This is a coincidence. Not in a suspicious sense — there is no reason it had to be this way, and it will not last. The Moon is slowly receding from Earth at about 3.8 centimeters per year. In a few hundred million years, the Moon will be too small in the sky to cover the Sun, and total solar eclipses will not be possible. We happen to live in the narrow era of Earth's history when they are.

## What the Angles Are Telling You

Everything in this chapter — the backwards seasons, the phases of the Moon, the incommensurability of the calendar, the rarity of eclipses — arises from a small set of geometric facts. The 23.5-degree tilt of Earth's axis drives the seasons and their latitude dependence. The Moon's orbital geometry drives the phase cycle. The irrational relationship between day, month, and year drives the complexity of every calendar humans have built. The 5-degree inclination of the Moon's orbit drives the rarity of eclipses.

None of these require additional mechanisms. They are outputs of the geometry.

What is worth holding onto is how consistently the first, intuitive explanations fail. Distance doesn't drive the seasons — angle does. Earth's shadow doesn't drive the Moon's phases — viewing geometry does. The calendar is not arbitrary — it is the unavoidable consequence of three periods that share no rational relationship.

In each case, the correct explanation requires you to think geometrically rather than in the more comfortable quantities of nearness and size. Astronomy rewards this shift of habit repeatedly. The sky is a geometry problem. Learning to see the angles is how you stop being confused by it.

**Exercise 1.** In Southern Hemisphere summer (December–January), Earth is near perihelion — its closest approach to the Sun. This means the Southern Hemisphere summer is slightly more intense (in terms of solar input) than the Northern Hemisphere summer. Prompt an LLM: "Does the Southern Hemisphere receive more solar energy per year than the Northern Hemisphere, given that it is summer when Earth is closest to the Sun?" **Exercise 2.** The Moon's orbital period relative to the stars is 27.3 days, but the cycle of phases takes 29.5 days. Prompt an LLM to explain this discrepancy. Does the explanation correctly invoke Earth's motion around the Sun as the cause? Ask it to draw a diagram (or describe one precisely) showing why the Moon must travel extra to return to the same phase. Evaluate whether the explanation is correct.

**Exercise 3.** In 1582, Pope Gregory XIII dropped ten days from the calendar. People who had been born on, say, October 7 went to sleep and woke up on October 18 with no intermediate days. Prompt an LLM: "Were people upset about the loss of ten calendar days in 1582? Did they lose ten days of wages, ten days of age, or ten days off their lease terms?" The historical answer is interesting. Evaluate whether the LLM gives a nuanced account of the social impact or oversimplifies.

**Exercise 4.** The Moon is currently receding from Earth at about 3.8 centimeters per year. Prompt an LLM to calculate: at this rate, approximately how many years from now will the Moon's apparent diameter in the sky be too small to cover the Sun's disk, making total solar eclipses geometrically impossible? The Moon currently subtends about 0.5 degrees and needs to shrink to about 0.53 degrees less than the Sun's apparent size to no longer cover it. Work through the geometry with the LLM, then evaluate its reasoning.

**Exercise 5 (challenge).** The Islamic calendar is a purely lunar calendar. Prompt an LLM: "In what year of the Gregorian calendar will Ramadan next begin in December? In January?

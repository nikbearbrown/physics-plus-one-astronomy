# Chapter 9 — Cratered Worlds: What the Moon and Mercury Tell Us About the Early Solar System

*How Dead Worlds Remember What Living Ones Forgot.*

---

On July 20, 1969, Neil Armstrong stepped onto the Moon and noticed something that, if you think about it carefully, is one of the most remarkable observations in the history of science. He noticed that his bootprint was clear. The dust held the impression perfectly — fine-grained, undisturbed, crisp at the edges.

That seems like a small thing. It is not.

A bootprint on Earth would last an afternoon. Rain would blur it. Wind would fill it. Frost would heave it. Even in the driest desert, within a few decades it would be gone. The surface of Earth is in constant motion — not just weather and erosion, but the deeper churn of tectonic plates grinding, of volcanoes resurfacing old terrain, of rivers cutting canyons through rock that was once ocean floor. Earth erases itself continuously. Its oldest surface rocks are about 4 billion years old, and they are highly processed — folded, heated, recrystallized. The events of Earth's first 500 million years are simply gone.

Armstrong's bootprint will still be there when humans return. It will be there in a thousand years. In a million. The Moon has no atmosphere to move the dust, no water to carry it, no tectonic heat to overturn it. The Moon is geologically dead, and because it is dead, it remembers. It has been remembering, without alteration, for more than three billion years.

The other will be gone in a week. The difference is not the boot. It is the world underneath it."] -->

That is the central fact of this chapter. Two worlds — the Moon and Mercury — are useless for life but invaluable for history. Their surfaces are archives. Every crater is a date stamp. Every rock sample is a clock. Read them correctly, and you discover that the early solar system was not an orderly, slowly condensing gas cloud. It was a shooting gallery. And the Earth was not spared.

## What the Surface Records

The Moon's surface has two unmistakable terrain types.

The bright regions — the highlands — cover about 83 percent of the surface, concentrated on the far side. They are densely, almost unimaginably cratered. They are made of a pale rock called anorthosite — low-density silicate that floated to the surface when the early Moon was a global ocean of molten rock — and they are the Moon's original crust, formed between 4.1 and 4.4 billion years ago.

The dark regions are the maria — from the Latin for seas, because early observers thought they might actually be water. They are not. They are basalt: volcanic lava poured out from the Moon's interior between roughly 3.3 and 3.8 billion years ago, pooling in enormous depressions that were themselves carved by impacts. The maria have far fewer craters than the highlands because they are younger. Lava covered whatever had accumulated before.

Different surfaces. The crater density is the clock."] -->

This gives us a simple principle: older equals more cratered. A freshly paved road has no potholes. An old road is full of them. Time plus incoming projectiles equals accumulation of holes.

The Apollo missions returned samples from both terrains, and the samples were dated by radiometric methods. The highlands dated to about 4.2 billion years. The maria dated to about 3.3 to 3.8 billion years. A difference of roughly 200 to 400 million years.

Now count the craters. The highlands have about ten times as many craters per unit area as the maria.

Here is where the logic gets interesting. If the cratering rate had been constant throughout the Moon's history, and the highlands are only 200 to 400 million years older than the maria, you would expect them to have a modest excess of craters — perhaps 5 or 10 percent more, not ten times more. To accumulate ten times as many craters at a constant rate, the highlands would have to be ten times older than the maria. Ten times 3.8 billion years is 38 billion years. The universe is only 13.8 billion years old.

The assumption of a constant rate must be wrong.

The resolution is this: in the early solar system, the bombardment rate was vastly higher. The first 500 million years of solar system history were catastrophic — asteroids and comets, leftover from the formation of the planets, careening through the inner solar system at high speeds. The highlands formed during this period of intense bombardment and show it. After about 3.8 billion years ago, the supply of large projectiles dropped sharply, probably because the giant planets had gravitationally sorted, ejected, or captured most of the debris. The maria formed during and after this decline, accumulating craters at the much lower rate we can measure today.

This is one of the central findings of planetary science: the early solar system was catastrophic. What we see on the Moon is not normal geological activity. It is the scar tissue of a formation process far more violent than anything in human experience.

## What Actually Happens When Something Hits

To read the archive, you need to understand what made it.

The Moon's gravity accelerates incoming objects to at least 2.4 kilometers per second — the escape velocity, which is also the minimum impact speed. But most asteroids and comets are already moving at 10 to 20 kilometers per second relative to the Moon. A typical impact occurs at 15 to 20 kilometers per second or more.

At those speeds, the impact is not a collision in any ordinary sense. It is a detonation.

Here is what happens in the first fraction of a second. The projectile strikes the surface and penetrates two or three times its own diameter before it stops. The kinetic energy — which scales as the square of velocity, so enormous at these speeds — converts almost instantly into a shock wave and heat. The shock wave propagates outward through the rock faster than the speed of sound in stone, fracturing and shattering everything it passes through. The heat is intense enough to vaporize the projectile entirely, and a substantial volume of the target rock as well.

The expanding vapor cloud behaves like an explosion — which is, mechanically, what it is. Pressure blows outward and upward, excavating a hemispherical bowl in the rock. Solid material at the margins is not vaporized but fractured and thrown upward and outward as ejecta, landing to form a rough ridge around the rim and a blanket of debris extending outward for distances comparable to the crater's own diameter. Some ejecta achieves escape velocity and leaves the Moon entirely. Some travels hundreds of kilometers and digs secondary craters wherever it strikes.

The crater that results is roughly 10 to 15 times the diameter of the projectile. A rock 1 kilometer across, moving at 20 kilometers per second, makes a crater 10 to 15 kilometers wide. A rock 10 kilometers across — like the object implicated in the Cretaceous mass extinction — makes a crater 100 to 150 kilometers wide.

<!-- → [DIAGRAM: four-panel cross-section sequence of hypervelocity crater formation — (1) projectile penetrates 2–3× its own diameter, shock wave initiates ahead of it; (2) kinetic energy converts to heat and pressure, vapor cloud begins expanding hemispherically; (3) ejecta thrown upward and outward, hemispherical bowl excavated, rim forming; (4) floor rebounds into central peak, raised rim complete, ejecta blanket and secondary craters visible at distance — label: shock wave, vaporized projectile, ejecta, raised rim, central peak, secondary craters; annotate "crater diameter ≈ 10–15× projectile diameter" below panel 4] -->

For very large impacts, the floor rebounds. Rock under the extreme pressures involved behaves almost like a viscous fluid. The center rises back up into a central peak — the mountains visible at the center of large fresh craters on the Moon. For still larger impacts, the rebound creates a ring of peaks. For the very largest — the impact basins, hundreds of kilometers across — the structure becomes more complex still.

And here is something that surprised early geologists: almost all impact craters are circular, regardless of the angle at which the projectile arrived. A bullet hitting a wall at 45 degrees makes an elliptical gouge. An impact at 20 kilometers per second at 45 degrees makes a circular crater. The reason is that at these velocities, the direction of the projectile matters almost nothing. The explosion radiates symmetrically outward regardless of where the impactor came from. Circular, therefore, almost always.

<!-- → [DIAGRAM: two side-by-side panels — left: low-speed oblique impact (arrow at ~20° from horizontal), resulting crater is elongated ellipse, annotated "low velocity: direction matters, gouge follows projectile path"; right: hypervelocity oblique impact (same 20° angle), resulting crater is circular, annotated "hypervelocity: explosion radiates symmetrically, direction of entry irrelevant"; this directly addresses the most persistent misconception students bring to this material] -->

The evidence for the impact mechanism is solid. Craters made by artillery shells and nuclear tests on Earth have the same structural features as lunar craters — raised rims, flat floors, ejecta blankets, secondary craters. Computer simulations produce craters with the observed diameter-to-depth ratios. And rock samples from Apollo show shocked quartz — quartz grains whose crystal structure has been deformed by pressures achievable only through impact — confirming that these are not volcanic features.

## Reading Time from Craters

Now the method becomes quantitative.

If you know how often craters of a given size form per unit area, you can invert the problem: count the craters you see, divide by the rate, and get the time. Crater counting as a clock.

The calibration comes from two sources that agree. The first is direct: radiometric dating of Apollo samples from the maria gives ages of 3.3 to 3.8 billion years, and the crater density on those same surfaces gives a rate of roughly one 1-kilometer crater per square kilometer per 200,000 years for the past few billion years. The second is independent: counting the asteroids currently in the solar system, estimating their orbital probabilities, and calculating how often one of a given size should hit the Moon. Both methods produce approximately the same answer.

But the method has limits. It assumes a constant cratering rate, which was not true early in solar system history. Before about 3.8 billion years ago, the rate was so much higher that the calibration curve is poorly known. For surfaces older than 3.8 billion years, ages from crater counting have large uncertainties.

The highlands demonstrate the limit directly. Their crater density is so high — so saturated — that new impacts as often destroy old craters as create new ones. A new impact frequently lands inside an old crater, erasing it and replacing it. Once saturation is reached, additional impacts do not increase the visible crater count; they just shuffle which craters are visible. The highlands have been at saturation for billions of years.

What this means: the highlands were formed and saturated during the first few hundred million years of solar system history. After about 3.8 billion years ago, the bombardment tapered sharply. Volcanism flooded the basins, resetting the crater count. After about 3.3 billion years ago, volcanism largely ceased. For more than three billion years, the Moon has been in stasis.

## Where the Moon Came From

If the early solar system was violent enough to saturate the Moon's highlands with craters, it was violent enough for something even more extreme: collisions between bodies of planetary mass.

The Moon's origin had puzzled scientists for a century. Three classical hypotheses competed.

The fission hypothesis proposed that the early Earth was spinning so fast that a chunk broke off and became the Moon. The calculations do not work. A spinning body of rock does not throw off a neat spherical companion into a stable orbit by this mechanism.

The capture hypothesis proposed that the Moon formed elsewhere in the solar system and was later captured by Earth's gravity. To capture a large object into a stable orbit requires it to lose a large fraction of its kinetic energy during the encounter — unlikely for a body the size of the Moon. And captured objects should have a different chemical composition from the planet that captured them.

The co-formation hypothesis proposed that the Moon formed alongside Earth from the same initial cloud of material. If so, they should have similar compositions. But the Moon has almost no iron core — only about 2 percent of its mass is iron, compared to about 32 percent for Earth. The Moon is also severely depleted in volatile elements — water, carbon, potassium — substances present on Earth but apparently driven off during the Moon's formation.

None of the three hypotheses explained all three facts simultaneously.

Enter the giant impact hypothesis.

Suppose the early solar system — the same chaotic system that produced the heavy bombardment — contained not just asteroids and comets but also partially formed planetary bodies on crossing orbits. Suppose one of them, roughly the mass of Mars, was on a trajectory that intersected Earth's. Suppose it struck Earth at a glancing angle, roughly 4.5 billion years ago.

The collision would be the most violent event in Earth's history. The impacting body — call it Theia — would have penetrated into Earth's interior before its kinetic energy destroyed it. Earth's mantle would have been partially vaporized. Vast quantities of hot silicate rock — material from Earth's mantle and from Theia's mantle — would have been ejected into orbit. This material would form a ring of hot debris, which gravity would cause to accumulate over millions of years into a single large body. The Moon.

<!-- → [DIAGRAM: four-panel sequence — (1) early Earth and Theia (Mars-sized, labeled ~10% Earth's mass) on oblique collision course; (2) glancing impact, both objects partially vaporized, iron cores beginning to merge at center while mantle material sprays outward; (3) hot debris ring of silicate material in orbit around Earth — annotate "iron stayed in cores; silicate mantle was ejected"; (4) debris ring accumulates into the Moon over millions of years — annotate "Moon: iron-poor because cores weren't ejected; volatile-poor because disk temperatures vaporized them off"] -->

This hypothesis solves the problems the others could not.

Why is the Moon iron-poor? Because the iron of both Earth and Theia had already sunk to their cores before the impact. What was ejected into orbit came primarily from their mantles — silicate rock, not iron. The Moon was built from the parts of both planets with the least iron.

Why is the Moon depleted in volatiles? The impact temperatures in the debris disk were extreme. Volatile compounds — water, carbon dioxide, ammonia — were vaporized and driven off. By the time the debris cooled and accumulated, most of the volatiles were gone.

Why does the Moon's oxygen isotope ratio closely match Earth's? Oxygen comes in three stable isotopes, and different parts of the solar nebula have slightly different proportions of them — a chemical fingerprint that varies with where material formed. The Moon's fingerprint closely matches Earth's, consistent with its being built primarily from terrestrial mantle material and from Theia, which had also formed near Earth.

The giant impact hypothesis does not explain everything. Current models have some difficulty producing a Moon whose isotopic match with Earth is quite as tight as the measurements show. This is an active area of research. But the hypothesis remains the leading framework because it explains the Moon's iron deficiency, its volatile depletion, its early formation date, and its stable orbit in a way that no alternative can. And it places the Moon's origin squarely in the context of heavy bombardment: a world born from the same catastrophic processes that cratered the highlands.

## Mercury's Extremity

Mercury is a different kind of testimony.

It orbits the Sun in 88 days. Its surface temperature swings between 430 degrees Celsius on the sunlit side and minus 170 at night. It has almost no atmosphere. It rotates three times for every two orbits — a 3:2 spin-orbit resonance that took decades to confirm because Mercury always appeared in the same orientation when visible from Earth. It is small: 4,878 kilometers in diameter, less than half of Earth's.

And here is the anomaly: Mercury is 60 percent iron by mass. Its core extends to within about 700 kilometers of the surface — roughly 83 percent of the planet's radius. Mercury is, in a meaningful sense, a planetary core with a thin rocky rind. Compared to Earth, with an iron core of about 32 percent by mass and a thick silicate mantle, Mercury's silicate layer has been almost entirely stripped away.

Every hypothesis for why must explain how most of its silicate mantle disappeared."] -->

Why? Two hypotheses, both rooted in catastrophe.

The first invokes giant impacts. Mercury, small and close to the Sun during the heavy bombardment, was a target. One or more large impacts may have stripped away the silicate outer layers, ejecting mantle material completely out of Mercury's weak gravitational field. What remained was disproportionately iron-rich.

The second invokes the young Sun. In the earliest stages of the solar system, before the Sun stabilized, the disk of gas and dust extended inward to Mercury's present orbit. The young Sun radiated more intensely in ultraviolet and X-rays than it does today. At Mercury's location, that radiation could have evaporated the lighter silicate material from the proto-Mercury, leaving the denser metals preferentially behind. Mercury would have formed already depleted in silicates.

Both mechanisms produce the same symptom: too much iron. From the symptom, we cannot yet fully distinguish the cause.

The surface of Mercury testifies to heavy bombardment as clearly as the Moon's. The Caloris Basin — nearly 1,500 kilometers across — is one of the largest impact structures in the solar system, formed by a projectile that must have been hundreds of kilometers wide. The basin's formation was so violent that seismic waves from the impact traveled around the entire planet and converged at the antipodal point — the point diametrically opposite the impact — where they buckled and deformed the surface into a chaotic terrain of hills and valleys. Caloris on one side of Mercury; chaos terrain on the other.

But Mercury has a feature the Moon does not: long cliffs called lobate scarps, running for hundreds of kilometers and standing a kilometer or more high. They cut across craters, which means they formed after the craters. They appear to be compressional features — wrinkles in Mercury's crust caused by the planet shrinking. Mercury, cooling and contracting, has reduced in radius by a kilometer or two over billions of years. Enough to crumple the surface into these great scarps.

A world cooling and shrinking. The Moon's volcanism switched off. Mercury's surface wrinkling. Both telling the same story: small worlds lose their internal heat. Their geologies come to an end. They become archives.

And then there is the ice.

At Mercury's poles, in craters large enough to always shade their floors from the Sun, radar measurements from Earth first suggested something unexpected: a highly reflective material consistent with water ice. MESSENGER confirmed it. In the cold traps at the bottoms of permanently shadowed polar craters — where temperatures never rise above about minus 170 degrees Celsius, even though the rest of the planet bakes above 400 — water ice has been preserved for billions of years. Delivered by comets and asteroids over geological time, protected from sublimation by permanent shadow.

On the closest planet to the Sun. Ice.

## What the Dead Worlds Teach

The Moon and Mercury are often classified as geologically dead — no more volcanism, no plate tectonics, no weather. They are the kind of worlds that get overlooked in favor of the dynamic: Io's volcanoes, Titan's hydrocarbon lakes, Europa's hidden ocean.

But their apparent deadness is exactly their scientific value.

When a world is geologically active, it writes over its own history. Earth has been writing over its history for 4.5 billion years, and the original text is nearly gone. We have no surface samples from Earth's formation. We have no rocks from the heavy bombardment era. We cannot measure the early impact flux from Earth's surface.

The Moon and Mercury have done something more valuable than remain interesting: they have remained unchanged. Every crater is still there. Every impact basin is still there. Every ancient lava flow is still there. The surface is a continuous record stretching back to within a few hundred million years of the solar system's formation — a span of time that Earth has completely obscured.

From these two dead worlds, we have learned things we could not have learned any other way. That the early solar system was bombarded with objects far larger than anything we need to worry about today. That the Moon was formed by a collision that nearly destroyed Earth. That even Mercury's composition — the most extreme of any planet — may be the signature of catastrophe, not of orderly formation.

The bootprint in the dust that opened this chapter is a metaphor for all of it. The Moon preserves footprints because it preserves everything. It preserves everything because nothing has changed. And because nothing has changed, we can read the record and discover that everything has changed — that the solar system of today is the survivor of a formation process we would not recognize if we could see it.

**Exercise 1.** The Moon's surface preserves a near-complete record of impacts over the past 4 billion years because there is no weathering, plate tectonics, or active geology to erase craters. Prompt an LLM: "Why are crater counts on planetary surfaces a useful tool for dating those surfaces? If a region of the lunar maria has only 100 craters per million square kilometers, while the lunar highlands have 10,000 per million km², what does this tell you about the relative ages of the two regions?" Evaluate whether the LLM correctly identifies that more craters indicate longer exposure to impacts, hence older surface, and engages with the calibration of crater density to absolute age via radiometric dating of returned samples.

**Exercise 2.** The Moon formed approximately 4.5 billion years ago via a giant impact between proto-Earth and a Mars-sized body called Theia. Prompt an LLM: "What evidence supports the giant-impact hypothesis over alternative theories (capture, fission, co-formation)?" The key evidence includes: (1) Moon's bulk composition similar to Earth's mantle, (2) lack of volatile elements consistent with high-temperature formation, (3) angular momentum of Earth-Moon system consistent with a single-impact event. Evaluate whether the response correctly identifies these as the diagnostic features.

**Exercise 3.** The Moon shows the same face to Earth always — it is tidally locked. Prompt an LLM: "Explain how tidal locking arises. Why does a body's spin slow down to match its orbital period, and what determines how long this process takes?" Then ask: "Will Earth eventually become tidally locked to the Moon, with the same face always toward the Moon? How long would this take if the process continued?" Evaluate whether the response correctly identifies that the Moon's tidal forces gradually slow Earth's rotation, and that the process is limited by the eventual end of the Earth-Sun system before complete locking would occur.

**Exercise 4.** Mercury's surface is heavily cratered like the Moon's, but Mercury also shows compressional features (lobate scarps) suggesting global contraction. Prompt an LLM: "Why is Mercury contracting? What does this tell us about its internal cooling history? How does Mercury's small size relative to Earth and Mars contribute to this contraction?" Evaluate whether the response correctly identifies that smaller bodies cool faster (less volume relative to surface area for heat retention), Mercury's iron core cooling has caused the entire planet to shrink slightly, and the lobate scarps are surface evidence of this contraction.

**Exercise 5 (challenge).** The Moon's south polar region contains permanently shadowed craters where temperatures stay below 100 K. The 2009 LCROSS mission detected water ice in these craters. Prompt an LLM: "Why does water ice persist in lunar polar craters when the Moon as a whole is too hot for surface ice? What are the leading hypotheses for the source of this ice (cometary impacts, solar wind interaction, primordial)?" Then ask: "Why is lunar polar ice considered strategically important for future human missions?" Evaluate whether the LLM correctly identifies the cold-trap mechanism (permanently shadowed craters never see sunlight, preserving any deposited ice indefinitely), engages with the source debate, and notes the practical importance: water can be split into hydrogen (fuel) and oxygen (life support) for in-situ resource utilization.

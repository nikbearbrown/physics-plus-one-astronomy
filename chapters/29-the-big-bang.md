# Chapter 29 — The Universe's First Three Minutes: Evidence for the Beginning

*How three independent observations — galaxies flying apart, ancient microwave light, and the ratio of hydrogen to helium — converge on a single story.*

---

In 1964, Arno Penzias and Robert Wilson at Bell Laboratories in New Jersey were trying to build a sensitive radio antenna and kept finding a persistent hiss in their receiver. It was not interference from New York City. It was not thermal noise from the equipment. It came uniformly from all directions in the sky and did not go away at any time of day or any season of the year. They cleaned pigeon droppings from the antenna horn. The hiss remained.

What they had discovered — without intending to — was the oldest light in the universe. It had been traveling unimpeded for 13.8 billion years. It had started as the glow of a plasma hotter than any star, and the expansion of the universe had stretched it to microwave wavelengths and cooled it to 2.73 degrees above absolute zero. It was everywhere, arriving from every direction, because it had originated everywhere simultaneously, in an epoch when the entire observable universe was a single hot plasma.

This is the cosmic microwave background, and it is the most direct evidence we have for what the universe looked like when it was young. But it is only one of three independent lines of evidence that converge on the same story. Each is worth understanding separately, because together they are almost impossible to doubt.

---

## The Expanding Universe: Running the Film Backward

Start with the observation that settled the question of whether the universe has always been what it is today.

Edwin Hubble, publishing in 1929, showed that distant galaxies are receding — and the farther away a galaxy is, the faster it moves away. The relationship is linear:

$$v = H_0 \times d$$

where $v$ is the recession velocity, $d$ is the distance, and $H_0$ is a constant now called the Hubble constant. Its modern value is approximately 70 kilometers per second per megaparsec, meaning a galaxy one megaparsec away (about 3.26 million light-years) recedes at 70 km/s; a galaxy two megaparsecs away recedes at 140 km/s; and so on.

The immediate implication is almost too simple: if everything is currently moving away from everything else, then running the film backward in time brings everything together. At some finite moment in the past, all the matter we now observe was compressed into a single region. The time since that moment — the age of the universe — can be estimated by inverting the Hubble constant:

$$T_0 \approx \frac{1}{H_0} \approx 13.8 \text{ billion years}$$

This estimate assumes constant expansion. In fact, the expansion has not been constant — gravity slowed it in the past, and something else (dark energy) is accelerating it now. When these effects are properly accounted for, the age remains 13.8 billion years to good precision.

The expanding universe is the opening argument. It tells you the universe had a beginning and gives you a rough timescale. But an expanding universe alone does not tell you what conditions were like at the beginning. For that, you need the other two lines of evidence.

## The Microwave Background: A Fossil from 380,000 Years

The second argument comes from the radiation Penzias and Wilson found.

To understand what they detected, you need to know what the early universe was like. For the first 380,000 years after the Big Bang, the universe was too hot for neutral atoms to exist. The temperature was above 3,000 Kelvin everywhere. At that temperature, any hydrogen atom that formed would immediately be ionized by a photon — the electron knocked free of the proton. The universe was a dense plasma of free electrons, protons, and photons, all interacting constantly. Photons could not travel far without hitting a charged particle and scattering. The universe was opaque in exactly the sense that a thick cloud is opaque — not because there was no light, but because every photon kept scattering off electrons and changing direction.

Then the universe cooled to 3,000 Kelvin. At this temperature, electrons and protons could finally combine into neutral hydrogen atoms and stay combined. This is called recombination. Suddenly, the photons had nothing left to scatter off. They decoupled from matter and streamed freely through space.

Those photons have been traveling ever since. The universe has expanded by a factor of about 1,000 since recombination, which means the wavelengths of the photons have been stretched by the same factor — shifted from visible and infrared wavelengths to microwaves. Their effective temperature has cooled from 3,000 Kelvin to about 2.73 Kelvin. This is the cosmic microwave background.

The critical feature of the CMB is not just its existence — any hot beginning would produce such a relic — but its extraordinary uniformity. Look in one direction in the sky and measure the temperature. Look in the opposite direction. The temperatures agree to one part in 100,000. The early universe was uniform to this remarkable precision.

But not perfectly uniform. The tiny temperature variations — the fact that the CMB is 2.730001 Kelvin in one direction and 2.729999 in another — carry an enormous amount of information.

Before recombination, the plasma was not quite uniform. Regions of slightly higher density attracted more matter through gravity, compressing further. This compression heated the plasma. The pressure then pushed back, expanding the region. The expansion cooled and rarified the plasma. Gravity pulled it together again. The plasma oscillated like sound waves in air — but these were waves in the entire primordial plasma, at scales of millions of light-years, propagating at about half the speed of light.

When recombination happened and the photons decoupled, these sound waves were frozen in place. The pattern of compressions and rarefactions imprinted itself on the temperature map of the CMB. Today we call this the acoustic peaks — a pattern in the CMB power spectrum showing which angular scales carry the most temperature variation.

The positions and heights of these acoustic peaks are extraordinarily sensitive to the universe's composition and geometry. The first peak — corresponding to sound waves that had just completed one full oscillation by the time of recombination — tells you the geometry of space. If space is flat (which general relativity, in the presence of sufficient energy density, predicts), sound waves should peak at a particular angular scale on the sky. If space is curved, that scale shifts. The Planck satellite, which mapped the CMB with extraordinary precision, finds that space is flat to better than 0.1%. The universe is not a closed sphere or an open saddle — it is Euclidean.

The heights of the second and third peaks tell you the density of ordinary matter (protons, neutrons, electrons) relative to dark matter. Dark matter does not interact with photons; ordinary matter does. This difference affects how strongly the plasma oscillates. From the relative heights of the peaks, we can infer that ordinary matter makes up about 5% of the universe's total energy content. Dark matter, which does not interact with the plasma, makes up about 27%. The remaining 68% is dark energy.

These numbers come from fitting the precise shape of the CMB temperature fluctuation map. They are not theoretical preferences. They are what the data require.

## The Light Elements: A Calculation That Worked

The third argument is the oldest and, in some ways, the most direct.

George Gamow and Ralph Alpher worked out the theory of Big Bang nucleosynthesis in 1948. The key idea: in the first few minutes after the Big Bang, the universe was hot enough for nuclear fusion, but only briefly. At temperatures above about 1 billion Kelvin, nuclear reactions could proceed. Below that, the universe was too cool and too dilute for reactions to occur at significant rates. There was a narrow window — roughly the first three minutes — during which light nuclei could form.

The calculation proceeds from first principles and quantum mechanics.

At one second after the Big Bang, the temperature was about 10 billion Kelvin. Free neutrons and protons existed in roughly equal numbers, constantly being converted into each other by reactions involving electrons, positrons, and neutrinos. As the temperature dropped, these conversion reactions became inefficient. The ratio of neutrons to protons froze at roughly one neutron for every six protons.

From this point, the sequence is straightforward. Neutrons are unstable when free — a lone neutron decays with a half-life of about ten minutes. But at temperatures still above about 1 billion Kelvin, neutrons rapidly combine with protons to form deuterium (one proton, one neutron). Deuterium is fragile and gets broken apart by photons at high temperatures, but as the universe cools, deuterium survives long enough to undergo further reactions. Deuterium combines with another neutron to form tritium, or with another proton to form helium-3. Helium-3 combines with a neutron to form helium-4 (two protons, two neutrons). Helium-4 is tightly bound and stable.

Nearly all the neutrons end up in helium-4. Starting with a ratio of one neutron per six protons: if we consider 2 neutrons and 12 protons, we can form 1 helium-4 nucleus (consuming both neutrons and 2 of the protons) and 10 protons left over as hydrogen. By mass, helium-4 has mass 4 units, hydrogen has 1 unit per atom. So the fraction by mass in helium is $4 / (4 + 10) = 4/14 \approx 28\%$. The actual calculation, accounting for the exact freeze-out ratio and the fact that some neutrons decay before combining, gives a helium mass fraction of about 25%. Hydrogen makes up the remaining 75%.

A tiny amount of deuterium survives without reacting further — about 1 part in 100,000 by number. Helium-3 survives in similar trace amounts. Lithium-7 is produced in trace quantities by several reaction pathways.

Now compare this to observations. The helium abundance is measured in old, metal-poor stars and in gas clouds between galaxies, where it has not been much altered by stellar processing. The measured helium fraction by mass is about 24 to 25%. The measured deuterium abundance in pristine gas clouds is about $2.5 \times 10^{-5}$ by number relative to hydrogen. The lithium-7 abundance in the oldest stars is about $1.6 \times 10^{-10}$ relative to hydrogen.

The agreement between prediction and observation for helium and deuterium is remarkable. The helium fraction is what Big Bang nucleosynthesis predicts to within the measurement uncertainty. The deuterium abundance pins down the baryon density (the density of ordinary matter) with precision that independently matches what the CMB acoustic peaks tell us. Two completely different measurements — one from the microwave sky, one from measuring isotope ratios in ancient gas clouds — give the same baryon density.

There is one discord: the observed lithium-7 abundance in old stars is about a factor of three lower than predicted. This is called the lithium problem, and it remains unresolved. It may reflect that old stellar atmospheres have processed some lithium away; it may indicate a gap in the nuclear reaction network; it may require some new physics. But it is worth noting that helium and deuterium — the two light elements produced in by far the largest quantities — agree perfectly. The lithium discrepancy is real and puzzling, but it does not overturn the framework.

The key point: no other theory of the universe's origin predicts these abundances. If the universe had always been static, or if it had started cool rather than hot, or if all elements were produced in stars alone, the predicted abundances would be wrong. Big Bang nucleosynthesis succeeds because in the Big Bang model, there is a specific temperature-density history that makes the calculations come out right.

## The First Three Minutes in Detail

With this framework in hand, we can trace the universe's history from the first moments.

Before about $10^{-43}$ seconds, quantum gravity effects dominate and our equations break down. We genuinely do not know what happened before this point.

By $10^{-12}$ seconds, the temperature was about $10^{15}$ Kelvin — enough to dissemble protons and neutrons into their constituent quarks. The universe was a quark-gluon plasma, a state of matter recreated briefly in high-energy collider experiments. As the universe cooled below this threshold, quarks became confined inside protons and neutrons.

By one second, the temperature was about 10 billion Kelvin. The neutron-to-proton ratio froze at roughly 1:6 as weak-force reactions became too slow to keep the ratio in equilibrium.

Between 1 and 200 seconds, at temperatures between 10 billion and 1 billion Kelvin, nucleosynthesis occurred. Deuterium formed and then reacted quickly to helium. Within about three minutes, the reactions stopped. The universe was too cool and too dilute to continue. The light element abundances were frozen.

For the next 380,000 years, the universe was a hot plasma — cooling, expanding, neutral atoms unable to form. The matter and radiation were coupled, oscillating together, setting up the acoustic pattern that would be imprinted on the CMB.

At 380,000 years, recombination: neutral atoms formed, photons decoupled, the universe became transparent. The CMB we detect today was last scattered at this moment.

From this point, the structure of the universe — the large-scale pattern of galaxies and voids — grew from the tiny density perturbations that had been oscillating in the plasma. Over billions of years, regions of slightly higher density collapsed under gravity, forming galaxies and galaxy clusters. The universe went from uniform to structured, and we can see the seeds of that structure in the CMB map.

## Three Observations, One Story

Step back and look at what three completely independent observations say.

The expansion of the universe tells you: everything was once compressed together. The age of the universe is about 13.8 billion years.

The cosmic microwave background tells you: the early universe was hot, dense, and nearly uniform. The plasma oscillations before recombination froze into a pattern that we observe today. Space is geometrically flat. Ordinary matter is 5% of the total energy content.

Big Bang nucleosynthesis tells you: in the first few minutes, when the universe was hotter than any star, nuclear reactions forged the light elements in specific ratios. Those ratios match what we observe today in the oldest, least-processed material in the universe.

These three observations come from completely different phenomena — the motions of galaxies, the temperature of microwave radiation, and the isotope chemistry of ancient gas clouds. They point to the same history. This convergence is not what you would expect from coincidence or from a framework that is fundamentally wrong. It is what you expect when the framework is correct.

What is not yet understood: the physical nature of dark energy, the cause of the Hubble tension, and what mechanism triggered inflation — the rapid early expansion that made the universe so flat and so uniform. These are open questions at the frontier of cosmology.

What is settled: the universe began in a hot, dense state about 13.8 billion years ago. We can measure when. We can trace the physics from the first second onward. And three independent measurements confirm the same story.

The hiss that Penzias and Wilson heard in their antenna in 1964, after cleaning out the pigeon droppings and checking every possible source of interference, was 13.8-billion-year-old light. It had been traveling since the universe first became transparent. It is still arriving today, from every direction, at 2.73 Kelvin, carrying in its tiny temperature variations the fossil record of the universe's birth.

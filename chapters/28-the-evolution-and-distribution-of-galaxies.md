# Chapter 28 — The Evolution and Distribution of Galaxies

*The universe is not organized. It assembled itself, from nearly nothing, over thirteen billion years — and we know this because light takes time to travel.*

---

In 1995, astronomers aimed the Hubble Space Telescope at a patch of sky smaller than the shadow of a grain of sand held at arm's length. They chose this patch deliberately: it contained no bright stars, no known galaxies, no obvious reason to look at it. Then they left the shutter open for ten days.

What came back was not darkness. The image — the Hubble Ultra-Deep Field — contained roughly ten thousand galaxies, layered back through time like geological strata. Some were recognizable spirals and ellipticals. Others were clumpy, irregular, chaotic — not fitting any category. And the pattern was consistent: the farther away the galaxy, the younger it appeared, and the younger it appeared, the stranger it looked.

This is not a coincidence. It is the key to understanding how galaxies formed.

---

## Light Is a Time Machine

Here is the fact that makes this chapter possible. Light travels at a finite speed — 300,000 kilometers per second. When you look at a galaxy ten billion light-years away, the light you are receiving left that galaxy ten billion years ago. You are not seeing that galaxy as it is now. You are seeing it as it was when the universe was only about four billion years old.

This is not an approximation or a metaphor. It is what "ten billion light-years away" literally means.

The consequence is that telescopes are time machines. Looking farther into space is looking further back in time. If you want to know what galaxies looked like when they were young — when the universe was one billion years old instead of fourteen — you do not need to wait for a young galaxy to form nearby. You look at galaxies that are very far away, whose light has been traveling since the universe was young, and you see their youth directly.

The method requires measuring distances. For distant galaxies, the distance comes from the redshift. As light travels through an expanding universe, space stretches, and this stretching elongates the wavelength of light toward the red end of the spectrum. The more space the light has crossed, the more it has been stretched. A galaxy with redshift $z = 1$ has had its light wavelength doubled during transit; it is about seven billion light-years away, and we see it as it was about seven billion years ago. A galaxy with $z = 10$ is so far that we see it as it was when the universe was only about 500 million years old.

So the question "how did galaxies form and evolve?" becomes, observationally, "what do galaxies look like at high redshift, and how does that change at lower redshift?" Every observation of a distant galaxy is an observation of the past. The Hubble Ultra-Deep Field is an archaeology dig, with depth measured in billions of years rather than meters.

## What Young Galaxies Actually Look Like

The answer, when astronomers collected the data, was surprising.

Nearby galaxies — the ones we see at low redshift, the ones whose light left them recently — fit into clean categories. Ellipticals: smooth, featureless spheroids, old red stars, little gas, no star formation. Spirals: flat disks with rotating arms, young blue stars mixed with old red ones, plenty of gas, active star formation. The categories are not perfect, but they describe most of what we see.

Galaxies at redshift $z = 1$ — seen as they were seven billion years ago — look similar but skewed. More of them are forming stars. Spirals are brighter and bluer. Ellipticals are rare. The early universe seems to have had fewer finished ellipticals.

At $z = 2$ or $z = 3$ — ten to eleven billion years ago, when the universe was two to three billion years old — the picture changes dramatically. The clean categories largely disappear. Most galaxies are clumpy and irregular. They do not have smooth spiral arms. They do not have the ordered structure of nearby galaxies. They are actively forming stars at rates five to ten times higher than typical nearby galaxies, and they are doing it in chaotic, turbulent environments.

At $z = 10$ and higher — now accessible with the James Webb Space Telescope — galaxies are small, luminous, and extraordinarily blue, indicating stars so young and hot that the galaxy is essentially all starburst. These are infants. And infants look nothing like adults.

The pattern is clear: early galaxies were smaller, more irregular, more actively star-forming, and less organized than the galaxies we see today. The large, ordered spirals and ellipticals of the nearby universe are not primordial. They assembled themselves over billions of years.

## How Galaxies Grow

The mechanism is mergers.

When two galaxies approach each other, gravity pulls them together. Their mutual attraction builds as they close the distance. The galaxies are distorted by tidal forces — the difference in gravitational pull on the near side versus the far side — before they even touch. Long tails of stars are flung outward. The shapes are destroyed. Eventually the two systems interpenetrate and, over hundreds of millions of years, coalesce into a single galaxy.

A critical fact: the stars inside the two galaxies almost never actually collide. Stars are so small relative to the distances between them that two galaxies can merge completely while their individual stars pass through each other without a single stellar collision. The Milky Way and Andromeda will merge in about four billion years. The Sun will almost certainly not be destroyed by a direct impact with another star; it may simply be nudged into a new orbit around the combined galaxy.

What does collide is the gas. Interstellar gas clouds from the two galaxies slam together. The collision compresses the gas. Compressed gas is unstable. It collapses into dense clumps. Dense clumps form stars. A galaxy merger can trigger a starburst — an explosive episode of star formation at rates a hundred times higher than normal — lasting a few hundred million years before the gas is used up.

These starbursts are visible. The blue light of young hot stars blazes out across cosmic distances. Many of the clumpy, irregular, intensely blue galaxies seen at high redshift are mergers in progress, starbursts ignited by collisions, galaxies in the process of assembling themselves into something larger.

In the early universe, mergers were common. The universe was smaller then — not in the sense of being physically smaller in any one region, but in the sense that all the structure was packed closer together. Galaxies were more densely distributed. Collisions happened frequently. This is why the early universe had such high star-formation rates: it was full of galaxies crashing into each other, each collision igniting a starburst.

Over time, as the universe expanded and the distances between galaxies grew, merger rates declined. Today, only about 5% of nearby galaxies show clear signs of ongoing merging. The violent youth of the universe has given way to a quieter middle age.

## The Black Hole Feedback Problem

There is a complication in this picture that took decades to understand.

Most large galaxies have supermassive black holes at their centers — objects with masses from millions to billions of times the Sun's mass. When a merger brings gas into the galactic center, this gas spirals into the accretion disk around the black hole, heats to millions of degrees, and releases an enormous amount of energy. Some of this energy escapes in jets — narrow beams of material shot outward at near-light speeds, carrying energy into the galaxy's gas.

The jets do not just pass through the gas. They heat it. They scatter it. They prevent it from cooling and condensing into new stars. The black hole, by eating gas, destroys the conditions that would allow new stars to form.

This is called AGN feedback — active galactic nucleus feedback — and it appears to be one of the main reasons that large elliptical galaxies stopped forming stars. The sequence goes like this: mergers bring gas into the central region, triggering both intense star formation throughout the disk and intense accretion activity around the central black hole. The black hole's jets then heat and expel the remaining gas. The starburst shuts down. The galaxy runs out of fuel. Star formation stops. What is left is an elliptical galaxy dominated by old, red stars — red and dead, in the astronomers' phrase — slowly cooling and fading for the next several billion years.

This explains a pattern that otherwise seems puzzling. Elliptical galaxies, which are the most massive galaxies in the universe, contain the oldest stars. You might expect that bigger galaxies would have more time to form stars and thus have newer stars. Instead, the most massive ellipticals formed most of their stars more than ten billion years ago and have been coast

ing ever since. The answer is AGN feedback: the black holes in massive galaxies were more active, shutting down star formation faster and more completely.

Spiral galaxies took a different path. They formed in more isolated regions with less dramatic mergers, lower AGN activity, and more gradual, continuous star formation sustained over billions of years. The Milky Way is still forming stars today — a few solar masses per year — because the conditions for star formation were never completely disrupted.

## The Shape of Everything

Zoom out far enough and a strange pattern appears.

Galaxies are not distributed randomly. They are concentrated into sheets and filaments, like the walls of gigantic bubbles. Between the walls are vast voids — regions hundreds of millions of light-years across that contain almost nothing. The filaments connect at knots where clusters of galaxies sit, like dew drops gathered at the intersections of a spider web.

This is the cosmic web, and it is real. The Sloan Digital Sky Survey measured the redshifts of millions of galaxies and mapped their three-dimensional positions. The filaments and voids were unmistakable — a sponge-like structure on scales of hundreds of millions of light-years. Galaxies occupy less than 10% of the universe's volume. The rest is void.

How did this pattern arise? Not from the galaxies themselves. It was imprinted in the early universe.

In the first few hundred thousand years after the Big Bang, the universe was a hot plasma — gas and light so thoroughly mixed that the gas was opaque. Sound waves traveled through this plasma, compressing some regions and rarefying others. When the universe cooled enough for electrons and protons to combine into neutral hydrogen atoms, the plasma suddenly became transparent. The sound waves froze in place. Their pattern — slightly denser here, slightly less dense there — was recorded in the distribution of matter.

These density variations were tiny: about one part in 100,000. But gravity is patient. Over billions of years, the slightly denser regions attracted more matter from their surroundings. Matter flowed along the paths of least resistance — into filaments, then into clusters at the filament junctions. The underdense regions were drained of matter and became voids.

The cosmic web you see today is the acoustic pattern of the early universe, amplified by thirteen billion years of gravity.

## The Invisible Scaffolding

There is one more element necessary to explain why structure formed as fast as it did: dark matter.

Ordinary matter — atoms, the stuff that makes stars and planets and people — cannot collapse fast enough on its own to produce the structures we see by the age of the universe today. The problem is that before the plasma became transparent, ordinary matter was too tightly coupled to radiation to collapse. The photons pushed back against any developing concentration, preventing atoms from falling into the gravitational wells that would eventually become galaxies. Only after the plasma cleared could ordinary matter start to collapse, and by then — if ordinary matter were all there was — there had not been enough time for gravity to build the structures we observe.

Dark matter solved this problem. Dark matter does not interact with light. It was not held back by the pressure of photons during the plasma era. It began collapsing much earlier, forming what are called dark matter halos — concentrations of invisible mass that grew over the first few hundred million years. When ordinary matter was finally free to collapse after the plasma era, it fell into the gravitational wells that dark matter had already prepared. The stars that formed from this gas lit up the centers of dark matter halos. The halos were the scaffolding; the galaxies were the architecture built on top of it.

This is why dark matter matters for galaxy formation. Without it, galaxies could not have formed as quickly as they did. The timeline does not work. Dark matter built the wells; ordinary matter fell into them.

The evidence for dark matter in galaxies is independent and convergent. Rotation curves of spiral galaxies are flat — stars far from the center orbit at the same speed as stars near the center, which requires mass distributed far beyond the visible disk. Galaxy clusters contain ten times more mass than all their visible stars and gas can account for. Light from galaxies behind a cluster is bent and distorted by the cluster's gravity — gravitational lensing — and the mass inferred from this lensing far exceeds the visible mass.

Dark matter constitutes about 27% of the universe's total energy content. Ordinary matter is about 5%. Dark energy — the repulsive force accelerating the universe's expansion, whose nature we do not understand — makes up the remaining 68%.

The universe is, in this sense, mostly unknown to us. The stuff that makes stars and planets and life is a minor constituent. We live in a universe whose dominant components we cannot see and do not understand.

## What We Are Looking At

When you look at the night sky, you are looking at the near-end result of a thirteen-billion-year assembly process. The galaxies nearest to us — the Milky Way, Andromeda, the other members of the Local Group — are mature, large, organized structures that assembled themselves through repeated mergers, starbursts, and the gradual accumulation of gas and stars over most of cosmic history.

When the Hubble Space Telescope looks at a patch of sky and finds ten thousand galaxies in a region the size of a grain of sand's shadow, it is seeing that history laid out in depth. The nearby galaxies are old. The distant ones are young. The transition from young to old, from clumpy to ordered, from intensely star-forming to quiescent, is visible in the data. The universe evolved.

The key insights are these. The universe was not born organized — it grew organized, through gravity amplifying tiny initial density variations into filaments and voids and clusters. Galaxies assembled from mergers, and mergers were common early on and rare today. The black holes at galaxy centers regulated star formation through feedback, shutting down star formation in massive galaxies and giving ellipticals their red and dead character. Dark matter provided the invisible scaffolding on which all visible structure was built.

We understand this process in broad outline. We do not understand all of it. The very earliest galaxies — those observed at $z > 10$ with JWST — are forming stars at rates that seem, under current models, too high for the time available. The mechanism that produced the first stars and the first black holes within the first hundred million years is still not fully understood. The nature of dark matter and dark energy remains unknown.

The story of how the universe organized itself is largely written. The first words are still being deciphered.

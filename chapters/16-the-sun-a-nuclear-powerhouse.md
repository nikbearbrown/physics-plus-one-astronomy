# Chapter 16 — The Sun Burning: Why Four Billion Years of Light Comes from Turning Mass into Energy

*The Sun is not on fire. It is doing something far stranger — and the proof came from a tank of cleaning fluid buried in a gold mine.*

---

Charles Darwin died worried about physics.

He had spent the last years of his life defending his theory against religious objection, and he had won most of those arguments. But there was one he could not answer. It came from physicists, and it was not about the origin of species. It was about the origin of time.

Evolution requires enormous stretches of it. For natural selection to produce the diversity of life Darwin observed, hundreds of millions of years were needed — perhaps more. Yet the physicists Thomson and Helmholtz had calculated how long the Sun could possibly shine, and their answer was about 100 million years. Darwin returned to this problem repeatedly in his later correspondence, unable to resolve it.

He was right to worry. The conflict was real. The physicists were right that their mechanism could not power the Sun long enough. They were wrong only in assuming they had identified the right mechanism. The energy source they had missed would not be discovered until after Darwin was dead, and it required overturning nearly everything Victorian physics believed about matter and energy.

---

## Why the Obvious Answers Fail

The first thought about what powers the Sun is the obvious one: it burns. Like coal in a furnace.

The calculation is simple. Burning coal releases a known amount of energy per kilogram. The Sun radiates at a known rate — about $4 \times 10^{26}$ watts. Calculate how much coal would need to burn per second. The answer is roughly 100 million tons per second. Now assume the entire Sun is coal and calculate how long it lasts. About 5,000 years.

We know from geological evidence that liquid water existed on Earth 4 billion years ago. Chemical burning is ruled out by a factor of nearly a million.

Thomson and Helmholtz had a better idea: gravitational contraction. The outer layers falling inward under their own weight. As matter falls, kinetic energy converts to heat. Calculate the energy available from contracting the entire Sun from a diffuse cloud to its present size, and the answer is genuinely impressive — about $10^{42}$ joules. At the Sun's current output, this lasts roughly 100 million years.

That is the number that worried Darwin. And gravitational contraction is a real energy source — young stars actually shine partly this way, before fusion ignites. But the rocks demand more time.

The correct answer requires two things that were not known in Darwin's lifetime.

First: atoms have internal structure. They contain nuclei — tiny dense cores packed with protons and neutrons, held together by a force far stronger than electricity. The nucleus is not a passive lump. It is a reservoir of energy bound up in the arrangement of its particles.

Second: mass and energy are not distinct. They are two forms of the same thing, related by $E = mc^2$. The $c^2$ is an enormous number — the square of 300,000 kilometers per second. Even a tiny amount of mass, converted fully to energy, releases something staggering.

Put these together and the Sun's energy source becomes conceivable. If the Sun can fuse lightweight nuclei into slightly heavier ones, and if the product weighs slightly less than the ingredients, that missing mass has become energy. Not burned. Not compressed. Annihilated and replaced by radiation.

---

## The Barrier That Should Make It Impossible

To fuse hydrogen nuclei, you must bring protons together. Protons are positively charged, and like charges repel. The closer two protons approach, the stronger the repulsion. This is the Coulomb barrier.

For two protons to fuse, they must get within about $10^{-15}$ meters of each other — the scale of a nucleus — where the strong nuclear force, which is attractive but extremely short-ranged, can grab them. To reach that distance classically, a proton needs to be moving at over 1,000 kilometers per second. At the Sun's core temperature of 15 million Kelvin, the average proton moves at about 500 kilometers per second.

By classical physics, fusion in the Sun should be essentially impossible. The protons lack the energy to penetrate the barrier. The Sun should be dark.

The resolution is quantum mechanics.

At the scale of nuclei, particles do not behave like bullets. They behave like waves, and waves can partially penetrate barriers rather than stopping cleanly at them. A proton approaching another proton is not a ball hitting a wall. It is a probability distribution — a wave function with a small but nonzero amplitude on the far side of the barrier. The proton does not go over the barrier. It appears, probabilistically, on the other side. This is quantum tunneling.

The probability is small. Vanishingly small. A single proton in the Sun's core bounces off other protons billions of times a day, and it will do this for billions of years before it finally tunnels through and fuses. The average time before a given proton fuses is roughly 14 billion years — longer than the current age of the universe.

But the Sun's core contains about $10^{57}$ protons — more than the number of stars in the observable universe. Even though each individual collision has almost no chance of producing fusion, the number of collisions per second is so enormous that the improbable becomes inevitable, in aggregate, at a rate that matches the Sun's observed luminosity exactly.

The Sun shines because the improbable happens constantly, scaled up by numbers too large for intuition.

---

## How Hydrogen Becomes Helium

The conversion happens in three steps, and the bottleneck is the first one.

**Step one.** Two protons collide. One of them undergoes a transformation: a proton becomes a neutron, emitting a positron (the antimatter twin of an electron) and a neutrino. What remains is deuterium — a nucleus with one proton and one neutron. The positron immediately encounters a nearby electron and annihilates, producing gamma-ray photons. The neutrino, interacting with almost nothing, escapes the Sun directly.

$$^1_1\text{H} + {^1_1\text{H}} \longrightarrow {^2_1\text{H}} + e^+ + \nu$$

This step is the rate-limiting bottleneck. It requires not just tunneling but also the weak nuclear force — the mechanism that converts a proton to a neutron. The weak force is, as its name says, weak. The average proton waits billions of years for this step.

**Step two.** The deuterium nucleus collides with another proton and fuses, producing helium-3: two protons, one neutron. A gamma-ray photon is released. This step is much faster — once deuterium forms, it survives only about six seconds before being struck and consumed.

$$^2_1\text{H} + {^1_1\text{H}} \longrightarrow {^3_2\text{He}} + \gamma$$

**Step three.** Two helium-3 nuclei collide and fuse. The result is helium-4 — ordinary helium — plus two protons that return to the core. The helium-3 nuclei float through the core for roughly a million years before finding each other.

$$^3_2\text{He} + {^3_2\text{He}} \longrightarrow {^4_2\text{He}} + {^1_1\text{H}} + {^1_1\text{H}}$$

Net: four hydrogen nuclei in, one helium nucleus out, plus energy.

Now weigh the ingredients and the product.

Four protons have a combined mass of $4 \times 1.007276 = 4.029104$ atomic mass units. One helium-4 nucleus has a mass of $4.001506$ atomic mass units. The difference is $0.027598$ atomic mass units — about 0.7% of the starting mass.

That 0.7% has become energy. Not released from a chemical bond. Not converted from kinetic energy. It has ceased to exist as mass at all and become electromagnetic radiation.

Apply $E = mc^2$ to get the scale. The Sun radiates $4 \times 10^{26}$ watts. The mass that must vanish per second:

$$\dot{m} = \frac{P}{c^2} = \frac{4 \times 10^{26}}{(3 \times 10^8)^2} \approx 4.4 \times 10^9 \text{ kg/s}$$

About 4 million metric tons disappear into energy every second. Every second. For 4.5 billion years so far.

The Sun's total mass is $2 \times 10^{30}$ kilograms. At 4 million tons per second, the Sun would take roughly 15 billion years to lose even 1% of its mass. Darwin needed hundreds of millions of years. The Sun has billions to spare.

The problem that stopped Darwin is solved. The mechanism — mass converting to energy at 0.7% per cycle — gives the Sun a total lifespan of about 10 billion years. We are 4.5 billion years in.

## What Happens to the Energy After It Is Made

A gamma-ray photon born in the solar core does not travel to Earth in a straight line. It cannot.

The interior of the Sun is a dense plasma — a sea of ionized atoms and free electrons, all moving fast, all colliding constantly. A photon born in the core travels about one centimeter before striking an electron and being absorbed. Then it is reemitted — but in a random direction. Up, down, sideways. No preference.

This is a random walk. Each step averages about one centimeter. The distance to the surface is 700,000 kilometers — $7 \times 10^{10}$ centimeters. In a random walk, the expected number of steps to travel a distance $d$ in steps of length $\ell$ is $(d/\ell)^2$. That is $(7 \times 10^{10})^2 = 5 \times 10^{21}$ steps, each of one centimeter. At the speed of light, this takes roughly 100,000 to 1,000,000 years.

The photon landing on your face right now was created in the Sun's core before multicellular life existed on Earth. It spent most of its existence trapped in the solar plasma, bouncing in random directions, diffusing outward. Only the last eight minutes of its journey were in empty space.

Neutrinos, by contrast, exit immediately. They interact only via the weak nuclear force, which means matter is essentially transparent to them. A neutrino born in the solar core passes through the entire Sun as if it were not there, then through Earth, and continues outward. Travel time from core to Earth: about two seconds.

The million-year photon and the two-second neutrino were born in the same fusion reactions. One is delayed by a million years of random walking through plasma. The other arrives before we have finished counting.

## The Missing Neutrinos

In 1970, physicist Raymond Davis Jr. lowered a tank containing 400,000 liters of perchloroethylene — dry-cleaning fluid — 1.5 kilometers into a gold mine in South Dakota. The depth blocked cosmic rays. The cleaning fluid was the detector.

The reasoning: solar neutrinos would occasionally collide with chlorine nuclei and convert them to radioactive argon. The argon could be extracted and counted. Theory predicted roughly one argon atom per day.

Davis extracted the argon every month and counted it carefully. After years of work, the result was consistent and deeply puzzling: only about one-third the predicted number of neutrinos was appearing.

This was not a small discrepancy. Not a calibration error. One-third is a large fraction to be missing. Either the solar model was wrong — the Sun was not fusing at the calculated rate — or physics was missing something about neutrinos.

Astrophysicists spent decades on this. The solar model was tested using helioseismology — measuring sound waves propagating through the Sun's interior, which constrain the density and temperature at every depth. The model was correct. The fusion rate was correct. The neutrinos were being produced at the predicted rate.

They were disappearing in transit.

The answer arrived through a Canadian experiment buried two kilometers underground in a nickel mine. The Sudbury Neutrino Observatory used heavy water rather than chlorine-containing fluid. The critical difference: Sudbury could detect all three types of neutrinos — electron, muon, and tau. Davis's experiment could only see electron neutrinos.

The result: the total number of neutrinos arriving at Sudbury matched the solar model exactly. But only one-third were arriving as electron neutrinos. The other two-thirds had become muon or tau neutrinos during the eight-minute journey from the Sun.

The electron neutrinos were not disappearing. They were changing.

This transformation — neutrino oscillation — can only occur if neutrinos have mass. Massless particles cannot oscillate between types. The neutrinos created by fusion are electron neutrinos, but as they travel, they cycle through all three types. A detector sensitive only to electron neutrinos catches only the fraction that happens to be in that state when they arrive.

The Nobel Prize in Physics 2015 was awarded for this discovery. The solar neutrino problem was not solved by revising our understanding of the Sun. It was solved by discovering that neutrinos themselves have a property — mass, however tiny — that the standard model of particle physics had not predicted.

The Sun was correctly understood. The particle was not.

## What Holds the Sun Up

There is one more thing worth saying, because it explains why the Sun does not explode and does not collapse.

The Sun is in hydrostatic equilibrium. At every depth, the pressure of hot gas pushing outward exactly balances the weight of all the gas above pushing inward. The fusion energy at the core maintains the temperature that maintains the pressure that maintains the balance.

This is self-regulating. If fusion slows — if something cools the core slightly — the pressure drops, the overlying layers compress the core, the temperature rises, and fusion accelerates until the pressure is restored. If fusion accelerates, the extra pressure expands the core, the temperature drops, and fusion slows back to equilibrium. The Sun sits in a stable configuration, corrected back whenever disturbed.

This stability is why the Sun has been shining steadily for 4.5 billion years. The same feedback that makes it robust is what allowed Earth to maintain a stable temperature long enough for life to evolve.

The Sun will burn at approximately its current luminosity for another 5 billion years, until the hydrogen in its core is substantially depleted. Then the balance will shift and the structure will change — but that is a story for another chapter.

What matters now is the mechanism. The Sun converts 4 million metric tons of matter into energy every second. The energy escapes through a million-year random walk of photon scattering, then travels eight minutes in straight lines to reach us. The neutrinos from the same reactions arrive two seconds after being born, and we know they are real because we built a tank of cleaning fluid in a hole in the earth to catch them.

Darwin could not have imagined any of this. But the resolution of his trouble is one of the most satisfying stories in science: the threat to his theory came from physics, and physics answered it better than he could have hoped.

**Exercise 3.** Quantum tunneling allows protons to fuse at temperatures far below what classical physics would require. Higher temperature means higher collision energy, which means the wave function penetrates the barrier more deeply.

# Chapter 25 — The Milky Way Galaxy

*We live inside the thing we are trying to map. Everything we know about its shape, we learned by developing instruments that could see through the obstacles between us and the rest of it.*

---

In 1785, William Herschel and his sister Caroline did the only experiment available to them: they counted stars. They pointed their telescope in different directions across the sky and tallied how many stars appeared in each direction. The same number appeared in every direction along the bright band of the Milky Way, and almost none above or below it. Herschel concluded the system was disk-shaped and that the Sun must be near its center.

He was right about the disk. He was catastrophically wrong about the center.

The thing that defeated him was dust.

The Milky Way is not transparent. Between the stars and the gas clouds lies fine dust — particles of carbon and silicon compounds the size of smoke particles, manufactured in the furnaces of dying stars. This dust absorbs visible light systematically. A star a few thousand light-years away appears dimmer than it should. A star ten thousand light-years away is nearly invisible. Herschel was counting stars within roughly 6,000 light-years of Earth — a tiny bubble inside a structure 100,000 light-years across. He saw the same number of stars in every direction because the dust made the Galaxy look symmetric around him, like someone trapped in a foggy room concluding they are at the center of the world.

For 130 years after Herschel, nobody knew where we actually were.

---

## Finding the Center

The correction arrived from an unexpected direction.

In 1917, Harlow Shapley at Mount Wilson Observatory was studying globular clusters — dense spherical collections of hundreds of thousands of old stars, orbiting the Galaxy in the halo far above and below the disk. These clusters are mostly free of dust. They can be seen at enormous distances. Shapley measured the distances to 93 of them using Cepheid variable stars: stars that pulsate with a regular period, and once you know how intrinsically bright they are, you can calculate how far away they must be to appear as bright as they do.

When Shapley plotted all 93 clusters in three-dimensional space, they did not surround the Sun symmetrically. They formed a sphere — but the sphere was centered not on us, but on a point in the direction of Sagittarius, roughly 26,000 light-years away. Shapley made the obvious inference: if the globular clusters orbit the center of the Galaxy, and the center of their distribution is in Sagittarius, then the center of the Galaxy is in Sagittarius. The Sun is not at the center. It is in the suburbs.

This was correct, and it required developing new distance measurement tools and finding objects that dust could not hide. It took 130 years to answer a question Galileo had opened. The lesson is not that Herschel was foolish. He used the best tools available. The lesson is that the tool was wrong for the problem, and no amount of counting could reveal what the dust was hiding.

## What Penetrates Dust

To map the disk — to trace the spiral arms, measure the thickness, understand the full extent — required tools that did not exist in 1917. The dust problem was still there.

What penetrates dust is radio waves. In the 1950s, radio astronomers discovered that neutral hydrogen atoms — the most abundant substance in the universe — emit a characteristic signal at a wavelength of 21 centimeters, when the electron in a hydrogen atom flips its spin. This signal passes through dust as easily as a thrown ball passes through a chain-link fence. Radio telescopes tuned to 21 centimeters could trace the distribution of hydrogen throughout the Galaxy, regardless of how much dust lay between us and it.

Infrared light also penetrates dust. For the galactic center, wrapped in particularly dense dust clouds that make it completely invisible at optical wavelengths, infrared became the only window. The center of the Galaxy cannot be seen with an ordinary telescope. It glows only in radio and infrared.

What these tools revealed, assembled over decades, is this.

## The Architecture

The Galaxy is a layered system, and each layer has different properties.

At the center: a nuclear bulge, roughly peanut-shaped, about 20,000 light-years across, dominated by old reddish stars. In its very center, a supermassive black hole with a mass of 4.6 million Suns, occupying a volume no larger than Mercury's orbit.

Surrounding the bulge: the thin disk, a flat structure 100,000 light-years across and only 2,000 light-years thick. This is where most of the Galaxy's star formation is happening right now, where the spiral arms are, where the gas and dust and molecular clouds are concentrated. The Sun lives here, about 26,000 light-years from the center, orbiting at 220 kilometers per second. At that speed, one orbit takes about 225 million years.

Outside the thin disk is the thick disk — a puffier layer of older stars extending farther above and below the midplane. Above and below that, the halo: a nearly spherical cloud of the oldest stars in the Galaxy, some 13 billion years old, moving in elongated randomly oriented orbits. This halo also contains the globular clusters that Shapley used to find the center.

And beyond the visible edge of everything — extending to at least 200,000 light-years — is the dark matter halo, a sphere of invisible material we have never directly detected, but which we know is there because the Galaxy does not work without it.

## The Spiral Arms Are Not What They Look Like

When you see a photograph of a spiral galaxy — Andromeda, or M101 — the arms look like permanent physical structures, like threads on a screw. They are not. They cannot be.

The Galaxy rotates differentially: stars closer to the center orbit faster than stars farther out, just as Mercury orbits faster than Neptune. If the spiral arms were fixed patterns of stars rotating with the disk, differential rotation would wind them tighter and tighter. After a few billion years, they would disappear into a featureless smear. The Galaxy is 13 billion years old. Its arms should have been erased billions of years ago.

They have not been. The spiral structure persists.

The resolution is that spiral arms are not structures of stars. They are density waves — regions of slightly higher-than-average density where stars and gas are momentarily compressed. Think of a traffic jam on a highway. Cars pile up behind the jam, move slowly through it, then accelerate away on the other side. The traffic jam persists as a location on the highway even though individual cars move through it and continue on. A spiral arm is a place where gas clouds spend slightly longer than usual as they orbit the center.

The critical effect of this extra density is star formation. When gas clouds pile up in the density wave, they are compressed. Compression triggers gravitational collapse. Collapse ignites new stars. The young, hot, blue stars that form outline the arm brightly before they burn out after a few million years. Behind them, new clouds enter the compression zone, new stars ignite, and the arm persists. It persists because it is continuously replaced.

This solves both problems at once: the arms do not wind up because they are not winding structures, and they shine brightly with young stars because compression perpetually produces them. A spiral arm is not a feature of the Galaxy. It is a place where something perpetually happens.

## The Evidence for Something Invisible

In the 1970s, Vera Rubin and Kent Ford made careful measurements of how fast stars orbit the galactic center at various distances. If the mass of the Galaxy were concentrated near the center — all those billions of stars in the bulge and inner disk — then stars farther out should orbit more slowly, just as Neptune orbits the Sun more slowly than Mercury. This follows directly from Newton's law of gravity.

The stars did not obey this prediction. Stars at 50,000 light-years from the center orbit at roughly the same speed as stars at 30,000 light-years. Stars at 100,000 light-years, near the visible edge of the disk, orbit at roughly the same speed as the Sun. The rotation curve is flat.

It does not. The mass required to keep it flat is not visible."] -->

A flat rotation curve, when you work through the mathematics, implies that the total mass enclosed within any given orbit keeps increasing as the orbit grows larger. If orbital speed is constant and radius grows, the enclosed mass must grow proportionally. But we can see all the stars. The visible mass stops growing at the edge of the disk. Beyond that edge, the enclosed mass required to explain the orbital speeds is growing, but there is nothing visible to account for it.

The conclusion is inescapable: there is matter beyond the visible disk that we cannot see. A lot of it. The visible matter — stars, gas, dust, everything that glows — amounts to perhaps 100 billion solar masses. The orbital speeds demand a total mass of at least 2 trillion solar masses. The difference, roughly twenty times the visible mass, is invisible. It is distributed in a sphere extending far beyond the visible disk. It is called dark matter.

Dark matter does not emit light. It does not absorb light. It does not interact with anything we can measure except through gravity. We do not know what it is. We know only its existence and its distribution, because the stars tell us, through the speeds of their orbits, what mass surrounds them.

This is not a problem confined to our Galaxy. Every spiral galaxy observed shows a flat rotation curve. Every galaxy cluster shows more mass than visible matter can account for. Dark matter appears to constitute about 27% of the total energy content of the universe. The ordinary matter that makes stars, planets, and people — all of it — is about 5%. We live in a universe whose composition we do not understand.

## Weighing the Black Hole We Cannot See

The galactic center is behind the thickest part of the dust. Ordinary telescopes cannot see it. But infrared telescopes can.

In 1992, two independent teams — one led by Reinhard Genzel at the Max Planck Institute, one led by Andrea Ghez at UCLA — began tracking individual stars near the galactic center using infrared telescopes with adaptive optics: computer-controlled mirrors that flex dozens of times per second to correct for atmospheric blurring.

Over 20 years of observation, they found a population of stars orbiting a common point in rapid, highly elliptical orbits. One star, designated S2, was tracked through a complete orbit. It takes 15.6 years. Its closest approach to the center brings it within 124 astronomical units. During that closest approach, it moves at about 3% of the speed of light.

From the orbital parameters — period, size of the orbit — you can calculate the mass of the object at the center using Kepler's third law, the same formula used to weigh the planets. The mass enclosed within S2's orbit: 4.6 million solar masses, occupying a region no larger than Mercury's orbit.

No known collection of stars or gas can achieve that density and remain stable over any timescale comparable to the Galaxy's age. A dense cluster of stars would collapse under its own gravity into a black hole within a hundred thousand years. Since the Galaxy has existed for 13 billion years and this concentration still exists, it must be a single black hole. The concentration is the black hole.

We did not see it directly. We saw its gravitational imprint on the orbits of the stars around it. We used Kepler — a tool developed to understand planets — to weigh a black hole 26,000 light-years away. The Nobel Prize in Physics 2020 was awarded to Genzel and Ghez for this work.

## A Galaxy With a History

The Milky Way's structure is not primordial. It has been built and rebuilt over 13 billion years by collision and merger.

In 1994, astronomers discovered the Sagittarius dwarf galaxy, 70,000 light-years away, being torn apart by the Milky Way's gravity and stretched into streams of stars that spiral through the halo. In a few hundred million years, it will be absorbed completely. This is not ancient history. This is happening now.

Since that discovery, astronomers have found the remnants of at least a dozen similar collisions — streams of stars from other galaxies, torn apart and absorbed over billions of years. The thick disk may be the relic of a major merger roughly 10 billion years ago, when a smaller galaxy collided with the Milky Way and scattered some thin-disk stars into higher, more disordered orbits.

The future is already calculated. In about 4 billion years, the Andromeda Galaxy, currently approaching us at 110 kilometers per second, will collide with the Milky Way. The two spiral galaxies will merge into a single elliptical galaxy over several billion years. The Sun will not be destroyed — the distances between individual stars are vast enough that direct collisions are vanishingly rare — but it may be flung into a completely different orbit. The Milky Way as it currently exists will cease to be.

## The Oldest Stars Remember

In 1944, working by lamplight under wartime blackout conditions in California, Walter Baade discovered while observing Andromeda that there are two distinct populations of stars in a galaxy. The nuclear bulge and halo are filled with old, reddish stars he called Population II. The spiral arms contain hot blue stars he called Population I.

The difference runs deeper than color. Population II stars are chemically primitive — they contain very little of anything heavier than hydrogen and helium, sometimes less than one ten-thousandth the iron concentration of the Sun. These are the oldest stars, formed when the Galaxy was young and the interstellar medium had not yet been enriched by stellar deaths.

Population I stars are chemically mature. The Sun has about 2% of its mass in elements heavier than helium — iron, silicon, carbon, oxygen, the raw material of planets. The youngest stars forming now contain even higher concentrations. These stars were born from gas that had already passed through previous generations of stars, each generation leaving heavier elements behind when they died.

The Galaxy's chemical maturity was not given. It was earned, one stellar death at a time, over 13 billion years.

And encoded in the spectra of individual stars is this entire history. The oldest halo stars carry the signature of the Galaxy's youth: almost no heavy elements. The youngest disk stars carry the signature of its maturity: the accumulation from a billion supernovae.

We are not separate from this history. The atoms that make up Earth — and the ones that make up you — were forged in previous generations of stars, scattered by their deaths, and incorporated into the cloud that became the solar system 4.6 billion years ago. The Galaxy's chemical history is literally in your body. The spectrum of a star is not an abstraction; it is the same story told in a different language.

A single-point velocity measurement is ambiguous — a star moving fast could be in a small tight orbit or a large elliptical orbit.
